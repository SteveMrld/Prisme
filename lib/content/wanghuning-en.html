Wang Huning, or the architect
of the world after freedom

He traveled through America in 1988, notebook in hand, and returned with the conviction that liberalism carried within it the seeds of its own ruin. Since then, Wang Huning has built, in the silence of back offices, the doctrine that governs Xi Jinping's China, sketching the contours of a model of governance that democracies regard with a revulsion not entirely unrelated to envy.

There is something almost novelistic about the journey that changed everything. In 1988, Wang Huning is a young political science professor in Shanghai, thirty-three years old, already regarded in academic circles for the precision of his comparative analyses. He receives a fellowship, boards a plane for the United States, and spends six months traversing a country he observes with the cold minuteness of an entomologist. He sees the homeless of San Francisco, the muffled violence of Detroit's neighborhoods, the disintegrating families of Midwestern suburbs, and everywhere that absolute, almost religious certainty that the American system is the finished form of human political organization. He does not write travel anecdotes in his notebook. It is the symptoms of a civilization that believes it has resolved the question of power without having resolved the question of belonging.

He returns to Shanghai and writes America Against America, a book that will never be translated into English but will circulate in Chinese Communist Party circles as a founding document. The thesis is simple in its formulation, vertiginous in its implications: Western liberalism generates the conditions of its own destruction because it cannot answer the only question that matters in the long history of civilizations, namely how to hold together societies that modernity inexorably fragments.

The ideologue of three rulers

Within the Chinese political landscape, Wang Huning occupies a position without equivalent, not through the radicalism of his thinking but through the absolute discretion with which he has translated it into power. He has never governed a province, commanded an army, or managed a state enterprise. He has thought. And three successive presidents have made him their closest adviser: Jiang Zemin, for whom he fashions the doctrine of the "Three Representations"; Hu Jintao, for whom he forges the concept of the "harmonious society"; and finally Xi Jinping, for whom he invents the "Chinese Dream" and the thinking of "Xi Jinping on Socialism with Chinese Characteristics for a New Era," that formula as long as a river and as opaque as its waters, now inscribed in the Party Constitution.

Surviving three reigns in the Chinese Politburo without ever being caught in a purge is a feat that requires either prodigious luck or an absolutely exceptional intelligence of power dynamics. Wang Huning has been a permanent member of the Politburo Standing Committee since 2017, third in the country's formal hierarchy, and his name remains unknown to the Western public at large, even as his doctrine structures the behavior of a state that governs the lives of 1.4 billion people and shapes the world order.

Trust as foundation

To understand China's social credit system without caricaturing it, one must return to the context in which Wang Huning's doctrine encountered the realities of China in the 2000s. The country was racing out of a planned economy into global capitalism, with a brutality of transformation that few societies had experienced in contemporary history. Corruption had colonized every level of the administration. Food scandals followed one another with a regularity that betrayed a systemic collapse of controls: from melamine-tainted milk that had killed infants to counterfeit medicines circulating in provincial hospitals, to school buildings constructed without standards that had collapsed on their pupils during the 2008 Sichuan earthquake. Trust between economic actors, between citizens and the state, even between neighbors, had reached historically low levels.1

It is in this soil that the document published in 2014 by the State Council under the title "Plan for the Construction of a Social Credit System" takes root. This text, which Western journalists read through an Orwellian lens, resembles a technocratic response by a state confronted with a deep crisis of legitimacy more than it does a totalitarian project. The stated objective is to "build a culture of honesty," to give institutions the means to enforce their decisions, to make contractual behavior predictable in a society where it was not. Surveillance here is not an end in itself. It is the instrument of a governance ambition.

Wang Huning understood before many others that the question of surveillance is not a technological question. It is a political question about the kind of social contract a society is prepared to sign, and about what it is willing to surrender in order to no longer have to trust the unknown.

The system that results is far less unified than its media representation suggests. There is no single score assigned to every Chinese citizen. What does exist, however, are sectoral blacklists fed by courts, financial regulators, and local administrations, which can lead to very concrete restrictions on rights: in 2023, the Supreme People's Court recorded thirteen million people barred from taking planes or high-speed trains on account of unpaid debts or ignored judicial decisions.2 What also exists is a progressive normalization of the idea that individual behaviors can be evaluated, that this evaluation is legitimate, and that the state has not only the right but the duty to draw consequences from it.

The Xinjiang laboratory

To measure what this model can produce at its maximum intensity, one must look at what has been happening in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region since 2017. What the Chinese government calls a "deradicalization" campaign is documented by researchers, journalists, and international tribunals as a large-scale control system: omnipresent facial recognition, checkpoints every five hundred meters in major cities, systematic collection of DNA and biometric data, internment in "vocational training centers" whose population has been estimated at more than one million people by independent researchers.3

The significance of this laboratory extends beyond its geographic borders: the technologies developed and refined there have since been commercialized internationally. The SafeCity program, which sells turnkey surveillance solutions to foreign governments, draws directly on the expertise accumulated in Xinjiang. Hikvision and Dahua Technology, placed on the US commercial blacklist since 2019, have equipped dozens of cities in countries that are not dictatorships in the formal sense but which find in these tools a practical answer to concrete problems of public order and migration control.4

By the end of 2023, the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace had counted more than seventy countries that had imported Chinese surveillance technologies through such partnerships. Ecuador has deployed thousands of Hikvision cameras in its major cities. Zimbabwe has signed with CloudWalk Technology to build a national facial recognition database. Countries in Central Asia and sub-Saharan Africa have adopted rating systems for their companies as part of technical cooperations presented as administrative modernization.5

What the mirror reflects

The temptation to conclude that this does not concern us is real, and it must be resisted. Not to equivocate about the fundamental difference between an authoritarian regime and a liberal democracy, but because that difference is less absolute than we like to believe, and making it absolute too easily spares us from examining what is happening at home.

American digital platforms have built, over the past twenty years, behavioral profiling systems whose granularity exceeds what most Chinese government agencies are capable of producing. A company like Meta knows its users' political preferences, their psychological vulnerabilities, the state of their affective relationships, and their consumption habits with a precision that the Communist Party probably envies. The difference lies in the purpose, advertising rather than political control, and in the nature of the actors, private companies subject to imperfect law rather than a sovereign state. It is an important difference, but it is not one of kind, and above all it is not a guarantee.

What Wang Huning perceived with disturbing acuity is that our liberal democracies have no satisfactory answer to the question of social cohesion in societies that globalization, inequality, and social networks fragment with an efficiency that traditional political institutions can no longer compensate for. We have good answers to the question of formal freedoms. We have weaker ones to the question of belonging.

The promise and its limits

In 2022, Xi Jinping secured a third presidential term by breaking the tacit convention established by Deng Xiaoping to prevent the return of a personal concentration of power. This rupture is the logical fruit of Wanghuningist doctrine: if stability takes precedence over freedom, and if stability requires strong and continuous leadership, then the institutional mechanisms that constrain that leadership are a luxury that history does not permit.

The problem is that this logic operated under conditions of growth that legitimized the regime's implicit promise, namely that prosperity would come before freedom, that the Chinese people would first receive material goods and rights afterward. Those conditions are less evident in 2026. Growth is slowing. Youth unemployment among graduates has reached levels the government has stopped publishing. The real estate crisis has destroyed a considerable portion of middle-class savings. Wang Huning's promise faces its first long-term test.

His story does not teach that the authoritarian digital model is viable over time. It is that it answers questions that liberal democracies struggle to formulate clearly, because formulating them clearly would mean admitting that freedom alone is not enough to hold a society together. Wang Huning said as much in 1988, watching America come apart. We will have to find an answer to him eventually.
