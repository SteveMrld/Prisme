In October 2023, the US Department of Commerce published an update to its export control rules that effectively prohibited Nvidia from selling its most advanced chips to Chinese customers without a specific license. Nvidia is not a defense company. Its circuit boards do not carry nuclear warheads. But Washington had decided that computing power was now a strategic variable, and that allowing China free access to it amounted to ceding a future military advantage. A unilateral decision, taken in Washington, thus redrew in a matter of weeks the commercial prospects of a California company, compelled its European and Asian competitors to fall into line or lose access to the American market, and set back the development of artificial intelligence in China by several years. No treaty. No UN Security Council resolution. An administrative rule.

This is not an accident. It is the result of an architecture built layer by layer since 1944, of which Trump's tariffs or sanctions against Russia are merely the most visible manifestations. Understanding this arsenal requires going back to its foundation.

Bretton Woods, or how to manufacture a universal lever

In July 1944, in a hotel in New Hampshire, the Allied powers laid the foundations of the postwar monetary order. The dollar became the international standard, global trade was denominated in American currency, and the global financial system progressively converged toward infrastructures whose critical nodes were located in the United States or fell under American law. This accounting detail would prove to be a weapon of considerable reach. Any transaction in dollars, wherever it takes place in the world, can in theory fall under American jurisdiction. It is on this foundation that the rest of the edifice was built.

The FCPA, or the birth of offensive extraterritoriality

In 1977, in the wake of Watergate and revelations about the corrupt practices of American companies abroad, Congress adopted the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act. For the first time, an American law applied to conduct committed outside American territory, provided a company was listed in the United States, used American banking correspondents, or an email transited through an American server. The connecting criterion was deliberately minimal: a tenuous thread sufficed for American jurisdiction to assert itself. For two decades, the FCPA remained relatively dormant. It was the 2000s that reawakened it, revealing its potential as an instrument of industrial policy as much as judicial policy. Total, Alcatel, Siemens, and later Alstom would experience this. In Alstom's case, the Department of Justice proceedings unfolded precisely during the period when General Electric was seeking to acquire its energy assets, and their outcome considerably facilitated that acquisition. The boundary between justice and industrial strategy was never thinner.

CFIUS, or the architecture of protection

While the FCPA constituted the offensive component, the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States handled the defensive one. Created in 1975, reinforced in 1988 following Fujitsu's acquisition of Fairchild Semiconductor, then considerably expanded in 2018, CFIUS can block or unwind any acquisition of an American company by a foreign investor whenever it judges the operation likely to threaten national security, a notion whose elasticity has grown continuously over the years. Foreign companies can thus be prosecuted for what they do abroad, and prevented from acquiring what they covet on American soil.

Sanctions, or the dollar transformed into a weapon

The Office of Foreign Assets Control manages the most formidable component of the arsenal. Its sanctions lists have direct extraterritorial reach: any entity, wherever it is in the world, that does business with a sanctioned target risks being excluded from the dollar financial system, which in most cases amounts to economic death. In 2014, BNP Paribas paid nine billion dollars in fines for having processed dollar transactions with Cuba, Iran, and Sudan. A French bank, sanctioned in Paris, for operations conducted outside American territory. The transmission mechanism was simple: the transactions were denominated in dollars, and the dollar belongs, ultimately, to American jurisdiction.

From the Cloud Act to chips: the digital extension

The Cloud Act of 2018 extended this logic into digital space. It authorizes American authorities to compel any cloud service provider subject to American law to disclose data stored on its servers, including when those servers are physically located outside American territory. No contractual clause can stand in the way: the law prevails. Chief information officers who demand immunity clauses for their sensitive data are asking, perhaps without realizing it, that Washington voluntarily disarm a central component of its power apparatus. The only technically coherent response would be to repatriate this data onto infrastructure not subject to American law, something that technical logic has long demanded, and that economic and political constraints continue to make difficult to accomplish at the necessary scale.

The illusion of normative reciprocity

This is not improvised legal imperialism varying with administrations. It is a coherent architecture, built layer by layer over eight decades, with the dollar as its central nervous system and each instrument filling a complementary function in the overall apparatus. Against this, a coordinated normative response has value, but it does not alter the fundamental balance of power. Legal sovereignty without real reciprocal capacity, monetary, technological, industrial, remains a statement of intent. The history of this arsenal teaches one simple thing: the United States did not build it by drafting regulations, but by patiently manufacturing the material conditions that gave those regulations their weight.
