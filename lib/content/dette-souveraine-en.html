One hundred trillion dollars. Let us take a moment to grasp what this figure represents. Not an alarmist projection, not a dystopian scenario forged by catastrophist economists. Global public debt has crossed this threshold. It now exceeds 95% of world GDP. It is not the debt itself that concerns us. What the debt reveals, that is the real subject: societies that have systematically preferred to borrow from the future rather than make difficult decisions in the present. The bill is arriving. Not as a spectacular crash. But in the much quieter form of a state that shrinks, that gives up educating its children, treating its sick, defending its borders. Public debt is not a problem of national accounting. It is an anthropological indicator.

Five countries alone concentrate nearly 70% of this mass: the United States, China, Japan, France, and Italy. Their trajectories diverge. Their shared diagnosis is the same: debt is no longer for them an instrument of public policy. It has become the constraint that has substituted itself for policy.

The empire working for its creditors

The United States lived for half a century under the regime of what Valéry Giscard d'Estaing called the "exorbitant privilege." Borrowing in its own currency, in a world that erected the dollar as the universal reserve asset. This privilege is fracturing. In 2025, interest payments exceeded $1.1 trillion, becoming the first item of federal expenditure, ahead of defense, ahead of Medicare. When debt service becomes the first budget item of an empire, it means the empire is working for its creditors. It is no longer sovereign in the full sense of the word.

China presents the most opaque case in the ranking. Its official debt, 77% of GDP, appears reasonable. But once local government debts hidden in off-balance-sheet financing vehicles are incorporated, the real figure probably exceeds 110 to 130% of GDP. The property crisis since 2021, with the collapse of Evergrande, exposed this fragility. Authoritarian debt management avoids short-term panic, but it prevents the price signal that would force necessary adjustments.

Japan is the statistical anomaly that has fascinated economists for thirty years. At 230% of GDP, no confidence crisis. 90% of the debt is held by residents. But accelerating aging means that today's savers will become tomorrow's dissavers. Japan proves that debt at 230% of GDP is not automatically lethal. It does not prove that it is indefinitely sustainable.

France embodies a painful contradiction. It built one of the world's most generous social protection systems. And it can no longer finance it without borrowing more. In five years, interest payments doubled: from €52.9 billion in 2023 to €60.2 billion in 2024. Nearly one in two debt securities is in the hands of non-resident investors.

Italy is the chronic patient of the eurozone, with 137% of GDP for a quarter century. This is no longer a crisis; it is a permanent state. Together, France and Italy represent nearly half of total eurozone public debt.

What debt prevents

The annual cost of French debt is comparable to the entire national education budget: €63 billion. Money that trains no one. Money that builds nothing. This crowding-out is measured in the Shanghai Ranking, in the exodus of researchers toward London, Boston, Singapore. When a state must choose between paying its interest and educating its youth, it chooses its interest. Financial markets do not wait. The children of the suburbs, however, can wait.

What debt prevents does not appear in any official balance sheet. It consists of absences: the hospitals that were not built, the rail networks that were not renovated, the public research laboratories that were not funded. Debt does not only destroy present wealth; it amputates the capacity to produce it in the future. This is what economists call the crowding-out effect: every euro devoted to debt service is a euro subtracted from productive investment. France, in 2025, devotes to its creditors a sum greater than what it spends on energy transition, defense, or higher education taken separately.

In 2025, the weight of French debt interest exceeded that of the national defense budget. A nuclear power, permanent member of the Security Council, devotes more money to remunerating its creditors than to financing its own military security.

There is also a geography of constraint. Global South countries long bore alone the condescending gaze of Bretton Woods institutions on their financial management. The structural adjustment plans imposed by the IMF on Ghana, Argentina, or Zambia have been the subject of exhaustive critical analyses. But the dynamic now hitting advanced economies is not fundamentally different: it is the same logic of debt substituting itself for policy, the same shrinkage of democratic space under market pressure. The difference is that no one calls it structural adjustment.

The blind spot: what we choose not to perceive

Every year, between €80 and €100 billion evaporates from French public coffers through tax evasion. That is more than the total income tax collected. Meanwhile, the General Directorate of Public Finances has lost nearly 34,000 full-time equivalents since 2008. We are reducing tax enforcement while complaining that the coffers are empty. Tax evasion and public debt are two faces of the same transfer: one of the most elaborate forms of socializing losses and privatizing gains.

There exists another category of blind spots: tax expenditures. In France, these represent more than €80 billion annually: loopholes, tax credits, exemptions whose economic effectiveness has never been evaluated. They accumulate through legislative sedimentation, each government adding its own without removing its predecessors'. This is public spending disguised as foregone tax revenue. And since it does not appear in the budget, no one votes to eliminate it. The public debate exhausts itself discussing marginal adjustments (a few hundred million here or there) while dozens of billions lie untouched in arrangements whose beneficiaries have learned to render invisible.

The most serious critique one can address to French debt management is not technical. It is political. The instruments exist. The levers are known. What is lacking is the will to activate them against organized interests that have learned to neutralize any reform attempt. In this sense, debt is not the symptom of collective incompetence. It is the product of a power relationship.

A question of sovereignty

A nation that can finance its choices only on condition that external actors lend it the necessary resources is no longer entirely sovereign. It is under guardianship. Soft, invisible, but real. De Gaulle understood this before everyone else: "We cannot have an independent policy and an independent defense if we do not have an independent economy and sound finances." Fifty-five years after his death, French public debt has been multiplied by eight.

Budgetary sovereignty is not merely an accounting abstraction. It determines the real perimeter of political decision. When interest rates rise by 100 basis points, France mechanically spends several additional billions on debt service, without any government, parliament, or citizen having been consulted. The decision belongs to the ECB, to bond markets, to rating agencies. These are unelected institutions that partly determine the framework within which French democracy operates. This reversal of legitimacy deserves to be named clearly: it constitutes a form of dispossession, consented to through the accumulation of successive deficits.

Emerging countries that have gone through sovereign debt crises (Argentina in 2001, Greece in 2010) experienced this reality brutally: the state forced to negotiate with its creditors the very nature of its social, wage, and educational policy. France is not there yet. But the direction taken over the past forty years points in that sense. The democratic debate should not only address deficit reduction. It should address what debt accumulation transfers, progressively and silently, outside the sphere of collective decision-making.

The invisible debt

There exists a debt about which almost no one speaks. In France, if one cumulates net public debt with off-balance-sheet commitments (of which civil servant pension liabilities alone represent €1.8 trillion) one exceeds €7 trillion. That is more than double the official accounting debt. In 2025, for the first time since the end of World War II, the natural balance of the French population became negative. A public debate that agitates over the €3.46 trillion of official debt while ignoring off-balance-sheet commitments twice that size is, intellectually, a dishonest one.

A choice, not a fatality

Public debt is a choice. The accumulation, over five decades, of democratically validated choices that all opted for the same solution: consuming today resources that belong to tomorrow. This is not corruption. It is short-sighted political rationality. No government has ever won an election by promising austerity.

Alternative trajectories have nonetheless existed. Sweden, in the early 1990s, faced a financial and budgetary crisis of a severity comparable to that of Greece in 2010. It chose adjustment: painful, rapid, conducted with broad political and union consensus. In less than a decade, it had transformed a deficit of 12% of GDP into a budget surplus. It today has one of the most solid public finances in Europe. This is not a Nordic miracle. It is the result of a deliberate and assumed societal choice.

The comparison is not meant to advocate for undifferentiated austerity. It aims to demonstrate that budgetary constraint is not a natural law: it is the residue of accumulated decisions. And that other decisions are possible, provided one does not confuse the inevitable with the comfortable. The question is no longer whether France will need to adjust its public finances. It is whether this adjustment will occur within the framework of a chosen political project, or under the pressure of markets that no one will have known how to anticipate.

The sovereignty of tomorrow is built in the budgets of today. If we continue to defer, it will not be the markets that take our freedom. It will be ourselves who will have mortgaged it.
