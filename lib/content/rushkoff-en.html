The flight

Douglas Rushkoff receives a mysterious invitation one day. A handful of Silicon Valley executives want to hear him speak about the future. He expects to discuss innovation, artificial intelligence, virtual reality. Walking into the room, he discovers five men, all billionaires. The first question falls, blunt: "How do we keep control of our bodyguards when money no longer has value?"

Rushkoff understands then that they are not trying to anticipate the future. They are trying to survive what they call The Event, climate crisis, social collapse, pandemic. The scenario matters little. They dread the loss of control itself.

In Survival of the Richest, Rushkoff recounts this encounter as a fable. These men who connected the planet through cables and algorithms now dream of extracting themselves from it. Some are buying land in New Zealand. Others are digging shelters, as if survival were a question of engineering.

"Perhaps the best way to survive is to learn to trust. Not after. Now."

The trap

Algorithms negotiate in nanoseconds on the markets. Artificial intelligence renders verdicts we can no longer explain. Social networks propagate fires that no one lights. And faced with what they have created, their response is neither repair nor transformation.

It is flight.

What is at work here exceeds ordinary cynicism. These men are not all predators without conscience. Some believed in what they were building, the belief that technology could repair what it damages, that optimization could replace wisdom, that growth could indefinitely defer its own contradictions. This belief met reality. And reality won.

No elsewhere

Civilizations have collapsed before ours. Rome, the Maya, Byzantium. Each time, something remained: another shore, an intact territory, a margin in which to begin again. What has changed is the absence of an outside. Financial capitalism, resource extraction, digital infrastructure have colonized the last corner. There is no longer a place to go that is not already taken, measured, valorized.

But this anguish has something obscene about it when set against what hundreds of millions of people in the Sahel, eastern DRC, Bangladesh, and the Pacific atolls have been living for decades. For them, collapse is not a scenario to be warded off in an air-conditioned room. It is the drought that burns a harvest. The flood that erases a village. The mine that poisons an aquifer to fuel our batteries.

These populations have long lived with what the wealthiest dread in theory. And they survive, not through bunkers, but through solidarity networks woven in adversity, collective knowledge transmitted across generations, trust built through shared ordeals. What these men are about to discover, the peoples they have so often ignored have always practiced as a vital necessity.

The collapse of meaning

We speak of collapse as a horizon. But I sometimes wonder whether we are not already living at its center.

We are connected at every moment, and loneliness has never been so massive. We produce more than ever, and few of us feel we are building something that lasts. We optimize our sleep, our productivity, our relationships, and we end up exhausted by the effort of existing efficiently.

It is not the infrastructure that is collapsing. It is the meaning.

The machine is running. It is even accelerating. But it runs on itself, for itself, according to a logic whose direction no one, not even those who built it, any longer controls.

What they do not understand

In their survival scenario, who will grow their food? Who will repair their generators? Who will care for their children? They imagine they can purchase all of this. But without mutual trust, their armed guards will eliminate them within a few months. This is, incidentally, what Rushkoff told them, calmly, and what disconcerted them most.

Survival cannot be decreed. It is built over time, in reciprocity, in real knowledge of those around us. This is not a gentle idea. It is a hard law.

These men built empires on the promise of connecting us. They end up huddled alone, behind walls, wondering how to buy the loyalty of their own guards.

What has never been automated

Trust resists everything. It cannot be scripted, trained, deployed at scale. It is built slowly, in small kept commitments, in presence when it counts, in winters weathered together. It cannot be optimized. It does not scale.

This may be the only thing that fifty years of technological revolution has not managed to replace.

The real luxury today is not an armored shelter in New Zealand. It is having people to count on when everything wavers. A community that catches you. Neighbors who know your name.

That cannot be purchased anywhere.

And the men who spent their lives selling everything have just understood this.

Key takeaways
01

Tech billionaires are consulting survival experts to secure their post-apocalyptic bunkers. This is not eccentricity: it is the revelation of a deep terror among those who built the digital world.

02

Their survival plan contains a fatal contradiction: in a bunker, who will grow the food? Who will care for the children? Without mutual trust, the guards end up eliminating those they protect.

03

The only thing we have never managed to automate is trust. It cannot be optimized, cannot be coded. It is built over time, which Silicon Valley has never known how to model.
