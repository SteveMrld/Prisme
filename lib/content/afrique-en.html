On July 26, 2023, General Abdourahamane Tiani seized power in Niger. It was the third coup in the Sahel in two years, following Mali in 2021 and Burkina Faso in 2022. In the days that followed, thousands of Nigeriens took to the streets of Niamey, some brandishing Russian flags, others chanting anti-French slogans. The French embassy was stormed. In less than six weeks, France was forced to withdraw its soldiers and its ambassador. A military presence spanning several decades collapsed in a matter of weeks.

This was not an accident. Nor was it a surprise to anyone who had been watching Africa without Paris's lens. It was the culmination of a long, foreseeable process that French diplomacy had systematically refused to acknowledge.

  • 2Russia, China, and Turkey are not offering a better model. They are offering a model without conditions, which, for governments little concerned with their people, is a difficult offer to refuse.
  • 3A lasting relationship between France and Africa is possible, but it requires a genuine break with Françafrique, not in speeches, but in practice.

The continent France thought it knew

Sub-Saharan Africa today counts 60% of its population below the age of 25. This figure is not a statistic among others; it is a geopolitical force in formation. A youth that did not experience colonization but has inherited its resentments and its narratives. A youth that looks at France differently from its parents, and which possesses, through social media, an unprecedented capacity for organization and expression in the history of the continent.

France, for its part, continued to look at Africa as it always had: through the prism of its own interests, its own fears, and its own narratives. It maintained military bases as guarantees of stability, diplomatic networks as tools of influence, Francophone elites as natural interlocutors. It believed these structures were sufficient to secure its presence. It was wrong.

A record that must be named

Relations between France and Africa bear the marks of a singularly painful past. Slavery, colonization, pillaging of resources, support for authoritarian regimes within the framework of Françafrique, this system put in place from the moment of independence allowed Paris to maintain political and economic influence over its former colonies at the cost of silent complicity with corrupt elites.1 For decades, France protected leaders with little concern for their populations' development in exchange for preferential contracts, access to raw materials, and strategic positions.

This record is damning. It must be named without seeking to minimize it, not to condemn present-day France, but because no lasting relationship can be built on consensual amnesia. African peoples remember. And new generations have access to the archives.

It would be too simple, however, and intellectually dishonest, to make France the exclusive scapegoat for Africa's difficulties. Certain African states, particularly in francophone Africa, must confront their own responsibilities. Too many leaders have chosen paths of corruption and personal enrichment at the expense of their populations. This dual gaze, at both external and internal responsibilities, is the condition for an honest analysis.

"True development cannot be stolen by anyone; it must be the work of the people themselves."

Thomas Sankara, President of Burkina Faso (1983–1987)

A continent all the powers are contesting

Africa is not merely a developing region. It is a first-order power stake.

Faced with these resources, the great powers did not come out of idealism. China has invested massively in African infrastructure since the 2000s, building roads, ports, and railways, with counterparts in terms of access to raw materials and political influence.2 Russia played the sovereignty card against Western interference, mobilizing Wagner and then its successors to offer Sahelian juntas what France could no longer sell them: military protection without democratic conditions.3 The United States, through AFRICOM created in 2007, maintains a discreet but structuring military presence across the continent.

Africa risks finding itself, once again, a playground for foreign interests, with new actors but the same logic of extraction. This is precisely what African youth refuses: to be the spectator of a geopolitical drama whose consequences it will bear.

The signals France failed to see

The coups in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger were not accidents. They were announced earthquakes, preceded by rumblings that French diplomacy systematically underestimated. French military presence, perceived as protective in Paris, was experienced as sovereign humiliation in Bamako, Ouagadougou, and Niamey. French military bases, far from being guarantees of stability, had become symbols of dependence that the juntas exploited with formidable skill.4

France was deaf to the urgent demands of African populations who claimed to be treated with respect and dignity. It maintained for too long a paternalistic vision of a continent it believed it knew because it had been present there, without understanding that this presence itself had become the problem.

2023
Niger expels the French ambassador and terminates military cooperation agreements. In less than two years, France had lost its last military positions in the Sahel, Mali, Burkina, Niger, without having had time to negotiate a dignified exit.

In 2007, Nicolas Sarkozy delivered a speech at Cheikh Anta Diop University in Dakar that crystallized everything Africa held against France. Henri Guaino had written for him that "the African man had not sufficiently entered history." With the kind of irony that only History knows how to distill, it is now France that risks exiting African history, not by its own decision, but under the force of events it failed to anticipate.

What remains to be built

Françafrique is dissolving. This is not necessarily bad news, provided France knows what to put in its place. The mistake would be to treat this withdrawal as a defeat to be erased, or as a liberation allowing a definitive turning of the page. Neither of these postures corresponds to the reality of a sixty-year relationship that, despite everything, has produced real economic, cultural, and human ties.

Europe as a whole has a role to play here that France alone can no longer assume. Italy, through its Mediterranean position, can strengthen commercial exchanges. Germany can share its industrial expertise. Spain can facilitate dialogue on migration issues. Together, these countries can offer Africa what neither China nor Russia provides: a partnership that acknowledges its history, respects its sovereignty, and is not conditioned on the extraction of its resources.

But this requires a prerequisite that France still struggles to fully assume: naming what has been, without evasion. Acknowledging that the record is not acceptable. Not out of masochism, not out of ideology, but because no lasting relationship between peoples can be built on a shared lie.

Africa is on the move. Its youth is shaping its destiny with an energy the entire world is beginning to measure. France can still choose to be its partner, provided it finally accepts being its equal.
