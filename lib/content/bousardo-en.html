A lawyer since 2006, Vanessa Bousardo holds a master's degree in general private law from the University of Paris II. Trained as a civil-law jurist, she has practiced criminal law for twenty years. In 2010, she became Secretary of the Conference of the Paris Bar, that institution founded in 1810 through which generations of jurists who became consciences have passed.

She worked for nearly ten years alongside the bâtonnier Pierre-Olivier Sur before founding her own firm dedicated to criminal litigation and white-collar criminal law in 2016. A member of the Bar Council from 2019 to 2021, she was elected on 29 June 2023 Vice-bâtonnière of the Paris Bar for the 2024-2025 term, and became at the same time president of the EFB, the largest school for lawyers in Europe.

In October 2024, she launched the Appel des Bâtonnières du Monde (Call of the Women Heads of Bars of the World), today an association that she chairs and that brings together more than sixty women who head or have headed bars across the world, from Abidjan to Washington, by way of Ukraine or Dallas. The association carries a strong conviction: the law is one of the most powerful levers for advancing equality.

Soara met with her.

France has a tradition of law and of defense that is almost a culture in its own right. Badinter, Halimi. Lawyers who were the consciences of their time. But this tradition long had a single face. What does the law represent for you, intimately, you who were the first Antillean woman lawyer to reach the head of the Paris Bar?

The law was a revelation for me. I had first taken a detour through a first year of medicine, which can, in fact, be reduced to a few weeks. Very quickly, I knew, with regret, that I would not stay there.

The following year, when I arrived on the benches of Paris-X Nanterre, today Paris Nanterre University, it was self-evident. More than that: a revelation. I felt at once in the right place, where I belonged.

I discovered the law as one discovers a new language, a new reasoning, a new way of looking at the world, of decoding it. The legal syllogism, the rigor of the reasoning, the capacity to qualify the facts, to name situations, to render them in legal terms: all of this gripped me at once.

Each area of the law became for me an illumination of the world. From constitutional law to family law, by way of criminal law, the law was for me a user's manual of our society. From then on, I had the feeling of seeing reality differently, through the prism of the law.

Because, at bottom, everything is law. No element of our daily life truly escapes it: the family, work, freedom, responsibility, speech, the body, social relations. The law is everywhere, and I find that fascinating.

But I also understood, as my studies went on, that beyond the intellectual interest, if the law is an extremely powerful tool, it is so only for those who know it, who gain access to that knowledge.

The law is a matter of power that discriminates against those who cannot assert it, who cannot make it enforceable for lack of access to its knowledge. Not knowing one's rights creates and aggravates vulnerability.

This obviously touches on the question of access to the law, which is a major issue. There is no possible access to justice without access to the law. The law should not be a luxury.

I did my first four years of law at Nanterre, then my fifth year at Panthéon-Assas University. And I believe it was also over the course of this path that my desire to become a lawyer took shape. At the time I knew no lawyers. It was a world I was entirely unaware of, very far from my daily life and my circle. But I allowed myself to dream of it.

And in September 2004, there was a turning point. I passed the entrance exam for the bar training school. What I felt then was a kind of appeasement. Something deep, almost silent. What had been in the conditional was now certain: I would be a lawyer.

Is there a book, a text, a word that founded your relationship to justice? Something you read or heard that told you this was where you had to be?

The Execution and Abolition, which were the bedside books of a great many students, had a strong impact on me. At a moment when I was destining myself for contract law, for business law, I was literally seized by criminal law, by the desire, the need to defend the rights of those subject to the law, whether accused or victims. That drive still animates me.

You have a mantra: "One must never forbid oneself anything." Where does this conviction come from?

It comes from my mother. Very early, she pushed me, encouraged me, carried me. She always passed on to me the idea that work, from the tenderest childhood, is a means of tracing one's own trajectory, of overcoming social determinisms and of refusing to let oneself be assigned a place.

This mantra comes from there: from that deep conviction that one must never forbid oneself anything in advance. Neither social origin, nor the territory where one grows up, nor the name one bears, nor gender, nor age, nor even the codes one possesses or does not should determine the place one can occupy in society.

I am very attached to the question of equality of opportunity, but also to that of equality of possibilities. And unfortunately, this remains a major issue, because while we are equal in law, in fact, real equality remains a harsh daily struggle.

Opportunities still depend too often on the environment in which one moves, on the encounters one makes, on the encouragement one receives or does not. It is not always easy, but I have learned that one had to go out and seek opportunities, cross certain barriers and not forbid oneself to try.

For me, injustice begins when some people take in very early the idea that their dreams have limits set by others: "this is not for me." I find that sentence deeply violent. And it is probably against it that I have always moved forward.

Of course, forbidding oneself nothing does not mean never doubting. It does not prevent failures, or self-questioning, or sometimes anguish. But it consists in refusing to have the path traced before it has even been attempted. It is a message I believe it is important to pass on to the younger generations.

You speak of that sentence, "this is not for me," as a form of violence. You, the first Antillean woman lawyer at the head of the Paris Bar, did you hear it, and how did you hold it at a distance?

No, I was not directly confronted with a challenge to my legitimacy or my place, whether when I aspired to the office of Vice-bâtonnière of the Paris Bar by launching into the campaign, or afterward in the exercise of my duties. Some may have thought it, but I no doubt arranged not to hear it in order to move forward.

A bar rests on traditions and values to which I am deeply attached. But it seemed to me important that these traditions also adapt to our era, to the contemporary difficulties of lawyers, to those who practice in small structures, but also to the situation of women, still too often confronted with obstacles within the profession.

I wanted to carry these fights for my bar. And it is this commitment that carried me, with, no doubt, a sense of responsibility aimed at showing that, whatever our paths and our origins, everyone has a role to play.

I have never experienced the origins that are mine as an obstacle. Quite the contrary. They are a richness, a part of my history, and they necessarily nourish the gaze I have today and what I am able to bring.

On the other hand, that sentence, "this is not for me," I hear a great deal among young people. And yes, I find it extremely violent, because it speaks of the limitation of dreams, of prospects and of ambitions. It is the expression of untapped potential, of personalities that do not manage to unfold because they took in very early that this or that path was not made for them, often because society itself sends back to them a compartmentalized image of what they might aspire to.

I believe one must fight against that.

There was Maëlys, Lola, then Lyhanna, eleven years old, whose presumed murderer had been the subject of two complaints dismissed without action. The gendarmerie receives four complaints from minors per hour, every day of the year. And in April 2025, the European Court of Human Rights condemned France for its failings in the handling of sexual violence against minors. With each tragedy, the same promises. When an institution repeats the same errors after being condemned for committing them, can it still reform itself?

The fight against sexual violence toward women, but also toward children, has been presented for years as a national priority. The daily judicial handling of these cases nonetheless still struggles to give substance to that ambition. Delays lengthen, difficulties persist, and the tragedy of little Lyhanna has, once again, tragically recalled it.

Of course, no one contests the significant budgetary efforts that have been made in recent years for justice. But these efforts, however substantial they may be, are not enough to make up for decades of underinvestment in justice.

At the same time, the number of complaints continues to rise. The courts struggle to absorb the volume of cases. Magistrates, investigators and civil servants face considerable pressure linked to the mass of proceedings and to the insufficiency of available means, which makes the real-time handling of cases almost impossible.

Of course, malfunctions can exist, but I believe that beyond individual situations, we must have the courage to question more fundamentally the fit of our judicial organization to the scale of the violence.

How can one not wonder about the way this national priority is handled day to day when legal professionals, beginning with lawyers, are the first witnesses to the months, often the long years, required to handle these cases that are nonetheless particularly serious?

A thorough reflection is called for. The question of means is essential, but it is not the only one. It is the very architecture of our judicial response that deserves today to be rethought.

It is precisely from this observation that I had proposed, as early as August 2025, in an op-ed published in Le Monde, to open a reflection on the creation of a national prosecutor's office for the fight against sexual and gender-based violence. Not to mechanically reproduce a foreign model, even if the Spanish experience obviously deserves to be observed with attention, but because it appears more and more clearly that our current organization does not make it possible to respond fully to the scale of the phenomenon.

When a phenomenon becomes massive, repeated, and affects thousands of victims, it can no longer be treated as ordinary litigation. It calls for a strong, coherent and specialized institutional response.

I do not share the analysis according to which a national prosecutor's office would not be suited to this litigation because of its massive character, and because these cases require proximity. I believe, on the contrary, that it is precisely because this litigation is massive that a national coordination becomes necessary.

When a phenomenon reaches such a systemic dimension, one can no longer accept that the responses provided vary according to the jurisdictions or according to the services in charge of the investigations.

To allow oneself to reflect on the creation of a national prosecutor's office for the fight against sexual and gender-based violence does not mean calling for a centralized single window. It would rather be a prosecutor's office of steering and coordination, continuing to rely on the territorial prosecutors while ensuring an overall coherence, harmonizing practices, disseminating the most effective investigative methods, facilitating the circulation of information and coordinating serial or particularly complex cases, notably when they involve numerous victims or several judicial districts.

And this, in the image of the national prosecutors' offices with which the Republic has equipped itself in recent years. By way of illustration, the National Financial Prosecutor's Office does not handle the whole of French financial crime. More recently, the National Anti-Organized-Crime Prosecutor's Office is not meant to handle the entirety of organized-crime cases in France. These offices, on the other hand, exercise a function of impulse, of coordination, of specialization and, in certain cases, take up the most complex or the most sensitive cases.

It is true that proximity is indispensable for the reception of victims, for field investigations and for the daily handling of proceedings. But proximity does not preclude coordination; on the contrary, it makes it all the more necessary, so as to prevent proximity from leading to a geographic lottery, depending on the place where the complaint is filed.

There also arises the question of the modernization of the files, of their interconnection and of the sharing of information among the various actors. As long as we do not have a global, structural and operational vision of this fight, we will remain in incantation rather than in action.

If we genuinely consider that the fight against sexual violence is a national priority, then this priority must be embodied institutionally and over time.

You say yourself that justice lacks means and is buckling under cases. To create a national prosecutor's office, is that not to add a layer of coordination to an already congested machine, rather than giving it the means it lacks?

The idea is not to create an additional bureaucratic level in a judicial architecture already under strain, but on the contrary to structure the existing in order to make the judicial response more effective in the face of a phenomenon that is massive, systemic and unevenly handled across territories.

By definition, reorganization, structuring and specialization are not meant to weigh down the criminal response, but to make it flow more smoothly.

And once again, it would not be a question of divesting the local prosecutors in order to send the totality of the litigation up to Paris, with the exception of particularly complex or sensitive cases, but of making practices uniform through a national doctrine, deployed with specialized relays within the territorial prosecutors' offices.

The question of means cannot be considered independently of that of a thorough reorganization.

The Epstein affair showed that in the United States, a man rich and connected enough was able to obtain a secret agreement putting an end to federal prosecutions, immunizing his accomplices along the way. As a criminal lawyer, do you think France is structurally safe from this kind of two-speed justice?

The criticism of two-speed justice is a recurrent one.

I believe our judicial institution has demonstrated its capacity to judge and to condemn powerful figures, political, economic or media figures, without complacency. From this point of view, I am not sure one can assert that our system would structurally protect the powerful.

But it would obviously be naive to consider that a judicial system could be entirely impervious to the power relations that run through society.

It is true that a case that benefits from strong media exposure, whether linked to the fame of one of the protagonists or to the emotion it arouses, necessarily produces effects on the course of the proceedings, in any event on their timing. This media coverage can accelerate judicial time, where cases with no media coverage at all sometimes remain stuck in the bottleneck of justice.

Power relations, moreover, are not always expressed where one expects them. They can also translate into a difference in access to the law, into a greater capacity to defend oneself, to mobilize resources or to withstand the length of a procedure.

The challenge, then, is that complaints and cases can be handled with the same diligence and the same effectiveness, regardless of the fame of the persons concerned, of their media exposure or of their capacity to make their voice heard.

Justice has already made mistakes. Men have spent years in prison for crimes they did not commit. And yet, we continue to trust it, because we have no other choice. Is that trust still deserved?

Justice is, by definition, a human work. It rests on texts, on principles, on conventions, but it is rendered by women and men, so that error is always possible. It is possible when an innocent person is accused. It is also possible when a victim is not heard, is not protected.

But the question of trust toward justice today clearly goes beyond the sole question of judicial error. What is at stake today is a genuine crisis of confidence toward the judicial institution, and the distrust is deep.

It is reproached at times for its laxity, at times for its severity. And it is increasingly charged with a form of impunity, in the face of the numerous dismissals without action and the excessive delays in the handling of cases.

Now a justice that no longer manages to be rendered within reasonable times inevitably generates frustration and a sense of injustice, and even of denial of justice.

As lawyers, we are often helpless in the face of the passing time and of the litigants who challenge us about this long wait. What can one answer to a victim of incest who, after more than ten years, has still not seen her case called before a court?

The risk, with this crisis of confidence, is that the criticism of an institution turns into a contestation of its legitimacy. That seems to me extremely dangerous, because justice is one of the most fundamental expressions of our social pact. It is a central pillar of our democracy. A society that no longer believes in its justice is a society that runs the risk of fracturing.

The current situation is, moreover, paradoxical, because this crisis of confidence toward the judicial institution manifests itself at a moment when there is expressed, more than ever, with force, a thirst, an expectation of justice within public opinion. This contradiction must alert us, because it says something about the state of our democracy.

As I indicated earlier, I think we will not be able to do without a deep reflection on our judicial system, on its means and on its effectiveness.

I also believe that the question of trust in justice goes through a better knowledge of the law. I believe deeply in education in the law. There is no access to justice without access to the law, without knowledge of one's rights. The law must not remain the thing of jurists alone.

If justice must be rendered within the judicial precinct, as opposed to the danger of the media trial, the law, for its part, is lived day to day, even beyond the courtrooms. It is lived in ordinary situations: access to an administration, to a school, to a social benefit, to a remedy.

The law is not an abstraction. The rule of law makes us a democracy, but let us not forget that it is measured above all by our capacity to make rights effective.

To defend someone everyone has already condemned, to assist a victim whose word no one wants to hear. What do twenty years in the courtroom do to someone inwardly?

This question puts its finger on the very definition of the lawyer's profession. It is to accompany. It is to stand at the side of the one we accuse. It is also to assist the one who asks for justice and whose word people do not want to hear.

After twenty years in the courtroom, one also understands how difficult it can be to speak for oneself, whether it is a matter of defending oneself, of recounting what one has undergone or of facing a procedure against the judicial machine.

This profession brings you into people's lives, sometimes into their intimacy. One becomes the keeper of a story, of a pain, sometimes of a shame, of an anger, of a silence.

Inwardly, it requires one to take in much suffering without merging with it, without losing oneself in it. One must find the right distance so as not to substitute oneself for the one we defend or accompany, but to allow him to stand upright in a moment that is always, for the person concerned, a personal ordeal.

Twenty years in the courtroom also give a direct look at our society. One sees there the violence, the flaws, broken lives, human contradictions. But one also sees the strength of certain people, their dignity, their courage, their capacity to get through the ordeal.

All of this certainly makes one more lucid, perhaps more grave as well. But it recalls, each day, why this profession is necessary.

You speak of the right distance so as not to lose oneself. Is there a case, a face, where that distance gave way, and that has never left you?

I believe I have, so far, managed to keep the right distance. But there are obviously cases, encounters, that one does not forget, because they mark one more deeply.

I think first of a young man I had defended when I was still a young lawyer. He was accused of criminal acts and proclaimed his innocence. He had been placed in pre-trial detention for a few months, before getting out and benefiting from a dismissal of the case.

I have the memory of an endearing personality, of a young man with whom the exchange was at first very fluid, very simple, and with whom contact was established naturally.

And then, after these months of incarceration, I remember receiving him at the firm and not immediately identifying what had changed, but I understood fairly quickly that he was no longer quite the same.

Something in him had closed up. Something had, as it were, moved away from reality. I remember him in the meeting room, almost in dialogue with himself.

In the weeks and months that followed, he became extremely aggressive. This troubled me deeply, because I will never know whether prison provoked this decompensation or whether it revealed a psychic fragility that pre-existed.

But I saw, in a very concrete way, the impact that incarceration can have on a human being, the deep traces it can leave. That marked me and is not forgotten.

More recently, I also think of a woman, a mother, who had the courage to denounce acts of incest suffered when she was a child.

We filed a complaint nearly ten years ago, and the investigation is still ongoing. Very regularly, month after month, I receive the same requests, the same follow-ups from her in order to know when the investigation will be closed and the case judged.

And tirelessly, I answer her that it will take more time still, even years. Except that this time, she is not sure she has.

A few months ago, she learned that she was suffering from a serious illness. She then shared with me her fear of perhaps never being able to attend "her trial."

That sentence deeply shook me.

It speaks of all the violence of judicial time when it becomes inhuman. It also speaks of how much a criminal procedure, by its length, can add suffering to suffering.

For me, it is the very face of secondary victimization, that moment when the procedure, which should protect, recognize, judge, ends up producing additional suffering for the person who had the courage to file a complaint.

For the lawyer, it is very difficult, because it places us before a strong sense of powerlessness, even as our role is to explain, to accompany and to defend.

You say that women are the majority in the profession but absent where decisions are made. Is it a problem of generation that will resolve itself with time, or something structural that resists?

I do not believe one can reduce this question to a simple matter of generation that would resolve itself mechanically with time. Of course, the progress is real, but it is never secured.

Women are today present in nearly all professional sectors. Yet, when one observes the places where decisions are made, the leadership positions, the functions of representation or the highest responsibilities, the balances remain very different. It is precisely this gap between the reality of the base and that of power that gives pause.

With regard, for example, to the profession of lawyer, it has been profoundly transformed. Women today represent nearly 60 percent of lawyers at the national level, and trainee lawyers are very largely women. Yet this reality is not found symmetrically in the pyramid of responsibilities, whether it is a matter of access to partnership, of leadership functions or indeed of levels of remuneration.

As I had occasion to recall in Washington, at the invitation of the Women's Bar Association last May, presence is not power.

It is in this context that I launched, in October 2024, the Appel des Bâtonnières du Monde. This collective, today become an association, brings together more than sixty women who have headed or are heading bars across the world, whether in Europe, in Africa or in the United States. This initiative rests on a simple conviction, that the law is a powerful tool of transformation which it is up to us to mobilize in favor of equality.

To defend the place of women is a deeply humanist fight. It is about defending equality. And indeed, defending equality and defending the rule of law are not two distinct missions, they are the two faces of one and the same commitment. For the rule of law is not simply a legal framework, it is the bedrock of fundamental rights, which protect individuals, guarantee freedoms and allow equality to be something other than a mere promise.

It was important for me to carry this message to New York last March, then to Washington, because at a moment when the rule of law and women's rights can be weakened, it is essential to carry these values beyond our borders.

I believe that time, on its own, is not enough to change things. Change supposes constant vigilance and concrete commitments.

In what world do you want to plead in twenty years?

In twenty years, it is very difficult to project oneself, to imagine precisely the functioning of justice, so fully are we today at a real turning point.

Justice must respond to ever stronger expectations from citizens, to a considerable mass of litigation, to constraints of means that remain significant. At the same time, technological developments are already profoundly upending our way of working.

It is obvious that artificial intelligence will be more than ever at the heart of the functioning of the judicial institution, but one must hope that it allows us to gain in efficiency, to shorten delays, to better organize the courts and to make the handling of cases flow more smoothly.

But above all, I hope that it will allow us to free up time for the essential, namely the time of the hearing, the time of speech, the time of listening.

I would also like us to have succeeded in building a more specialized, more legible and more protective justice.

Because beyond the decisions it renders, justice itself sometimes produces suffering. I think notably of the victims who must repeat the same facts on multiple occasions, of the procedures that stretch out over too many long years, of what is called secondary victimization.

I would like that, in twenty years, to bring a matter before justice no longer be perceived as an additional risk to take, but as the assurance of being accompanied, protected and heard throughout the procedure.

It is perhaps an ambitious wish, but I believe that each generation has the responsibility to improve the institutions it receives in order to pass them on more just to the next.
