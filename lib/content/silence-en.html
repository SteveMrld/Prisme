Every era has its moral dilemmas. Every generation, its moments of truth when tepidity is no longer an option. From one era to the next, what changes is the price one consents to pay to avoid having to choose. In the times we are living through, that price is called silent consent. And it is higher than we believe.

The silences that afflict

There are silences that soothe and others that afflict. The silence of contemplation, of modesty, of private grief: these are necessary, they are forms of respect. But there is another kind: the silence that wraps itself around compromise, that gives cowardice the appearance of wisdom, that transforms fear of judgment into an alleged neutrality. This silence thrives in institutional corridors, in boardrooms, in newsrooms, in embassies. It has a name that no one pronounces because no one wants to apply it to themselves: complaisance.

Each day, men and women in positions of knowledge, decision-making, and warning choose to look away. They clothe themselves in a comfortable resignation, excused by feigned ignorance. They wear their masks with deceptive ease, convinced that going along with a rigged game is a form of wisdom, that neutrality is a virtue, that waiting will eventually set things right. It never sets things right. It aggravates them silently.

Indignation, far from being audacity, should arise as self-evident, the first arrow of a liberating volley. The choice of silence often hides behind the excuse that this is neither the place nor the moment to speak. But every place and every moment carries its share of truth. So many events over the years should have awakened consciences, and instead dissolved into near-general apathy. What a sadness to see so much courage wasted. When an order collapses, it is often the reflection of small past decisions which, subtly, paved the way to disaster.

In a world where silence is interpreted as tacit approval, not speaking is already a position. It is the most cowardly of all.

These consenting silences feed a collective unease. They allow unacceptable situations to become ordinary, through the simple play of repetition and habituation. It is these silences, added one to the next, that constitute the breeding ground of every drift.

Courage as a requirement

The world taking shape today is not the fruit of fatality. It is the patient result of thousands of consented silences, of eyes averted at the right moment, of hands extended where they should have been withheld. Every time a game was manifestly rigged and no one said a word, the rigger took note. Every time a line was crossed without anyone standing up, that line became the new norm. Political, economic, and moral catastrophes do not emerge from nothing. They are preceded by a long series of renunciations that each person, taken individually, found reasonable.

True courage resides in speech. In the audacity to express oneself even at the cost of ostracism. This is not heroism. It is a discipline of life, a refusal to lie to oneself about what one sees. Influence, exercised with discernment, must support worthy causes, without pretension or arrogance, but with conviction. It is infinitely preferable to stand alone, resolute and proud, rather than join the comfortable ranks of those who look away.

The voices that refused

History has its lucid witnesses. Those who, in moments of rupture, refused comfortable blindness. Maryse Condé, whose narratives weave connections between cultures and eras thought separate, and who never ceased naming what others preferred to leave unspoken. Césaire, who refused negotiated dignity. Hugo, who saw in the silence of the powerful the factory of coming catastrophes. Camus, who understood that abstention is itself a choice, and that this choice has a price. These voices did not ask to be applauded. They asked not to be alone.

Speaking out is not a heroic act. It is simply refusing to be complicit. It is recognizing that silence has a cost, that it is always paid, and that it is rarely those who stayed silent who settle the bill. Peoples, for their part, always render their verdict in the end. For better or for worse. That is what makes silence so grave. And speech so necessary.
