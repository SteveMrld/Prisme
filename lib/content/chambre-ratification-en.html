This is not an image of war. No smoke over Tehran, no press conference, no missile in flight. It is an interior image, almost intimate in its geography of power.

The black SUV carrying Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu arrived at the White House just before 11 a.m. on February 11. The Israeli leader, who had been pressing the United States for months to approve a major offensive against Iran, was ushered inside without ceremony, out of sight of reporters.

American and Israeli officials first gathered in the Cabinet Room, adjacent to the Oval Office. Then Netanyahu went down for the main event: a highly classified presentation on Iran for President Trump and his team in the Situation Room, a space rarely used for in-person meetings with foreign leaders.

Trump sat down, but not in his usual seat at the head of the mahogany table. He took a seat on the side, facing the large screens mounted along the wall. Netanyahu sat on the other side, directly across from the president. On the screens behind him appeared David Barnea, the Mossad director, alongside Israeli military officers, creating the image of a wartime leader surrounded by his team.

Seventeen days later, on February 28, the United States went to war with Iran.

I. The scene

Netanyahu delivered his presentation in a "confident monotone," according to the New York Times. His thesis: Iran was ripe for regime change. He outlined four conditions described as heralding near-certain victory: Iran's ballistic program destroyed within weeks; the regime too weakened to close the Strait of Hormuz; retaliation against American interests judged minimal; and Mossad intelligence indicating that protests would resume inside Iran, allowing the opposition to topple the regime.

At one point, the Israelis showed a short video — a montage of potential leaders for a post-Islamist Iran. Among them, Reza Pahlavi: the exiled son of the last Shah, a Washington-based dissident.

"Sounds good to me."
— Donald Trump, responding to Netanyahu. Situation Room, February 11, 2026. Source: New York Times

The following day, in a meeting limited to American officials only, the intelligence services delivered their verdict. The first two objectives — decapitating the regime and dismantling Iranian military capabilities — were achievable. The popular uprising and regime change were "detached from reality."

"Farcical."
John Ratcliffe, CIA Director.

"In other words, it's bullshit."
Marco Rubio, Secretary of State.

"Sir, this is, in my experience, standard operating procedure for the Israelis. They oversell, and their plans are not always well-developed. They know they need us, and that's why they're hard-selling."
General Dan Caine, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff.

"If our goal is regime change or an uprising, we shouldn't do it. But if the goal is to destroy Iran's missile program, that's a goal we can achieve."
Marco Rubio, final meeting of February 26. Source: New York Times — Haberman & Swan, April 7, 2026

Tucker Carlson had come to the Oval Office several times to warn Trump that a war with Iran would destroy his presidency. A few weeks before hostilities began, Trump reassured him by phone: "I know you're worried about it, but it's going to be OK." Carlson asked how he knew. "Because it always is."

On February 26, the final meeting. Vance: "You know I think this is a bad idea, but if you want to do it, I'll support you." Hegseth, the only enthusiast: "We're going to have to take care of the Iranians eventually, so we might as well do it now." Each stated their reservations. Each fell in line.

"I think we need to do it."
Donald Trump, final Situation Room meeting, February 26, 2026.

"Operation Epic Fury is approved. No aborts. Good luck."
Order transmitted from Air Force One, February 27, 22 minutes before the deadline set by General Caine. Source: New York Times — Haberman & Swan, April 7, 2026

The New York Times concluded: "The U.S. decision to strike Iran was a victory for Mr. Netanyahu."

II. Thirteen days

Sixty-four years earlier, same city, same building. On October 16, 1962, McGeorge Bundy woke Kennedy at 9 a.m. A U-2 spy plane had photographed Soviet missile launch sites in Cuba.

Kennedy assembled fourteen advisers, the ExComm. From the first meeting, he asked a question no one else asked: "We ought to think of why the Russians did this." Understand the adversary's logic before deciding how to respond. This reflex had been forged by the Bay of Pigs, in 1961, where he had blindly trusted the CIA and the generals. He had said afterward: "How could I have been so stupid?" He had not forgotten.

He was also reading. Barbara Tuchman's The Guns of August, the story of how European leaders had slid into World War I without ever truly deciding to want it. General Curtis LeMay told him that refusing to strike Cuba immediately was the equivalent of Munich. Kennedy held.

The most dangerous day was Saturday, October 27, "Black Saturday." An American U-2 had been shot down over Cuba. And Khrushchev had just sent a second public letter, harder than the first: withdrawal of American missiles from Turkey in exchange for withdrawal of Soviet missiles from Cuba. The entire ExComm wanted to ignore this letter. Everyone: McNamara, Bundy, Rusk, Thompson, Robert Kennedy himself. It would make America look weak. It would compromise NATO.

Kennedy listened. Then acted differently. Publicly, he responded to Friday's letter — that is what his advisers wanted. But that evening, he sent Robert Kennedy to meet secretly with Soviet Ambassador Dobrynin. Message: the Jupiter missiles would be withdrawn from Turkey within six months. A secret deal. That was the real compromise Kennedy had sought from the beginning, obtained from the side, unsaid.

"These generals have a great advantage: if we listen to them and do what they want, none of us will be alive later to tell them they were wrong."
— John F. Kennedy, to aide Dave Powers. October 1962.

The crisis was resolved. The world did not burn. And during those thirteen days, no foreign leader crossed the door of the Situation Room to convince Kennedy of anything.

In 1962, Kennedy is the most diplomatically inclined president in a room full of hawks. His instincts push toward compromise, against the advice of his own institutions. He prevails.

In 2026, Trump is the most war-inclined president in a room where all his advisers tell him the plan is "farcical." His instincts push toward conflict, against the advice of his own institutions. He also prevails.

The direction of resistance is exactly inverted. The institutional outcome is identical: the president overrides. What this reveals is not a question of personality — it is a question of system.

III. Washington as a ratification chamber

1962
Cuba, Kennedy

President's instincts: toward diplomacy. Institutional pressure: toward strike. Kennedy resists his generals. No foreign leader in the room. Outcome: no war.

2026
Iran, Trump

President's instincts: toward war. CIA, Joint Chiefs, Vice President say "farcical." Trump overrides. A foreign leader framed the decision. Outcome: Epic Fury.

In 1962, the threat was established by American services, deliberated among Americans, resolved by a president who resisted his own hawks.

In 2026, the threat was defined, framed, and sold by a foreign leader. The hawks pushing for war are not the American generals — they are the ones saying "farcical" and "bullshit." And yet war occurred.

General Caine kept asking: "And then what?" But Trump, the New York Times notes, "seemingly heard only what he wanted to hear." This is not an individual failure. It is a description of a system where deliberation has been replaced by deference.

In 1962, the president was the filter between the bellicose impulses of his institutions and the final decision. In 2026, that filter is gone. The CIA still says "farcical" when a plan is farcical. Generals still calculate risks. What changed is what the president chooses to hear.

IV. The seventy-year loop

In the February 11 presentation, a detail the press almost entirely ignored: among the faces in the video projected on the Situation Room screens was Reza Pahlavi, "the exiled son of Iran's last shah, now a Washington-based dissident who had tried to position himself as a secular leader who could shepherd Iran toward a post-theocratic government," according to the New York Times.

Seventy years after the CIA had installed his father in power in Tehran, the son appeared on the same screens, in the same city, presented as the solution to a problem his father had himself engendered.

On August 19, 1953, Operation Ajax overthrew Mohammad Mossadegh, the democratically elected prime minister who had dared to nationalize Iranian oil. Kermit Roosevelt arrived in Tehran with suitcases of cash to buy crowds, corrupt newspapers, manufacture an opposition. The Shah was restored to his throne. He ruled for twenty-six years with the SAVAK, a secret police trained by the CIA, as his instrument of terror. In 1979, the Islamic Revolution produced exactly what America feared most. Khamenei drew a lesson stated without ambiguity: "We are not liberals like Allende and Mossadegh, whom the CIA can strangle."

1953 creates the Shah. The Shah creates the Revolution. The Revolution creates the Islamic Republic. The Islamic Republic creates the nuclear program. The nuclear program creates the threat Netanyahu sells to Trump in 2026. And on the Situation Room screens appears Reza Pahlavi, son of the regime fabricated by the CIA, proposed as the remedy for the disease that regime itself engendered.

Repeating the same acts while hoping for different results is strategic madness. Repeating the same acts while having forgotten one already performed them is something else entirely.

V. The spectacle and the decision

In 1962, Kennedy received black-and-white photographs taken by a spy plane. Cold, technical images, without narration. He had to construct the interpretation himself, project the consequences himself. It was this work — nourished by reading Tuchman, informed by the failure of the Bay of Pigs — that enabled him to resist his generals.

In 2026, Trump received a production. Large screens. Mossad director on video conference from Tel Aviv. Israeli officers in the background. A video, faces, names, a promise of a future. "It seemed to land well with the most important person in the room," the New York Times writes. The Situation Room had become a screening room.

Netanyahu did not sell a war plan. He sold a war film — clear exposition, credible action, happy ending. That Iran has been launching missiles every day for six weeks, that the Strait of Hormuz is closed, that the popular uprising did not occur — the film had already accomplished its work.

The New York Times reports that Trump's advisers saw him as "a great man of history" with an almost superstitious confidence in his instincts, and that his decision "was not driven by intelligence assessments or a strategic consensus among his advisers, which did not exist." Kennedy mistrusted his instincts. He subjected them to the test of facts, history, the adversary's logic. This is not a character trait: it is a method. And the method makes all the difference between deliberation and a film.

Epilogue

The ceasefire has been announced. Iran says the strait is closed. Netanyahu says the finger is on the trigger. The enriched uranium has not been removed. And Reza Pahlavi is still in Washington.

Seventy years after his father was installed in power by the CIA, the son waits in the wings of a story that repeats itself, without even bothering to change its title.

If Washington has become a ratification chamber, the next question is immediate: ratification of what, and by whom, next time?
