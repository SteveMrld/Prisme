The cobalt powering your electric car battery almost certainly comes from Katanga. In this province of southeastern Democratic Republic of Congo, children dig with their bare hands in improvised shafts. They are called artisanal miners. Behind them, a perfectly oiled logistics chain routes the ore toward trading posts run predominantly by Chinese companies, then toward the foundries of Guangdong, then toward the battery factories of Shenzhen, then toward the electric vehicles sold as symbols of a clean future. The green transition rests on an extraction that negates all its principles. This is the fundamental contradiction of our era.

Rare earths are not, strictly speaking, geological rarities. Cerium is more abundant in the Earth's crust than copper. Neodymium, yttrium, and lanthanum are found on every continent. What is rare is their concentration in exploitable deposits, and above all the industrial capacity to refine them. This is where geopolitics begins.

The geography of the subsoil

Three zones concentrate the bulk of the strategic resources needed for the energy transition. Cobalt and coltan in Congo. Lithium in the triangle formed by Chile, Bolivia, and Argentina. Rare earths in the strict sense, those used in the permanent magnets of wind turbines and electric motors, primarily in China, Australia, and Russia.

Congo accounts for over 70% of global cobalt production. This figure alone measures the fragility of the system. A country whose GDP per capita stagnates below $600, whose eastern territory has been in chronic war for thirty years, and whose mining institutions are riddled with corruption, holds in its subsoil a key to planetary decarbonization. Western multinationals long pretended not to notice. Chinese companies, for their part, bought up Congolese mining concessions from the 2000s onward, methodically, without compunction.

In South America, the "lithium triangle" concentrates more than half of known global reserves. Lithium is the central element of Li-ion batteries. Bolivia holds the largest theoretical reserves, but their exploitation remains embryonic, caught between the nationalizing ambitions of successive governments and the absence of industrial infrastructure. Chile extracts, Argentina follows. And in both cases, Beijing has staked its position.

The Chinese lock

China does not merely dominate extraction. It controls transformation. This is a distinction the West took decades to understand, often too late.

Today, more than 85% of global rare earth refining takes place in China. For certain critical materials such as natural graphite used in battery anodes, the share exceeds 90%. Even Australian or African ores ultimately transit through Chinese foundries for lack of alternative. Beijing built this position over thirty years of massive investment, industrial subsidies, and a deliberate tolerance of environmental externalities that liberal democracies could not afford politically.

In July 2023, China announced export restrictions on gallium and germanium, two critical metals for semiconductors and military optics. In October of the same year, graphite followed. These decisions are not accidents. They constitute a calculated show of force: Beijing signals to Washington and Brussels that it holds a considerable leverage, and that it will not hesitate to use it if the technology war intensifies.

The West's belated awakening

Europe was slow to name the problem. For two decades, dependence on Chinese raw materials was absorbed into the rhetoric of free trade and mutually beneficial interdependence. The Covid-19 pandemic, then the war in Ukraine, shattered this illusion. When supply chains seized up, European capitals discovered their structural vulnerability with dismay.

The Critical Raw Materials Act, adopted by the European Union in 2024, sets ambitious targets: extract 10% of critical materials on European soil by 2030, process 40% domestically, and cap dependence on any single third country at 65% for each material. The objectives are clear. The means, far less so. Reopening mines in Europe runs up against authorization delays of ten to fifteen years, near-systematic local opposition, and production costs incomparable to Chinese standards.

The United States took another path with the Inflation Reduction Act: massive subsidies to rebuild a national supply chain, conditioned on materials origin. The goal is to bypass Chinese dependence through budgetary force. The result: a transatlantic subsidy war that further weakens an alliance supposed to be presenting a united front.

What this foreshadows

The rare earth battle is not a transitory episode. It is structural, and will figure in the decade ahead as one of the major axes of great power rivalry.

Several dynamics are at work simultaneously. Demand for critical materials will triple by 2030 according to the International Energy Agency, driven by electric vehicles, offshore wind, and smart electricity grids. Global South countries, which hold the reserves, are beginning to refuse the role of mere suppliers of raw materials. Indonesia banned the export of unprocessed nickel in 2020. Zambia is considering doing the same with copper. Zimbabwe suspended raw lithium exports in 2023.

This resource nationalism is not irrational. It is the assertion of an economic sovereignty long confiscated. But it considerably complicates the energy transition plans of consumer countries, which are discovering that the multipolar world they helped weaken is now capable of dictating its own terms to them.

The green transition was meant to emancipate economies from fossil fuels and, by extension, from their unstable production zones. It has created a new dependence, different in its geography, comparable in its logic. Yesterday it was Gulf oil; today it is Katanga cobalt and Andean lithium. The resources change. The power structure they generate remains remarkably constant.
