BlackRock and Vanguard,
the new masters of the game

Together, BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street manage more than $28 trillion. Never in the history of capitalism have private funds concentrated such power over the global economy.

Steve Moradel
Economics & Finance

In the third quarter of 2025, BlackRock manages $13.5 trillion in assets.¹ Vanguard surpasses $9.9 trillion, State Street $4.7 trillion.² Together, the three American funds weigh more than $28 trillion, more than the combined GDP of the United States and the eurozone. To grasp what these figures mean concretely, one reference suffices: BlackRock alone manages more than the combined GDP of Germany, Japan, and the United Kingdom. These three funds, which financial circles call the "Big Three," are shareholders in 90% of the 500 largest publicly traded companies in the S&P 500 and control between 30 and 40% of their capital.³

Little known to the general public, these funds nonetheless exercise an influence extending to every sector of the global economy: pharmaceuticals, energy, technology, defense, food, media, automotive, banking. Common ownership, the simultaneous holding of shares in competing companies, has become the rule rather than the exception. Apple, Microsoft, Coca-Cola, Pfizer, Goldman Sachs, Amazon, Tesla, Boeing, Airbus, Nestlé, Volkswagen: in each of these companies, BlackRock and Vanguard appear among the leading shareholders. This phenomenon poses a question that competition law has not yet fully absorbed.

The rise of index funds

The Big Three's dominance is the product of a transformation in the financial industry that began in the 1970s but took on an unprecedented dimension after the 2008 crisis. The central mechanism is passive management through index funds, or ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds). Rather than selecting individual securities, these funds mechanically replicate a stock market index, such as the S&P 500 or the CAC 40, at costs far below those of active management. The massive inflow of capital toward these products, at the expense of actively managed funds, has concentrated considerable sums in the hands of a small number of managers.⁴

Three funds collectively manage more than China's GDP. This level of capital concentration has no equivalent in the history of capitalism.

BlackRock accelerated this dominance through the 2009 acquisition of Barclays' iShares subsidiary, becoming within a few years the world's leading ETF issuer. Vanguard, founded in 1974 by John Bogle on the principle of the low-cost index fund, followed a parallel trajectory. The two giants now compete for the global top spot, with Vanguard growing faster than BlackRock in recent years, to the point where some analysts envision an inversion of rankings in the decade ahead.

The tool that gives BlackRock a structural advantage over its competitors is Aladdin, a proprietary risk assessment system that performs billions of calculations each month on portfolios representing more than $20 trillion in assets managed on behalf of third parties, pension funds, insurance companies, central banks, sovereign wealth funds. Aladdin is not merely an internal tool: it is an infrastructure that BlackRock rents to other financial actors, creating a technological dependence that reinforces its central position in the system.⁵

Presence in major global markets

A few examples illustrate the extent of this presence. On Wall Street, Vanguard and BlackRock are the two largest shareholders of Microsoft. Vanguard is simultaneously the largest shareholder of both Ford and General Motors. Vanguard, BlackRock, and State Street are shareholders in the three leading American pharmaceutical companies, Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, and Moderna. On the London Stock Exchange, BlackRock is the first or second shareholder of Shell, Unilever, HSBC, and AstraZeneca.⁶

On the CAC 40, BlackRock alone accounted for 2.4% of the index in 2020, more than the French state at 2.2%, while Vanguard represented 2.1%.⁷ In Germany, the two funds figure among the leading shareholders of Volkswagen, Allianz, and SAP. In emerging markets, they appear among the largest shareholders of BHP in Australia, Petrobras in Brazil, Samsung in South Korea, and Toyota in Japan.

The question raised by Vanguard founder Jack Bogle shortly before his death in 2019 remains unanswered: "If historical trends continue, a handful of giant institutional investors will one day hold voting control of virtually all large US corporations."⁸ An analysis published in 2019 in the Boston University Law Review estimated that the Big Three could control up to 40% of S&P 500 shareholder votes in the years ahead. We are there.

The ESG war, a major political turning point

For a decade, Larry Fink, BlackRock's founding CEO, used his annual letter to corporate leaders to promote what he called responsible capitalism: diversifying boards, carbon neutrality, ESG criteria (environmental, social, governance). This posture earned him the label of "new conscience of Wall Street" from some, and "extraordinarily hypocritical" from others, including billionaire Samuel Zell, who pointed to contradictions between the fund's virtuous rhetoric and its actual investments.

Between 2022 and 2025, the Big Three's ESG policies became the center of a US political battle of growing intensity. Twenty-two anti-ESG laws were passed in Republican states in 2023, sixteen more in 2024.⁹ Texas withdrew $8.5 billion from BlackRock in March 2024, bringing total withdrawals by Republican state funds to more than $13 billion.¹⁰ In December 2024, eleven Republican attorneys general filed a class action lawsuit against BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street, accusing them of using their stakes in coal companies to restrict fossil fuel production, in violation of their fiduciary obligations.¹¹ In March 2025, Vanguard agreed to pay $29.5 million to settle the dispute, committing to no longer pressure its portfolio companies to reduce carbon emissions.¹²

The funds' response to these pressures was a visible strategic retreat: BlackRock left Climate Action 100+ in early 2024, reduced its support for environmental resolutions at shareholder meetings from 21% in 2022 to 4% in 2024, and multiplied declarations asserting that its role is that of a financial service provider, not an architect of public policy.¹³ This pivot illustrates the structural limits of mega-fund power: their influence is considerable, but it operates within a space of political, regulatory, and legal constraints that is tightening.

The pivot toward private markets

The most significant movement at BlackRock in 2024 and 2025 is not ESG. It is the massive pivot toward private markets. Over twelve months, the fund spent more than $28 billion on targeted acquisitions: Global Infrastructure Partners for $12.5 billion, private markets data provider Preqin for $3.2 billion, and private credit firm HPS Investment Partners for $12 billion.¹⁴ This strategy reflects a diagnosis about future returns: in an environment of first low then high interest rates, listed markets offer increasingly compressed margins. Private equity, infrastructure, and private credit promise superior returns, at the cost of reduced liquidity.

This pivot alters the very nature of the fund. BlackRock is no longer merely a passive index manager: it is becoming a major player in the direct financing of the real economy, in sectors as strategic as energy infrastructure, data networks, and motorways. The question of systemic regulation arises with renewed urgency.

The systemic question without an answer

It was in 2009 that Barack Obama entrusted BlackRock with the task of assessing the robustness of American banks in the context of the 2008 crisis, a private fund temporarily playing the role of a regulatory authority. In 2014, the ECB asked it to assess the European banking system. In 2020, the European Commission gave it a mandate to integrate ESG criteria into banking supervision.¹⁵ These interventions, framed by team isolation arrangements, triggered recurring criticism about the porousness between private actors and public institutions.

The fundamental question remains: if the system were to derail, if BlackRock or Vanguard were to face simultaneous massive capital outflows, would the global financial system be capable of absorbing the shock? These funds are not banks, they do not have direct access to central bank liquidity facilities, and they do not benefit from the rescue mechanisms mobilized during the 2008 crisis. The flash crash of May 2010, when the Dow Jones lost 9% in a few minutes before recovering, offered a partial glimpse of this type of dynamic. With assets under management five times greater than at that time, the consequences of a similar episode would be of a different magnitude.

As for the ownership structure of these funds: BlackRock is a publicly traded company, held by a multiplicity of investment funds. Vanguard is unlisted and owned by its own funds, which are in turn owned by the investors who place their savings in them. This mutual model confers a unique governance structure. One final observation, which alone summarizes the circularity of the system: Vanguard is BlackRock's largest shareholder.
