The factories without workers,
or the end of a certain idea of labor

On the outskirts of Beijing and Shenzhen, factories run twenty-four hours a day without a single human hand touching anything. This is not a projection. It is China's industrial present, and the economic equation it poses to the rest of the world has yet to find a satisfactory answer.

In July 2024, Xiaomi inaugurated a factory in Beijing capable of producing one smartphone every three seconds. Sixty devices per minute. Three thousand six hundred per hour. Across eighty thousand square meters, eleven production lines run continuously in total darkness. No light, because the machines have no need for it. No heating, because there is no one to keep warm. No breaks, because robotic arms know neither fatigue nor labor law. This is what Chinese engineers call dark factories.

Nine years earlier, in 2015, Huawei still required ten minutes to assemble a smartphone with human intervention. The comparison says something about the speed of a transformation that has no equivalent in contemporary industrial history. The industrial revolution of the nineteenth century took several generations. This one has taken less than a decade.

What the numbers conceal

The temptation is strong to treat dark factories as a technological phenomenon, one more advance in a long series of advances. That would be a categorical error. The stakes here are economic and political before they are technical.

A traditional factory carries considerable fixed costs: wages, social contributions, leave, training, turnover, workplace accidents, union demands. These costs are frictions. They slow production, create uncertainties, give workers a form of bargaining power. A dark factory eliminates all these frictions in a single movement. Fixed capital (machines) substitutes entirely for human capital (workers). What remains is a pure production function: inputs enter, products exit, with a loss coefficient close to zero.

According to the International Federation of Robotics, China had more than 470 robots per ten thousand manufacturing employees in 2023, a figure up 70% in five years, making it the most densely robotized country in the world in absolute terms.1 This deployment is not the result of a self-regulating market. It is the outcome of a deliberate industrial policy, massively subsidized by the state, which decided that the next competitive frontier lay not in the cost of labor but in the capacity to do without it entirely.

A dark factory does not only produce cheaper goods. It produces a new structure of power between those who own the machines and those who possess nothing but their labor.

The irony of recent history

There is a fierce irony in this trajectory that Westerners struggle to face squarely. For two decades, China accepted being the world's workshop. It assembled what others designed, manufactured what others sold, exported low value-added goods in exchange for currency and technology transfers it obtained sometimes through negotiation, sometimes otherwise. It was a position of acknowledged dependence, but a temporary one.

Meanwhile, Western economies convinced themselves that this division of labor was durable because it was structural. Asia produced, Europe designed. Asia assembled, the United States innovated. This representation was comfortable and partially true. Above all, it was static, which is the most dangerous form of analytical error.

The roles have reversed with a brutality few observers anticipated. Chinese companies no longer simply assemble components designed elsewhere: they design, they patent, they export high value-added goods. BYD does not make cheap electric vehicles because its workers are paid less than those in Stuttgart or Detroit. Its workers, moreover, are increasingly absent from the production process. BYD makes competitive electric vehicles because it masters the entire chain, from battery cell to embedded software, in factories where robotization has reached levels European manufacturers have not yet approached.2

The answer that cannot be found

Faced with this reality, advanced industrial democracies have responded with the instruments at hand: tariffs. The European Union has imposed duties of up to 45% on Chinese electric vehicles. The United States has done the same, with even higher rates on certain categories. The logic is understandable: protect strategic industries while rebuilding them, buy time to invest.

The problem is that tariffs do not close a productivity gap. They suspend it temporarily. Chinese manufacturers have responded by announcing factories in Europe, in Hungary, Spain, and Turkey, that will produce locally, bypassing trade barriers. The game of go was several moves ahead.

The reindustrialization that European governments have been calling for in recent years runs into a fundamental contradiction: reindustrializing without massive automation would mean rebuilding structurally uncompetitive industries. But massive automation means creating industrial jobs in far smaller numbers than those destroyed during deindustrialization, which poses a political question that few leaders are prepared to state openly.3

What the machine cannot do

It would be too simple to conclude from all this that the global industrial economy is converging toward a state where machines have replaced humans in production and where labor has ceased to be an economically relevant resource. The history of technological revolutions teaches that each wave of automation has created new jobs by eliminating old ones, that the weavers of the nineteenth century were right to fear for their trade but that their great-grandchildren found employment in industries no one had imagined.

The question is not whether this mechanism will repeat itself. It probably will. The question is on what timescale, with what intensity of social dislocation in the interval, and above all who will capture the value created by machines during the transition period. A dark factory generates considerable profits. Those profits go to those who own the machines, the patents, the control algorithms. They do not, by construction, go to those whose labor has been replaced.

This is where the technical question becomes political again. The dark factory is not an engineering problem. It is a distribution problem. And the societies that do not ask this question now, while the transition is still underway, will find it far harder to answer once it is complete.
