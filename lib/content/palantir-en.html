In September 2000, Elon Musk boarded a plane for Australia. A honeymoon deferred by nine months — the wedding had taken place in January, but work had not allowed him to leave before. In Johannesburg, during a stopover, he contracted malaria. While he was hospitalized upon his return to California, his PayPal associates organized an emergency board meeting behind his back. When he emerged from the hospital, he was no longer CEO. His temporary replacement was named Peter Thiel.

Twenty-five years later, Thiel and Musk are co-administering the American state together. One leads DOGE, the unofficial department tasked with dismantling the federal bureaucracy. The other orbits directly near power while his former collaborators occupy positions at the Pentagon, the Department of Homeland Security, the Department of Health. And Palantir, the company Thiel founded in 2003 with CIA money, processes the intelligence files of French domestic intelligence suspects, targets adversaries on war theaters, and surveils migrants at European borders.

This story does not begin in 2003. It begins in a Johannesburg hospital, during a boardroom coup.

The Mafia

The "PayPal Mafia" is the expression coined by the American press to designate the twenty or so PayPal alumni who, following the company's sale to eBay in 2002 for $1.5 billion, departed with their checks and their address books to found a parallel empire. LinkedIn, YouTube, SpaceX, Palantir, Anduril: companies all born from the same network, the same seed capital, the same Palo Alto dinners.

Thiel is the godfather. Founders Fund, his investment vehicle, is one of the first shareholders of SpaceX, Palantir, and Anduril. David Sacks, a PayPal alumnus, is today the White House's AI and crypto adviser. JD Vance, Vice President, is his direct political protégé. Musk runs DOGE. "They went from clever guys who dodged government regulation to being the government, in one generation," summarizes Steve Blank, a Stanford professor.

Musk wanted the physical infrastructure of the world. Rockets, cars, connected brains. Thiel wanted something different: to define what constitutes a threat.

What Gotham actually does

Palantir sells two main products. Gotham is aimed at government and military agencies: it connects heterogeneous databases, crosses trajectories, transactions, communications, movements, to surface patterns invisible to human analysis. Foundry transposes the same logic to the private sector. AIP adds an artificial intelligence layer that automates operational recommendations.

Palantir does not collect data. Its clients supply it. But Palantir decides how to cross-reference it, which signals to retain, which thresholds trigger an alert. The algorithmic criteria that make an individual a priority suspect in DGSI files are not written in Paris. They are written by engineers in Denver. The grammar of analysis belongs to the company, not to the state that signed the contract.

Karp himself has never concealed the nature of what Palantir builds. "Palantir is here to scare enemies and, in some cases, to eliminate them," he said in 2025, with a smile. This is not a sales pitch. It is a description of doctrine.

René Girard in Denver

Peter Thiel is a philosopher before he is an entrepreneur. In the mid-1980s, as a student at Stanford, he attended René Girard's lectures. The French anthropologist authored the theory of mimetic desire: human beings do not desire spontaneously — they imitate the desire of others. This dynamic inevitably produces rivalry, violence. To put an end to it, societies designate a scapegoat, a collective victim on whom everyone's violence crystallizes. Social order is founded on this mechanism of designation.

Thiel is, by his own account, deeply influenced by this reading. He dedicated a branch of his foundation to Girardian studies. Whoever controls the mechanism of scapegoat designation controls society.

Gotham automates this mechanism. It traverses millions of data points to statistically designate what resembles a threat. This process of designation belongs, in a democracy, to judges, prosecutors, sworn agents subject to democratic oversight. It has been partially delegated to an algorithm whose parameters are the property of an American private company. The states that have signed contracts with Palantir generally have no visibility into these parameters.

The web

France has been using Palantir at the DGSI since 2016, shortly after the November 13 attacks, in the urgency of a state that needed to process data volumes its own tools could not absorb. The contract was renewed in 2019, 2022, and again in December 2025, through 2028. At each renewal, the same official formula: "while awaiting the deployment of a sovereign French tool." This wait has lasted ten years.

France is Palantir's second-largest European market, behind the United Kingdom. In September 2025, the British Ministry of Defense signed a five-year contract for €855 million. Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Poland, Denmark, Norway, Hungary: the same movement everywhere — governments signing with infrastructure they do not control, based on criteria they do not know.

Switzerland suspended. In 2024, a military audit had confirmed the system's technical capabilities but noted that, operating under American jurisdiction, Palantir could not guarantee that Swiss military data would be inaccessible to Washington. The Swiss army halted cooperation. It is the only European state to have done so.

The CLOUD Act and the key

The CLOUD Act, adopted in the United States in 2018, obliges any American company to provide US authorities with the data it controls, regardless of its geographic location. It is not the location of servers that matters. It is the legal nationality of the company. Palantir is American. Its access to the data of its European clients is potentially seizable by an American warrant, even if the servers are in Europe.

Palantir asserts that each client operates in an isolated and secure environment, and that data is not shared between clients. This is true under normal operating conditions. The question is what happens when Washington issues a warrant. The contestation mechanism exists, but it is slow and presupposes a political will to use it. Under the Trump administration, with Thiel in the direct orbit of power, this is a fragile hypothesis.

"From the moment we saw that they could want to disconnect geospatial intelligence systems in Ukraine, it means that one day, they can do it to us too."
Pierre Gastineau, Les espions du président (Albin Michel, 2025).

What cannot be uninstalled

Dependence on Palantir is not primarily technical. The systems can theoretically be replaced. Slowly, painfully, but it is possible. The real dependence is cognitive.

Ten years of working with Palantir's tools means ten years of agents trained and structured by its categories, ten years of decisions founded on its algorithms, ten years of analytical habits built with its grammar. DGSI analysts think about terrorist threats using the variables Gotham defined. British officers plan using AIP recommendations. When a state uses a system for ten years to define what constitutes a danger, it cannot rid itself of the tool without losing part of its capacity for autonomous judgment.

You are not buying software. You are adopting an epistemology.

Epilogue

Palantir was founded with CIA money, by a man who wrote in black and white in 2009 that he no longer believed freedom and democracy to be compatible, in order to sell liberal democracies the instruments of their security. These democracies have signed, renewed, and extended their contracts because the tool works, because there is no ready alternative, because the threat is real and sovereign solutions are perpetually under development.

The question of who decides who is the enemy has no administrative answer. It does not await a technical solution. It awaits a political decision that no one, until now, has had the courage to make.
