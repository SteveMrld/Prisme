Machines already do mathematics better than any engineer. The question is not how to compete with them. The question is what they will never be able to do in our place.

This is not an abstract question. It sits at the heart of a debate taking place right now in ministries, universities, and the boardrooms of large technology companies. We are told that young people must be trained in artificial intelligence, that mathematics should be reinforced from primary school, that entire academic programmes should be devoted to algorithms. Those making this argument are serious people, driven by genuine convictions. They are not entirely wrong. But they are incomplete in a way that troubles me deeply, because this incompleteness is not innocent.

The real battle is not being fought at the level of algorithms. It is being fought at the level of language. Not language understood as a tool of communication, as a neutral vehicle carrying a fully formed thought from one mind to another, but language as the very condition of thought itself. In quantum physics, a particle exists in a state of superposition: several simultaneous states, until an act of observation forces it to choose one. An unformulated thought exists in the same state. It is vague, plural, unstable, inhabited by contradictory meanings that coexist without resolution. Writing is the act of observation that forces it to take shape, to choose one meaning among all possible meanings, to become real. Whoever delegates that act to a machine is not delegating the writing. They are delegating the thought itself. And whoever no longer knows how to write no longer quite knows how to think, without always knowing it.

More than two thousand years ago, Plato recorded in the Phaedrus Socrates' warning against the widespread diffusion of writing in Greek society. Socrates feared that men, now able to commit everything to paper, would cease to memorise, to dialogue, to build their thinking in the heat of conversation. That they would become learned in appearance, ignorant in substance. He was partly wrong: writing did not impoverish human thought, it transformed and deepened it. But he was also partly right. Every time a civilisation externalises a cognitive faculty into a tool, it gains something and surrenders something else, often without noticing, often without having chosen to.

We are living precisely that moment.

People no longer write; they dictate voice messages. They no longer search for their words; they have them generated. They no longer argue; they share posts whose text they have often not fully read. Language contracts, sentences shorten, subordinate clauses disappear, and with them nuance, condition, reservation, everything that distinguishes a thought from a reaction.

Science confirms this with a precision that should give us pause. Researchers at the Norwegian University of Science and Technology analysed the brain activity of some forty students writing by hand or typing on a keyboard. A vast portion of the brain activated during handwriting, with far richer and more complex functional connectivity in areas associated with attention, visual perception and language. Jean-Luc Velay, a CNRS researcher specialising in the relationship between writing and the brain, goes further: ceasing to write by hand produces multiple changes in human cognitive function. This is not nostalgia. It is a neurological observation.

What the most enthusiastic advocates of artificial intelligence do not say is that the rarest competence in a world where machines calculate infinitely better than we do is not coding. It is critical thinking. The capacity to doubt methodically, to nuance without contradicting oneself, to build a conviction from lived experience and a mind that has met the resistance of reality. All of this flows through language, and language is learned through slow reading, demanding writing, and adversarial debate with interlocutors who push back.

Strengthening mathematics is useful, and no sensible person would argue otherwise. But training entire generations to operate tools whose cognitive foundations they do not understand, without simultaneously teaching them to think for themselves, to write with precision, to read with attention, that is manufacturing better executors for machines that, precisely, have no need of them.

Socrates was asking the right question. Simply two thousand years too early.
