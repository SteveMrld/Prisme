Artificial intelligence entered the corporate world in the most deceptive form imaginable, that of a fixed-price subscription. For a few dozen euros per seat, anyone could query models whose training had cost billions and whose slightest answer drew on scarce processors. The gap between that price and that cost never disappeared. It was filled by capital, by investors betting that tomorrow's dominance would justify today's losses. The apparent free ride was a strategy, and the habits it installed were its real product.

The shift to usage-based billing, begun by OpenAI as by Anthropic, changes everything without a sound. Where the subscription hid the expense, the metering of compute units makes it visible with every request. Companies that had reorganized whole functions around autonomous agents saw their bills multiply overnight, sometimes sevenfold. The reaction was immediate and everywhere alike, made of monthly caps, instructions against waste, retreats toward older and cheaper models. In some houses, the meetings devoted to innovation have become meetings about budgetary restraint.

To reduce this moment to a matter of management would be to miss its meaning. The meter does not signal runaway spending. It signals that the economics of intelligence was never settled, and that the world is only now discovering its floor.

The labs thus find themselves before a paradox they will not easily undo. Their valuations, approaching a trillion, rest on the promise of massive and profitable use. The day they charge the true price, that use contracts, and every customer seeks to consume less. To make the real cost of intelligence visible is to threaten the very demand on which its value rested. The industry sold abundance, and must now live off the scarcity it is bringing to light.

Computation is not a copy

The entire digital imagination rested on a promise, that of a marginal cost tending toward zero. Software written once copies itself endlessly for almost nothing, and it is from this cost-free reproduction that the idea of abundance was born. Artificial intelligence breaks this law. Where software is copied, a model's answer is computed, and all computation burns electricity and matter. For a machine, thinking consumes. The digital age had made us forget physics, which artificial intelligence brings back to the foreground.

This difference becomes vertiginous with agents. An assistant waits to be asked. An agent works alone, without interruption, and multiplies: for a single human, the industry projects ten, a hundred, sometimes a thousand, that never sleep and consume without respite. The most frequently cited projections forecast compute consumption multiplied more than twentyfold before the decade is out, and a lasting shortage of the chips that make it possible. The cost of thinking passes entirely through a single bottleneck, Nvidia's silicon, whose scarcity sets intelligence a limit that finance cannot circumvent. The trajectory the industry takes for granted, that of a cost collapsing as scale grows, may never come.

Organizations have already begun to rebuild themselves around this supposedly limitless intelligence. Tasks have been handed to agents, jobs redrawn, skills redirected. To call today for use to be rationed is to walk back promises made to the markets as much as to employees. The promised productivity meets the wall of cost, and no one yet knows whether the gain will survive the price.

Power changes sides

If intelligence is bounded by energy and silicon, power does not fall to whoever holds the largest model. It falls to whoever thinks at the best cost. This shift is already under way. Carried by cheaper energy and greater efficiency, the Chinese models, DeepSeek foremost, price their compute units below the American labs, and have overtaken them in volume processed. The advantage moves from raw performance toward frugality.

This reversal redraws the map. The companies and countries that built their uses on the subsidized intelligence of a handful of Western labs find themselves dependent on a price they do not set. Those who run open models on their own servers, like Mistral's, find themselves sheltered. For much of the world, and first of all for the South, the question is no longer one of reaching the most powerful intelligence. It is one of not becoming its perpetual tenant. Sovereignty is no longer measured by the size of a model. It is measured by the capacity to think without asking the permission, or settling the bill, of another.

A resource that is counted

What is ending, then, is not a pricing policy. It is a belief, that of an infinite and almost cost-free intelligence, one that would have added itself to the world without drawing anything from it. The future looks more like that of a counted resource. Artificial intelligence may become what water and electricity are, a service that is metered, priced, rationed, fought over among powers, and whose access will draw hierarchies. The data centers that house it already seek energy and water wherever they can be found, and these sitings are political decisions before they are technical ones.

Today's unsustainability can be put in few words. The world reorganized its work, its trades and its expectations around an abundance it was not paying for. The bill is arriving, and with it a question that the fascination with power had pushed aside. How far a machine can think now matters less than how much thought the world will be able to afford, and who will settle its distribution.
