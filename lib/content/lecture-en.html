My mother read in the evenings. After dinner, she disappeared into an armchair and a book, and there was no point trying to talk to her. Her body was there. She was not. As a child, I watched this without understanding where she went. I remember asking once. She looked up, a little surprised at being caught, and said something strange: I am living. Then she went back.

I was seven years old. I did not understand. Now I know she had told me everything.

You do not enter a book to escape. You enter it to live more. To traverse existences you will never have, feel pain you have not experienced, understand people you would never have met. Reading is not a leisure activity like any other. It is a technology of the human, one of the most powerful ever invented, and one of the least well defended.

To read a book is an act of resistance. Not in the romantic sense. In the literal sense: to choose slowness over speed, depth over surface.

The brain that reads is no longer quite the same

For a long time, reading was thought to be essentially a visual and linguistic activity. Deciphering signs, associating them with sounds, constructing meaning. This is true. It is also terribly incomplete.

In 2013, a team of researchers from Emory University led by Gregory Berns published a study in Brain Connectivity that would change the way neuroscientists thought about reading. By scanning the brains of participants during and after reading a novel, they observed that fiction activated not only language areas but also the somatosensory cortex, the zone that processes physical sensations, and the motor cortex.1 Reading "she pressed her bare feet into the cold sand" activated the same neural circuits as if the feet were actually in sand. The metaphor was not a figure of speech. It was an instruction to the brain.

Stanislas Dehaene, research director at the EHESS, speaks of neuronal recycling to describe this phenomenon.2 The visual cortex, designed to recognize shapes in nature, reorganizes itself to recognize letters, words, sentences. It diverts from its primary function to accomplish something unprecedented. In Reading in the Brain, Dehaene shows that this learning reconfigures the brain in depth, including areas that have a priori nothing to do with language. Illiterate people and readers do not have the same brain. Not metaphorically. Anatomically.

What happens in an experienced reader's brain goes even further. In 2013, David Comer Kidd and Emanuele Castano published a study in Science showing that reading literary fiction significantly and measurably improved theory of mind, that fundamental capacity to understand that others have thoughts, desires, and beliefs different from our own.3 Literary fiction trains this capacity because it refuses to deliver its characters ready-made. It holds them in their ambiguity. It forces the reader to fill in the silences, infer motivations, inhabit a consciousness that is not their own without ever having the complete key.

What it does to a child

I often think about children who grow up without books. Not with condescending sadness, but with a precise anxiety.

Children who read regularly develop a richer vocabulary, higher attentional capacity, better working memory, greater cognitive flexibility. These are documented, measurable, reproducible effects. But what is less often said, because it is harder to quantify, is the effect of reading on the way a child constructs themselves as a subject.

Psychologist Nicole Girard, in her work on the development of narrative identity in children, emphasizes that fiction plays a central role in the construction of the self. A child who reads stories learns to narrate themselves as the character of a story. They integrate narrative schema, causality, temporality. They understand that events have causes, that people have histories, that nothing happens without reason even if the reason is obscure.

Maria Eugenia Panero and her colleagues at New York University measured that fiction readers score significantly higher on tests of cognitive empathy, notably the Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test, which consists of guessing a person's emotional state from the eye region alone.4 Fiction readers are better at this. Not because they are naturally more sensitive. But because they have trained themselves, without knowing it, to decode foreign interiorities. What this means for a child, concretely: they will be less inclined to reduce the other to a category. More capable of holding contradictory information about the same person simultaneously. More comfortable with the complexity of the social world.

People talk a great deal about resilience, emotional intelligence, relational skills. We look for programs, methods, training courses. The answer may be simpler and older. It fits in a book opened before sleep.

What we lose when we stop

Adults speak of reading with a nostalgia that moves and irritates me in equal measure. I used to love reading, before. Before what, before life became a succession of urgencies that make everything seem like it can wait except the urgent? Before screens colonized every interstice of silence?

What we abandon when we stop reading is not a pleasure. It is a practice of the self.

The philosopher Paul Ricœur devoted an important part of his work to what he called narrative identity, the idea that we understand ourselves through the narratives we construct about our own lives.5 The fiction we read enters into this construction work. It provides us with forms, figures, interpretive possibilities we would not have found alone. There are novels that have taught me things about myself that years had failed to name. Not because they were about me. Precisely because they were about something else, and in that gap between the character and me, something was said that could not have been said any other way.

In 2008, Raymond Mar and Keith Oatley showed that heavy fiction readers presented lower levels of social anxiety and a greater capacity to navigate complex situations.6 Not because reading had made them more confident in the banal sense. But because they had, in some sense, already seen everything. They had traversed breakups, bereavements, betrayals in the lives of characters. And this traversal had left resources. A familiarity with human pain and joy that made the world less opaque.

Open-mindedness is not a gift

Open-mindedness is a skill. And like any skill, it rusts if left unused.

The human brain is naturally economical. It categorizes, simplifies, reduces. It seeks confirmation of what it already knows rather than information that would disturb it. This confirmation bias, documented since the 1960s by psychologist Peter Wason, is not a failing: it is an evolutionary strategy.7 What saved us on the savanna prevents us, in a complex world, from thinking with precision.

Reading literary fiction is one of the few antidotes to this tendency. It forces the maintenance of an ambiguity the mind would prefer to close. A good novel does not resolve its characters. Anna Karenina is not a woman who made the wrong choices. Raskolnikov is not a convenient criminal. They are all of that and something else, simultaneously, irreducibly. And the reader who follows them must learn to hold this complexity without betraying it. In a world where algorithms build custom echo chambers, where nuance is systematically outperformed by the slogan, this capacity to hold the gray has become a rare skill. Almost subversive.

Reading as a political act

I was not expecting to write this when I began this article. But I believe this is where it must go.

The political scientist Martha Nussbaum, in Cultivating Humanity, argues that literature is indispensable to democracy.8 Not as a cultural ornament. As a foundation. A society whose citizens have lost the capacity to imagine the life of those different from them is a society running toward authoritarianism, because it can no longer tolerate alterity. Literature is what allows one to vote for policies that protect people whose experience one will never share, because one has learned, in books, to make it real.

In a world saturated with instant information, reading a book is an act of resistance. Not in the romantic sense. In the literal sense. It is choosing slowness over speed. Depth over surface. Ambiguity over certainty. It is refusing to let the complexity of the world be reduced to what fits in fifteen seconds.

My mother read in the evenings. She came back each time a little different, without it really showing. More patient perhaps. Or sadder, on certain evenings. Or simply more there, with a presence that had traversed something.

I do not know whether she thought about any of this while reading. Probably not. She read because books did her good in a way she could not have explained. That may be the best reason. The most honest one.

You enter a book alone. You emerge from it accompanied by all those you met inside. That may be the most accurate definition of what culture does to those who risk it.
