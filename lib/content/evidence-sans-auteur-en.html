It is enough to write that one and one make ten for a certain unease to set in. The mind protests, hunts for the error, then settles when it remembers that in base two the equality holds without the slightest strain, the number two having there no sign of its own and tipping at once into the carry. Nothing has been rigged. Quantity itself has not budged by a hair, it existed before any notation was assigned to it and will outlast every notation we might care to give it. What wavered, for the space of a second, was the quiet assurance with which we run along the row of digits as if their order belonged to nature. A convention we had stopped noticing became visible again, and that visibility was enough to make it strange.

The jolt is worth pausing over, for it brings to light a mechanism that reaches far beyond arithmetic. Counting in base ten never owed anything to the structure of the world. The practice comes from an accident lodged in the body, the ten fingers of an open hand, and it could have gone otherwise without anything collapsing. The scribes of Babylon counted in bundles of sixty, and their choice did not die with them, it slipped into the hours we still cut into sixty minutes, into the circle we divide into three hundred and sixty degrees. The seven-day week, the meridian that runs through Greenwich, the calendar that orders the administrative seasons, so many old decisions grown so familiar that they pass themselves off as laws of reality. Self-evidence, here, owes nothing to the truth of the thing. It is born of the forgetting of its making.

The part we hold from another

A convention one knows how to handle is still not knowledge one possesses, and the whole difficulty of what we call knowing lodges in that gap. Cognitive psychology has given the confusion a name, the illusion of explanatory depth. Asked how a zipper works, a lock, a pension scheme, each believes himself able to account for it, until the moment he has to and his understanding turns out to have stopped at the surface of the object. The experiment, conducted by Leonid Rozenblit and Frank Keil, is anything but anecdotal. It describes the ordinary condition of the mind in a complex society. Almost everything we take for granted has never been verified by the one who holds it. We know it because another said so, who held it from a third, and the chain climbs back toward a source no one thinks to climb. Ordinary knowledge is a matter of delegated trust, a debt we have no means of collecting ourselves and never feel any need to collect.

There is nothing shameful in the delegation. No single life would suffice to reconstruct on its own the chemistry of a medicine, the circulation of a currency, the path of a signal between two telephones. Shared life rests on a dispersed competence, spread across millions of heads, not one of which holds the whole. This competence rarely arrives bare. It is handed down accompanied by a story about its provenance, and that story has often been washed clean of its origins.

A notation come from elsewhere

The numerals Europe calls Arabic were born in India. Positional notation, which grants a sign a value that depends on its place, and zero treated as a number in its own right, able to enter into operations, rank among the most considerable inventions in the history of thought. Brahmagupta sets down their rules in the seventh century. The Arabic-speaking world of learning gathers them, extends them, spreads them, and the name of al-Khwarizmi, carried from manuscript to manuscript, ends by designating in Latin the very procedure of calculation, until it yields the word algorithm. The road of these numerals toward Europe passes through al-Andalus, the Spain then under Muslim rule, whose libraries of Cordoba and later the translation workshops of Toledo carry Arabic learning into Latin. In Christian lands, however, the welcome is anything but eager. Florence forbids their use in its merchant ledgers at the end of the thirteenth century, for fear of the falsifications that a figure too easy to retouch would make possible. Generations of reckoners cling to the abacus and to Roman numerals before giving way. What is taught today as the neutral and universal alphabet of calculation was first a foreign knowledge, suspected, long refused.

The episode is no scholar's curiosity. It throws light on what the reassuring formula, everyone knows, conceals. So-called universal knowledge presents itself as an inheritance without an author, fallen from the heaven of reason, when it is the settlement of a conflict whose losers were erased along with the sources. The universal does not stand at the beginning of history, it occupies its end, and an end that has taken care to make us forget the struggles that produced it. To decenter the story is therefore not to add a few names from elsewhere to an already closed list. It is to show that the list itself, beneath its look of neutrality, keeps the mark of the relations of force that decided what deserved to be known and what could be allowed to vanish.

The invisible ground of power

School is the place where, for each of us, the transmutation of choice into self-evidence is carried out. There we learn to count in base ten without any rival base ever being named, to read a map where north occupies the top and Europe the center without this arrangement being offered as one of the possible projections of a sphere that has neither top nor middle. The strength of the institution lies above all in everything it spares from discussion. A convention taught without a competitor soon ceases to resemble a convention. The most firmly settled knowledge is the one we receive before the age at which we think to ask where it comes from, and its hold feeds on that priority.

The last turn of the story is playing out before our eyes, in the base two with which it had all begun. Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz works out its mechanics at the start of the eighteenth century, and he believes he recognizes its echo in the hexagrams of the Yi King that missionaries bring him from China, convinced he has rediscovered an ancient wisdom in the dress of calculation. This two-sign notation, long a mathematician's elegance, today commands the better part of the world. The money that circulates, the words that are exchanged, the weapons that aim, the decisions that steer whole lives come down in the last resort to strings of zeros and ones that almost no one, among those whose lives depend on them, can read. A convention among other possible ones has become the ground of power, and that ground stays invisible for the very reason that makes it effective, its smooth working.

This is what the unease of the opening let us sense. Trust and knowledge, which we believe to be one, came apart from each other long ago, and civilizations stand upright on that gap more than on the sum of the things truly understood by those who inhabit them. Self-evidence is not the opposite of ignorance, it is its best-settled form, a question buried so deep that we no longer remember having asked it. To render a thing self-evident is to win the right never again to be questioned about it, and no power lasts longer than that one. To know that one and one make ten consoles for nothing and frees from nothing. At most it reminds us that the ground on which we move was laid by hands, at a date, for reasons, and that it could have been laid otherwise.
