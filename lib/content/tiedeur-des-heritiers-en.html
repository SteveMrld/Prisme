Machiavelli, who had served the chancellery of Florence before fortune's wheel cast him out of it, left in The Prince an observation whose dryness still stings. Nothing is harder to carry through, he wrote, nor more uncertain in its success, than to introduce a new order of things. Whoever attempts it finds before him enemies who defend the old order with ardor, and behind him supporters who back him only with coldness. The sentence is brief, and everything is in it. The reformer is born unarmed.

The reason for this weakness holds no mystery. Those who lose by a change know exactly what they are losing. The threatened privilege has a face, an address, a measurable interest, and it defends itself with the energy of something fighting for its own survival. Those who would gain from the new order own nothing yet of what is promised to them. Their advantage is distant, uncertain, shared among so many that no one feels personally charged with defending it. On one side a concentrated passion, on the other a diffuse approval. The contest is decided before it begins.

No reform of our time illustrates this asymmetry with such cruelty as the ecological transition. Here the coldness of the supporters reaches its outer limit. The beneficiaries of a decarbonized world are not merely scattered or hesitant. For the most part, they are not yet here. They are the generations who will inherit the climate we leave them, and those generations do not vote, do not assemble, fund no campaign. The living are asked to give up tangible advantages for the sake of absent ones who will never be able to thank them. The old order commands all the force of present interest. The new order pleads for those who have no voice.

The moment reform becomes dangerous

Tocqueville, studying the fall of the old French regime, extended Machiavelli with an observation just as unwelcome. The most perilous moment for a bad government, he noted, is the one in which it begins to reform itself. As long as the system oppresses without promising anything, it is endured. The instant it opens, the instant it announces change, it awakens expectations and angers it can no longer contain. Reform once begun is more dangerous than standing still.

The transition obeys this law with an almost mechanical regularity. To announce an ecological effort is to summon at once those it will cost, long before it has given anything to those it must protect. A tax on carbon brings into the street the driver who pays it tomorrow morning, never the child who will breathe more easily in thirty years. The cost is dated, local, felt in the flesh of a monthly budget. The benefit floats in an abstract future no one can wave like a placard. Every climate policy thus carries within it the seed of the revolt it triggers, and governments that have learned this retreat at the first rumble.

Such retreats are readily called cowardice. They are above all the effect of an arithmetic of passions. The minister who backs down yields not to a majority but to an intensity. An ardent, well-organized minority almost always defeats a lukewarm, scattered majority. The economist Mancur Olson made a law of collective life out of this. Small groups with dense interests coalesce without effort, while great masses with a shared benefit stay silent, each waiting for the other to bear the cost of mobilizing. The future, by its very nature, is the vastest and most silent of these groups.

The unchanging repertoire of reaction

When the old order can no longer defend its privilege openly, it argues. Albert Hirschman showed that this arguing always draws on the same repertoire, two centuries old, and that it unfolds through three tireless figures. The first promises a perverse effect. The reform will worsen the very ill it claims to cure, green taxation will impoverish the poor, the exit from carbon will destroy the industry it meant to clean. The second invokes futility. None of it serves any purpose, one country's effort weighs nothing while the others keep burning, so better do nothing. The third raises the specter of jeopardy. The change, however desirable, threatens something more precious still, prosperity, sovereignty, jobs, a way of life.

What strikes the reader of these objections is not their force but their permanence. They were turned against the abolition of slavery, against universal suffrage, against social protection. They resurface today against ecology, word for word, as though a common fund of the fear of change passed from one generation of conservatives to the next. To recognize these figures for what they are, rhetorical forms rather than analyses, is not enough to disarm them. But it at least allows one to stop mistaking them for arguments, and to see behind each the concrete interest it dresses up as prudence.

A hierarchy repainted in the colors of the future

It would be too easy to set the camp of the old order against that of the new, as if virtue stood wholly on one side. The transition carries its own injustices, and it is by looking toward the South that one sees them best. The nations that grew rich by burning coal for two centuries now ask those who have not yet burned theirs to abstain. The decarbonized world held up as a beginning looks, seen from Lagos or Dhaka, like the final sealing of a staircase whose rungs are pulled up once the top is reached.

The contradiction lodges in the very matter of the transition. The batteries meant to save the climate demand cobalt, lithium, metals torn from Congolese soil or Andean salt flats under conditions that prolong the old habits of colonial extraction. The new order thus has, in its turn, its concentrated beneficiaries and its diffuse victims. The first sit in the offices where clean technologies are designed, the second in the shafts where children dig to feed them. Machiavelli's asymmetry doubles. It no longer sets only the present against the future, but the North against the South, and the one who decides against the one who pays without having been consulted.

To think seriously about the transition therefore requires holding together two truths that get in each other's way. The exit from carbon is a fragile new order, which a coalition of powerful interests works to smother. And this new order, in its present form, reproduces a domination it claims to leave behind. To deny either truth for the convenience of the other is to condemn oneself to understanding nothing. Ecological justice and justice among peoples do not always march in step, and it is in that gap that the credibility of the world to come is at stake.

Arming what has no voice

Machiavelli did not stop at noting the weakness. He drew from it a brutal consequence. Armed prophets have prevailed, he wrote, and unarmed prophets have come to ruin. Whoever wishes to establish a new order cannot rely on the mere persuasion of those who ought to support it, for their coldness will not hold against the ardor of its enemies. He needs a force that does not depend on their enthusiasm.

Carried over to ecology, the formula loses any warlike sense and names something exact. If the beneficiaries of the transition cannot defend it, because they are future, scattered, or far away, then the transition must rest on something other than their support. On law that binds the living in the name of the absent. On institutions removed from the short cycle of angers, able to hold a course when governments bend. On rules that write into the present the interests of those who hold no seat in it. To arm the new order, here, means to give it defenders who do not depend on the passion of a majority, since that majority, by nature, will remain lukewarm.

Hannah Arendt saw in every beginning the highest and most fragile of political acts, because it brings into the world something that did not exist and that nothing guarantees. To found is to wager on what one will not see. The ecological order pushes that wager to its limit. It is perhaps the first that a society is called to institute entirely on behalf of those not yet born, with no return possible, no gratitude expected, against the immediate evidence of its own interests. The question Machiavelli bequeaths, five centuries on, has lost none of its edge. Can a democracy arm a reform whose beneficiaries do not yet vote, and consent, with open eyes, to remain the lukewarm defender of a world it will never know.
