The perennial Arctic ice, the ice that survived summer and formed the bedrock of the ice sheet, covered more than 3 million square kilometers in 1988. By 2019, NASA data indicated less than 116,000 square kilometers. A reduction of 96% in thirty years.¹ The Arctic is warming three to four times faster than the rest of the planet. What this melt produces extends far beyond climate equilibria: it opens routes, unlocks resources, and transforms one of the most remote regions on Earth into a theater of strategic rivalry between great powers.

The Arctic encompasses the Arctic Ocean and the northern regions of Russia, Greenland, Canada, Alaska, Norway, and Iceland. For centuries, ice made it a natural boundary, impassable for most of the year. Accelerated melting has changed this reality. The Northeast Passage, which runs along the Siberian coast, and the Northwest Passage, which runs along Greenland and Canada, are progressively navigable over increasingly long windows. What warming destroys on one side, it opens on the other. And several powers have decided to take advantage of this.

The Northern Sea Route, between myth and reality

The Northern Sea Route (NSR), which runs along the Siberian coast from the Barents Sea to the Bering Strait, represents a shortcut of roughly fifteen days compared to the Suez Canal route between Europe and Asia. In 2023, total traffic on this route reached 35 to 36 million tons, a historical record.² Transit traffic, vessels using the route from end to end, also broke records: 97 transits in 2024, 103 in 2025, totaling approximately 3.2 million tons.³

The record melting of the ice sheet opens unprecedented commercial routes and revives military rivalries thought buried with the Cold War.

These figures require perspective. The Suez Canal handles 1.6 billion tons per year. Arctic transit traffic represents less than 0.2% of Suez's volume. The Northern Sea Route remains a marginal route on the scale of world trade, seasonal, constrained by ice, by the need to use Russian icebreakers, and by the absence of sufficient port infrastructure along its shores.⁴ It is not an alternative to Suez. It is a niche corridor, strategically important, whose growth potential depends as much on the pace of deglaciation as on infrastructure investment.

Vladimir Putin has made it a personal project. His stated objective is to raise total traffic volume on the NSR to 200 million tons by 2030-2035, compared with 35 million today. The ambition is considerable. Russia is investing massively: a fleet of nuclear icebreakers numbering eight vessels in service, with a target of seventeen ships by 2030, operated by Rosatom, the world's sole operator of such a fleet.⁵

95% toward China, a deepening dependence

The most revealing fact about the true nature of the Northern Sea Route is this: 95% of the volume of goods in transit goes from Russia toward China.⁶ The route is not an open commercial waterway: it is a Russo-Chinese corridor. Russian crude oil avoiding Western sanctions, iron ore, LNG from the Yamal project: these are the flows the NSR primarily serves.

Sino-Russian cooperation in the Arctic began in the 2000s, accelerated after the first Ukrainian crisis in 2014, and took on a new dimension after 2022. In June 2024, at the Saint Petersburg International Economic Forum, a major infrastructure investment agreement was signed between the two countries. This agreement goes beyond the financing of ports and terminals: China commits to becoming the primary provider of transit control technologies, including low-orbit satellite constellations for Arctic telecommunications.⁷ Establishing itself in navigation control software, in telecommunications standards, in the financing of Russian infrastructure, means indirectly holding control of a route Moscow presents as sovereign.

In September 2025, Chinese company Xinxin Shipping launched the first regular containerized line between Asia and Europe via the Arctic, linking Qingdao, Shanghai, and Ningbo-Zhoushan with Felixstowe, Rotterdam, Hamburg, and Gdansk in eighteen days, less than half the time via Suez.⁸ The line operates from summer through October, for as long as ice permits. This is not yet a commercial revolution. It is a statement of intent.

China, self-proclaimed Arctic power

China has no Arctic border. Yet since 2018 it has defined itself as a "near-Arctic state" in its White Paper on Polar Policy. This formulation, without basis in international law, says everything about Beijing's strategy: creating a presence legitimacy through repetition and institutional anchoring.

The instruments are multiple. The Svalbard Treaty, signed in 1920, authorizes signatory countries to conduct economic and scientific activities in the Norwegian archipelago at the far north. China acceded in 1925, at France's initiative, which did not anticipate the use Beijing would make of it a century later. It has built the Yellow River research station there, which serves as its Arctic foothold. In 2013, China became a permanent observer member of the Arctic Council, the intergovernmental body that brings together the eight Arctic states. It is present at every meeting, documenting, influencing.⁹

The Chinese Arctic and Antarctic Administration runs regular scientific expeditions to both polar zones. China has invested in several research icebreakers, including the Xuelong 2, commissioned in 2019. It is negotiating access agreements to Russian and Norwegian Arctic ports. Militarily, its interests are also growing: an open Arctic route would give the Chinese navy additional access to the Atlantic, bypassing the traditional chokepoints of the Strait of Malacca and the Indian Ocean, where American naval superiority remains very strong.

Resources, the other stake

Beneath the Arctic ice lie considerable resources. A 2008 US Geological Survey study estimated that the Arctic could hold more than 22% of global undiscovered hydrocarbon reserves, including oil, natural gas, as well as deposits of nickel, zinc, gold, and uranium.¹⁰ The melt is accelerating access to these resources, which in turn attract extraction investment, creating a feedback loop whose climate effects compound those already underway.

Territorial tensions over the delimitation of the Arctic continental shelf have not disappeared. Russia and Canada have sparred for years over the Lomonosov Ridge, each claiming sovereignty over the corresponding seabed. These disputes are brought before the United Nations Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf, but the Arctic coastal states have never relinquished the option of resolving them through pressure rather than law.

Russia also adopted a 2022 law requiring foreign state vessels to notify their passage on the NSR three months in advance, and commercial vessels to use Russian icebreakers. These obligations contravene the principle of freedom of navigation enshrined in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea. They illustrate the Russian tendency to treat this route as a sovereign space rather than an open international waterway.¹¹

What the melt else liberates

The consequences of Arctic deglaciation extend beyond shipping routes and hydrocarbons. The thawing of permafrost, which covers approximately 25% of the northern hemisphere's land surface, releases methane and carbon dioxide stored for millennia, greenhouse gases that in turn accelerate warming. Researchers have documented the presence in permafrost of mercury in quantities potentially catastrophic for ecosystems if released at scale, as well as ancient viruses and bacteria whose effects on populations with no adapted immunity remain poorly understood.¹²

Indigenous Arctic communities, Inuit, Yupik, Nenets, Sámi, are experiencing these transformations directly and irreversibly: disappearance of traditional hunting and fishing conditions, collapse of infrastructure built on permafrost, forced relocations. Their situation constitutes the blind spot of most geostrategic analyses of the Arctic, which treat these territories as blank spaces to be divided rather than as environments inhabited for millennia.

The Arctic concentrates in a single space the deepest contradictions of the era: the climate catastrophe creates the conditions for an economic and military competition that accelerates that same catastrophe. The states that preach the fight against global warming in New York or Geneva are the same ones sending icebreakers and geologists to map the oil reserves made accessible by the melt. This contradiction is neither new nor incidental. It is structural. And the Arctic is its starkest mirror.
