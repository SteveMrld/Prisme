We may be the last generation to know what it means to think.

This is not an apocalyptic statement. It is an observation. We have known a world in which searching took time, in which putting an idea into words required that we pass through it ourselves, in which being wrong was a mandatory stage before understanding. That world has not disappeared. But it is becoming optional.

And that is where everything is decided.

We have always offloaded whatever our brains no longer needed to carry. The calculator took arithmetic. The GPS took our sense of direction. Each time, the handover was clean, circumscribed: we entrusted a task to a machine and got back time to think about something else. That is how technology has always worked.

But artificial intelligence does not take a task. It takes the faculty that allowed us to carry out all of them.

It does not take arithmetic. It takes reflection. It does not take our sense of direction. It takes judgment. For the first time, a tool reaches not what the brain does but the way it does it. This is not a prosthesis. It is a replacement.

In June 2025, the MIT Media Lab published a study titled Your Brain on ChatGPT. Fifty-four participants, divided into three groups: some wrote with ChatGPT, others with a search engine, the last on their own, with no tool at all. Their brains were monitored by electroencephalography across thirty-two regions. The results left no room for doubt. The ChatGPT group showed the weakest neural connectivity. Its texts were correct, well structured, grammatically clean. The teachers asked to assess them summed them up in a single word: soulless. As the weeks passed, the participants in this group had gradually let go of the work. They no longer thought. They copied and pasted. The lead researcher, Nataliya Kos'myna, named this phenomenon cognitive debt. We borrow intelligence from the machine. And we repay it in the capacity to think.

This is not a laboratory anecdote. It may be the most important signal of recent years. And one of the least discussed.

The generations to come will be born into a world where the tool already exists. The way one is born into a world where electricity already exists. With no memory of before. Without even the possibility of imagining that it could have been otherwise. They will not know what it was to build a thought alone, to lose it, to find it again, to be wrong and to learn from that mistake because it was their own. This inheritance is something we either hand down to them or take from them. The border between the two lies exactly where we now stand.

Writing transformed our relationship to memory. The printing press transformed our relationship to knowledge. At each threshold, a generation crossed over knowing what it was leaving behind. The one that followed inherited an already reconfigured world, with no way of imagining that it could have been otherwise. We are that generation. Except that this time, what is being reconfigured is not a relationship to the outside world. It is a relationship to oneself.

What we decide now, how we use this tool, which spaces we keep for unassisted thought, what we choose not to delegate, all of it will become, for those who come after, not a choice but a condition. They will live inside what we will have built, without knowing that it could have been built otherwise. Without even asking the question.

The question is not whether artificial intelligence is a good thing or a bad one. It is here, it will stay, it will grow. The question is whether we are building a tool that augments the human, or a human who lets himself be gradually replaced, so comfortably that he never notices.

There are responsibilities one does not choose. This one was given to us by the moment we were born into. We have seen the before. We are living through the during. The after is what we decide to make of it.
