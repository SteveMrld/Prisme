In 2008, Jeffrey Epstein pleaded guilty to two counts of soliciting prostitution in Florida. He was sentenced to eighteen months in prison, most of which he spent working in his offices six days a week, with daily release on work furlough. During that time, a secret deal had been negotiated between his lawyers and the federal prosecutor Alexander Acosta to close, once and for all, any federal investigation, immunizing along the way his accomplices, named and unnamed. The victims were never told. They learned of it years later, from the press.

This is not an anomaly of the American system. It is the American system working exactly as it was designed to work when the right names appear in an address book.

Jeffrey Epstein was not a lone predator acting in the shadows. He operated in plain sight, in his properties in Palm Beach and Manhattan, on his private island in the Virgin Islands, in his Paris mansion. He moved his victims aboard a registered Boeing 727 whose flight logs are public. All of this was known. Part of it was documented. Nothing moved.

The FBI had opened a file. Federal prosecutors had testimony. Journalists had begun investigating as early as 2005. The question was never why no one knew. The question, the only one worth asking, is why those who knew chose not to act.

What the Palm Beach County grand jury documents, declassified in 2024 by order of Judge Luis Delgado, reveal is telling in this regard. At the 2006 hearing, the two young victims who testified were questioned by the prosecutor about the possibility that they had engaged in prostitution and might face charges themselves. Fourteen-year-old girls treated as suspects in a case in which they were the victims. Spencer Kuvin, the lawyer who represented one of them, described these examinations as "harrowing". The grand jury, steered in this way, indicted Epstein on a single count of solicitation of prostitution. A petty offense.

Alexander Acosta, the man who signed the immunity deal declared illegal in 2019 by federal judge Kenneth Marra on the ground that it violated the law on victims' rights, would become in 2017 Secretary of Labor of the United States under the Trump administration. He would resign in 2019, a few days after the publication of the Miami Herald investigation. Not because of the deal. Because of the investigation.

It was Julie K. Brown, a journalist at the Miami Herald, who did what the institutions would not do. She spent years tracking down the victims, reconstructing the timelines, putting names to files that the justice system had deliberately buried. Her investigation, published in November 2018, forced the federal case to be reopened. She gave a voice back to dozens of women whom the system had made invisible twice: a first time by leaving them without protection, a second time by shutting them out of a plea agreement that sealed their silence without their consent.

What makes the Epstein affair structurally different from a mere predation scandal is the geography of his respectability. Epstein was not a man who hid. He showed himself. He funded. He bought himself institutional legitimacy by a method one might almost call industrial. Harvard received 8.9 million dollars from him between 1998 and 2007, according to the report published by President Lawrence Bacow in September 2019. MIT received more than 700,000 dollars and hosted him on its campus at least nine times after his 2008 conviction, its leaders having demanded that the gifts remain anonymous to protect the institution's reputation, according to the Goodwin Procter report of January 2020. JPMorgan, which had internal suspicions about cash withdrawals suggestive of sex trafficking, kept Epstein as a client for years because he was too valuable, according to the documents produced in the civil suit brought by the State of Virginia against the bank in 2023.

This is what a system is. It is not a conspiracy. It is a succession of individual decisions, made by people who knew or should have known, each of whom weighed his interests and chose to look away. Bankers, academics, prosecutors, members of the royal family, politicians. Each in his own sphere, each with his own rationalization. Together they built an impunity that no one had formally decided to build, but that each had helped to keep in place.

Ghislaine Maxwell, accomplice and organizer of the network that recruited the victims, was sentenced in December 2021 to twenty years in prison for the sex trafficking of minors by a federal court in New York. Prince Andrew, accused by the victim Virginia Giuffre of acts committed while she was a minor, reached an out-of-court settlement in February 2022 for an undisclosed sum, without admitting the facts. No criminal proceedings were brought against him. He was stripped of his official royal duties.

Jeffrey Epstein died on 10 August 2019 in his cell at the Metropolitan Correctional Center in New York. The surveillance cameras were out of order. The guards were asleep. The anti-suicide mattress had been removed from his cell a few days earlier. The New York City medical examiner ruled it a suicide by hanging. The independent medical examiner retained by the family, Michael Baden, ruled it a homicide. The question remains open and no further criminal investigation has been opened.

What this affair reveals is not the portrait of a monster. Monsters are convenient. They make it possible to confine the scandal to one individual, condemn him, and turn the page. What Epstein reveals is the anatomy of an impunity built over thirty years, brick by brick, favor by favor, silence by silence. He reveals what happens when wealth, connections and institutional cowardice combine within a political system that presents itself to the world as the model of liberal democracy.

This is not an American story. It is a story about power.
