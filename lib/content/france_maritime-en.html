Maritime France,
an ignored empire

The world's second-largest exclusive economic zone, presence in three oceans and on four continents. France holds a geostrategic advantage without equal in the West, and does not know what to do with it.

SM
Steve Moradel
Geopolitics & Strategy

No other Western power can say the same. France is simultaneously present in the North Atlantic, South Atlantic, Indian Ocean, North Pacific, and South Pacific. Its overseas departments and territories, French Polynesia, New Caledonia, Guadeloupe, Martinique, Guiana, Réunion, Mayotte, Saint-Pierre-et-Miquelon, Wallis-and-Futuna, confer upon it an Exclusive Economic Zone of 11.7 million square kilometers, the second largest in the world after the United States.¹ This EEZ represents 97% of the maritime space under French jurisdiction, a first-order geopolitical fact that French foreign policy has never fully integrated.

The comparison with China is revealing. Beijing devotes considerable resources to extending its maritime influence in the South China Sea, building artificial islands and claiming contested economic zones. France, by contrast, legally and sovereignly holds an immense maritime space in the Indo-Pacific — Papeete controls a zone as vast as continental Europe — and struggles to formulate a coherent doctrine around it.

The overseas territories as leverage, inverting the reading

The overseas departments and territories are generally perceived, in mainland French public debate, as liability territories: economically fragile, chronically deficit-ridden, net recipients of public transfers. This reading is not wrong on the accounting level in the short term. It is strategically blind.

France holds the world's second maritime space and does little with it. It is an oceanic power that does not think of itself as one.

Cyrille Martin, Institut français de la mer

Papeete and Nouméa are key positions in the Indo-Pacific, at the heart of the competition between the United States and China for regional influence. French Polynesia, a maritime territory as vast as Europe, is a geographical lock between the Pacific and the Americas that China cannot bypass without dealing with Paris. Cayenne places France in the Caribbean basin, at the confluence of North and South American interests. Mayotte and Réunion, located in the Indian Ocean, are natural platforms for a renewed partnership with African coastal countries, Mozambique, Madagascar, Comoros, Tanzania.²

These positions are not dormant assets. They are arguments in diplomatic, economic, and security negotiations that France conducts, or should conduct, with China, the United States, the African Union, and Pacific regional organizations. The question is not whether these territories "cost" the mainland. It is what their loss or deterioration would cost in global influence.

The French EEZ, an under-exploited resource

The exclusive economic zone gives the sovereign state exclusive rights over the exploration and exploitation of natural resources, fishery, mineral, energy, within the 200 nautical miles around its coasts. At this scale, the French EEZ is a considerable resource: seabeds potentially rich in polymetallic nodules and geothermal resources, fishery resources among the most important in the world, potential in marine renewable energies, wave power, tidal energy, floating offshore wind, ocean thermal energy, still largely unexploited.³

France is also, alongside the United Kingdom, one of the best-positioned countries in Europe to develop a marine energy industrial sector. Projects exist — floating wind at Fos-sur-Mer, tidal energy in Bordeaux, wave energy at Réunion — but they remain at the experimental stage for lack of a national industrial strategy and streamlined administrative authorizations that would allow them to scale up.⁴

This inertia has a direct opportunity cost. International competition for marine energies is structuring rapidly: the United Kingdom, Denmark, China, and South Korea are investing massively in these sectors. The window for building a competitive industrial position is open, but it will not remain so indefinitely.

French ports, an accumulating lag

The Port of Le Havre is France's first port by container volume. It is also, comparatively speaking, a modest-sized port on the global scale: in 2023, it processed approximately 2.9 million TEU containers against 47.3 million for Shanghai, 33 million for Singapore, and 17.8 million for Rotterdam.⁵ The gap is not only quantitative; it reflects investment, logistics, industrial integration, and governance choices going back several decades.

The comparison with the Netherlands is particularly instructive. Rotterdam, the port of a country of 18 million inhabitants, processes six times more containers than Le Havre and has established itself as the main maritime gateway to continental Europe, a position that geography naturally placed within France's reach. This is not geographic fatality: it is the result of coherent investments over several decades in infrastructure, logistics, and port activity zones.

Fishing, an unresolved European contradiction

The European Union's Common Fisheries Policy imposes rigorous environmental constraints on European fishers, quotas, biological rest periods, restrictions on fishing techniques, that do not apply to imports. Norway, Europe's leading fish supplier in 2023, and China, the second, practice fishing methods that would not meet European standards if applied in Community waters.⁶

This regulatory asymmetry penalizes European fishers twice: it reduces their catch capacity while exposing them to competition subsidized by less stringent standards. The solution is not to weaken European rules, which respond to a genuine ecological necessity, with stocks of several species in critical condition in European waters. It is to apply to imports an environmental border adjustment mechanism, on the model of the carbon border adjustment mechanism adopted by the EU in 2023 for high-emission industries.

The overseas territories and development, changing the paradigm

Overseas territories accumulate socio-economic indicators structurally degraded compared to the mainland: unemployment rates two to three times higher, significantly lower GDP per capita, dependence on imports that generates persistent high cost of living.⁷ These realities call for a differentiated policy, not because these territories deserve less, but because the tools designed for the mainland do not produce the same effects there.

The central issue is economic autonomy. The French Antilles, Réunion, and Polynesia import a considerable share of their food and energy, two areas in which their geographic and climatic situation would actually give them a comparative advantage if coherent policies of food sovereignty and renewable energy were pursued. Import dependence is not geographic fatality: it is largely the result of decades of territorial continuity policy that has favored alignment with mainland consumption patterns rather than the development of local industries.

The case of Mayotte deserves particular mention. The first French department in terms of demographic growth and the last in terms of GDP per capita, Mayotte concentrates social and migration tensions that have no purely security-based solution. Its stabilization depends on economic development anchored in its regional environment, the western Indian Ocean, the Comoros archipelago, the Mozambican coast, rather than in forced integration into the mainland model.⁸

What France could do, and does not

France is the only power to have a legal and sovereign presence in all the major ocean basins. Neither the United States, nor China, nor Germany, nor any other power has this configuration. This is a structural advantage in a world where maritime routes, seabed resources, and strategic positions in the oceans have once again become first-order issues, as the tension around the Taiwan Strait, the Houthi attacks in the Red Sea, or the Sino-American competition in the Pacific recalls.

Exploiting this advantage requires three things. First, a coherent maritime doctrine that articulates sovereignty, economic development of the territories, and diplomatic influence, a doctrine that does not currently exist in integrated form. Second, industrial investments in marine energies, ports, and sustainable fishing, commensurate with the stakes and with a long-term horizon. Finally, a renewed conception of the relationship between the mainland and its territories, no longer founded on territorial continuity as mimicry, but on equity as assumed differentiation and development as progressive autonomy.

The sea represents 90% of the planet's surface. The ongoing geopolitical shifts, Sino-American rivalry, energy transition, pressure on natural resources, make it a first-order strategic space for the decades ahead. France is present everywhere in it. The question is whether it chooses to take advantage of this.
