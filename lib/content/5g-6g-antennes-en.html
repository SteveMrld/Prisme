Modern life contains objects that structure existence without ever being looked at. Mobile telephone antennas belong to this category. Hidden on rooftops, flattened against the sides of church towers, slipped into the false chimneys of apartment buildings, they organise the circulation of information with a density few inhabitants measure. France today counts more than fifty thousand sites dedicated to 5G alone, to which are added those that continue to broadcast 4G, and in declining proportions 3G and 2G. This infrastructure, as invisible as it is decisive, carries almost all of contemporary communications on its own, whether professional or intimate, commercial or political.

Yet the question of what exactly an antenna does, what it emits, and what it implies for health and collective life remains one of the most poorly framed in public debate. Between the promotional narrative of operators, the sometimes ill-documented anxieties of certain activist groups, and the cautious silence of a science that acknowledges its uncertainties more readily than the controversy allows, a calm account must be attempted. What follows does not claim to settle the matter. The task is to establish what we know, what we do not know, and why.

A mobile telephone antenna is not an autonomous source of energy. It is the terminal node of a network that starts from a computer core, travels across kilometres of optical fibre, and reaches a radio installation placed at height for simple reasons of propagation. Its function is twofold. It receives the signal emitted by telephones, tablets and connected objects within a given radius, and it sends back to them the one intended for them. Each antenna covers a zone called a cell, which can range from a few hundred metres in urban centres to several kilometres in rural areas, depending on the frequency band used. This cellular division is what allows millions of users to communicate simultaneously without disturbing one another, each occupying a precise fraction of the spectrum, under the supervision of software that orchestrates the allocations in real time.

The frequencies used correspond to a physical compromise. Low frequencies, around 700 megahertz, pass through walls well and travel far, but carry little data. High frequencies, in the 3.5 gigahertz band, carry much more data but encounter obstacles quickly and require the network to be densified. The so-called millimetre frequencies, around 26 gigahertz, which France has not yet deployed commercially, would push this logic to its limit: considerable throughput, range reduced to a few dozen metres, and the necessity of numerous closely spaced antennas.

The technical specificity of 5G does not lie only in its throughput. It lies in the very mode of signal emission. Antennas of previous generations radiated their energy broadly, in the manner of a lighthouse continuously sweeping a sector. The smart Massive MIMO antennas, deployed with the arrival of 5G, are composed of multiple emitters integrated together in a single piece of equipment, which, driven by software, make it possible to focus the radio wave beam onto a given zone. This technique, called beamforming, concentrates energy where it is needed and cancels it elsewhere. The antenna ceases to be a diffuser and becomes a directed, mobile, adaptive projector.¹

This change has consequences that are often poorly presented in both directions. From the operators' side, it allows them to assert, with reason, that average exposure does not increase in proportion to throughput, since energy is better targeted and therefore less uselessly dissipated. From the critics' side, it nourishes the intuition that individual exposure, inside the beam, may be locally higher than what ambient measurements reveal. Both observations are true. They point to the same reality: the measurement tools inherited from earlier networks are no longer entirely suited to an infrastructure that emits dynamically and directionally.

French figures are now relatively stable in order of magnitude. According to the monthly observatories published by the National Frequencies Agency, France has exceeded the threshold of fifty thousand authorised 5G sites, a large majority of which are technically operational.² These sites are distributed across three main frequency bands, 700 MHz, 2.1 GHz and 3.5 GHz, each bearing a different compromise between range and throughput. Four principal operators, Orange, Free, Bouygues Telecom and SFR, compete for the density of this coverage, with different strategies. Free favours the number of antennas, Orange the quality of the 3.5 GHz band, Bouygues and SFR seek a balance, under pressure from their regulatory obligations. 2G and 3G, long emblematic of the mobile landscape, have in turn begun their slow extinction.

This figure may say little taken in isolation. Placed back into its history, it describes a silent transformation. In a few years, France has built the essentials of a new digital infrastructure, without a public debate on the scale of the one that accompanied the installation of the first antennas in the 1990s. The operation took place in successive layers, through agreements between operators and the sharing of existing sites, without citizens most often having the opportunity to weigh in on the technical or territorial choices.

6G, whose media promises far precede its industrial reality, does not yet exist as a deployable technology. It exists as an international standardisation programme. Under the aegis of the International Telecommunication Union, the framework known as IMT-2030 defines the vision of what it must make possible. The 3GPP, a body bringing together the major equipment manufacturers and operators worldwide, is working on the first properly 6G specifications, which are to feed, according to a timeline set by the ITU, a commercial rollout around the end of the decade.³

The technologies that will carry 6G are not all settled. They will probably include finer use of millimetre and sub-terahertz frequencies, the integration of radar detection functions into the communications infrastructure, and native coordination with low-orbit satellite constellations. The use cases cited, from remote surgery to autonomous vehicles, often overlap with promises already made for 5G, which have only partially come to pass. It is reasonable to listen to these announcements with the same mix of attention and scepticism owed to any industrial communication driven by considerable economic interests.

It is around health that the uncertainties concentrate most honestly, and where simplifications abound most massively. A few markers are indispensable in order to understand what science has established and what it continues to examine.

In 2011, a working group convened by the International Agency for Research on Cancer classified radiofrequency electromagnetic fields in group 2B, possibly carcinogenic to humans, with indications of an increased risk of glioma and acoustic neuroma.⁴ An agent classified 2B by the IARC designates one for which an association with cancer has been detected and can be interpreted causally, without chance, biases and confounding factors being ruled out with sufficient scientific certainty. This classification means neither that exposure to radiofrequencies causes cancer, nor that it is harmless. It means that doubt is not closed.

Since 2011, two large experimental studies, one by the United States National Toxicology Program and one by the Ramazzini Institute in Italy, have observed an excess of certain tumours in rats after prolonged exposure to radiofrequencies.⁵ Several epidemiological studies, going in the opposite direction, have not identified a clear signal at the scale of human populations. Recent meta-analyses conducted by the teams of Martin Röösli at the Swiss Tropical and Public Health Institute in Basel, and Maria Feychting at the Karolinska Institute in Stockholm, two of the most recognised centres of radiofrequency epidemiology, confirm this absence of a clear population-level signal, while acknowledging the methodological limits of a field where past exposure is difficult to reconstruct.⁶ The compatibility between these animal results and these human data remains one of the most debated questions in the literature. For this reason, an IARC advisory group has recently recommended the inclusion of radiofrequency fields among the priorities of the current Monographs programme, with a view to re-evaluation under the Agency's revised procedure. A formal re-evaluation is therefore under way, without its conclusions being presumable today.

In France, the National Agency for Food, Environmental and Occupational Health Safety, in its updated opinion, concluded that it was unlikely that the deployment of 5G would be accompanied by new health risks compared with 3G or 4G.⁷ This conclusion deserves to be read rigorously. It does not say that radiofrequencies are harmless. It says that the addition of 5G, within frequencies already in use or in the 3.5 GHz band, does not introduce qualitatively new risks that existing studies would have failed to identify. The Agency itself stressed that data on the 26 GHz band, which is not yet deployed in France, remained too few to allow a robust assessment.

The regulatory limits on public exposure, drawn from the recommendations of the International Commission on Non-Ionizing Radiation Protection, were revised in 2020. They rest on the principle that the established health effect of radiofrequencies is a thermal one, observable at levels far higher than those of everyday exposure. The measurements carried out by the National Frequencies Agency over the last observed decade give a concrete picture of this exposure. The average global exposure level in France has risen from around 0.68 V/m to around 1.2 V/m, with an average recorded in urban areas almost three times higher than in the countryside; the measured levels all remain well below the limit values set by law, which range between 28 V/m and 87 V/m depending on frequency.⁸ This rise is measurable over time. It remains, at this stage, well below the thresholds retained by health authorities.

Several methodological controversies nevertheless run through this apparent consensus. The question of so-called non-thermal effects, the hypothesis that biological phenomena might appear without a measurable rise in tissue temperature, remains open in the literature. The question of cumulative exposures, and their influence on children whose nervous system continues to form, is the subject of extensive but contradictory studies. The question, finally, of the independence of certain international protection bodies, foremost among them the ICNIRP, is the subject of a real debate in the literature, between those who document structural links between its members and industrial actors in the sector, and those who stress the inevitable narrowness of a restricted field of expertise where the same specialists are found on either side of the bodies being consulted.

An evidence must nevertheless be stated before closing. Connectivity is no longer today one technological choice among others, a consumer product to be adopted or rejected individually. It has become, over recent decades, the very substrate on which contemporary societies are organised. Cities, whether they present themselves as new or rearrange older fabrics, rest on data flows that regulate their transport, their energy, their public services, their security. Armed conflicts are now played out to a large extent through the mastery of sensors, satellite communications, drones and digital command systems. Medicine, from remote care to implantable devices and synchronised imaging, relies on permanent data exchanges between patients, practitioners and machines. The exploration and exploitation of space, which will determine an increasing share of global strategic balances, rest on satellite constellations that are themselves communication nodes. Artificial intelligence, which has become in a few years the principal lever of industrial and strategic investment at a global scale, functions only through the permanent circulation of data between computing centres, users and connected objects; its most massive models as well as its most everyday uses depend entirely on the bandwidth, latency and resilience of the networks that carry them. 6G is moreover designed, in its very specifications, to accommodate this load natively, by integrating learning mechanisms into its radio architecture; the infrastructure and the machine it powers now develop together, in a symbiosis that neither could now undo. Down to the most everyday life, finally, from payment to learning, which now passes through invisible infrastructures whose failure would immediately be felt as a collapse.

This irreversibility changes the nature of the question posed. It is no longer a matter of deliberating whether we want a connected world, as one deliberates the widening of a motorway or the closing of a power station. That world is already here, and societies that attempted to extract themselves from it unilaterally would not retreat to an earlier age, they would simply marginalise themselves within the present. The honest question therefore becomes a different one. It consists in asking how to inhabit an infrastructure on which we depend without always having chosen it, how to correct its excesses without denying its necessity, how to inscribe into its governance the demands that economy and speed tend to efface.

Public debate around 5G has often locked itself into a sterile opposition between progress and precaution. It would gain from recognising that these two attitudes are not alternatives. The digital infrastructure that today connects territories renders real services, to professions that depend on it, to areas long poorly served, to an economy whose competitiveness now relies on the speed of exchange. The health uncertainties that remain, minority though they are in the literature, are no less real and call for proportionate vigilance, not blind consent.

This vigilance passes through three simple requirements. That the measurement tools adapt to the adaptive nature of contemporary networks, instead of relying on methods inherited from eras when signals radiated uniformly. That long-term epidemiological studies, particularly among the most exposed populations, be funded with the ambition their importance calls for. That local siting debates, around antennas as around indoor equipment, be conducted in terms accessible to the populations concerned, without technical paternalism and without unnecessary amplification of fears.

The antenna which, from a rooftop no one looks at, weaves the fabric of our communications, is neither a totem nor a demon. It is a technical object whose democratic mastery presupposes that we first understand its function, then monitor its effects, and finally discuss its purposes. These three gestures, as 6G approaches, will lose none of their necessity. On the contrary, they will see it grow.
