There are, in modern life, objects that structure existence without ever being looked at. Mobile telephone antennas belong to this category. Hidden on rooftops, flattened against the sides of church towers, slipped into the false chimneys of apartment buildings, they organize the circulation of information with a density few inhabitants measure. France today counts more than fifty thousand sites dedicated to 5G alone, in addition to those still carrying 4G and, in declining proportions, 3G and 2G. This infrastructure, as invisible as it is decisive, alone carries the essential part of contemporary communications — professional or intimate, commercial or political.

The question of what it actually does, what it emits, what it implies for health and for collective life, remains one of the most poorly posed in public debate. Between the promotional narrative of operators, the sometimes poorly documented concerns of certain collectives, and the prudent silence of a science that more readily acknowledges its uncertainties than controversy permits, one must attempt a calm exposition. What follows does not claim to settle anything. The point is to establish what we know, what we ignore, and why.

A mobile telephone antenna is not an autonomous source of energy. It is the terminal node of a network that begins in a computing core, traverses kilometers of optical fiber, and arrives at a radio equipment placed high up for simple reasons of propagation. Its function is twofold. It receives the signal emitted by telephones, tablets and connected objects within a given radius, and it sends back to them the signal intended for them. Each antenna covers a zone called a cell, which can extend from a few hundred meters in an urban center to several kilometers in rural areas, depending on the frequency band used. This cellular division is what allows millions of users to communicate simultaneously without disturbing one another, each occupying a precise fraction of the spectrum, under the supervision of software that orchestrates allocations in real time.

The frequencies used respond to a physical trade-off. Low frequencies, around 700 megahertz, pass well through walls and carry far, but transport little data. High frequencies, in the 3.5 gigahertz band, carry much more data but are rapidly blocked by obstacles and require network densification. The so-called millimeter frequencies, around 26 gigahertz, which France has not yet deployed commercially, would push this logic to its limit: considerable bandwidth, range reduced to a few dozen meters, necessity of numerous and closely spaced antennas.

The technical specificity of 5G does not lie only in its bitrates. It lies in the very mode of signal emission. Antennas of previous generations radiated their energy broadly, like a lighthouse continuously sweeping a sector. The smart Massive MIMO antennas, deployed with the arrival of 5G, are composed of multiple emitters integrated into a single piece of equipment and, driven by software, they focus the radio wave beam onto a specific zone. This technique, called beamforming, concentrates energy where it serves and cancels it elsewhere. The antenna ceases to be a diffuser and becomes a directed, mobile, adaptive projector.¹

This change has consequences often misrepresented from both sides. On the operators' side, it allows them to assert, with reason, that average exposure does not increase proportionally with bitrates, since energy is better targeted and therefore less uselessly dissipated. On the critics' side, it nourishes the intuition that individual exposure, within the beam, may be locally higher than what ambient measurements reveal. Both observations are true. They point to the same reality: the measurement tools inherited from previous networks are no longer quite adapted to an infrastructure that emits dynamically and directionally.

French figures are now relatively stable in order of magnitude. According to the monthly observatories published by the National Frequency Agency, France has exceeded the threshold of fifty thousand authorized 5G sites, a large majority of which are technically operational.² These sites are distributed across three main frequency bands — 700 MHz, 2.1 GHz, and 3.5 GHz — each carrying a different compromise between range and throughput. Four main operators, Orange, Free, Bouygues Telecom and SFR, compete over the density of this coverage with differing strategies. Free privileges antenna count, Orange favors quality on the 3.5 GHz band, Bouygues and SFR seek a balance under the pressure of their regulatory obligations. 2G and 3G, long emblematic of the mobile landscape, are beginning their slow extinction.

That figure, taken in isolation, says little. Placed in its history, it describes a silent transformation. In a few years, France has built the essential of a new digital infrastructure, without a public debate comparable in scale to that which accompanied the first antenna implantations in the 1990s. The operation proceeded in successive layers, through agreements among operators, through mutualization of existing sites, without citizens having the opportunity, most of the time, to weigh in on technical or territorial choices.

6G, whose media promises largely precede its industrial reality, does not yet exist as a deployable technology. It exists as an international standardization program. Under the authority of the International Telecommunication Union, the so-called IMT-2030 framework defines the vision of what it will permit. 3GPP, the body that brings together the major equipment manufacturers and operators worldwide, is working on the elaboration of the first properly 6G specifications, which must feed, according to a calendar set by the ITU, a commercial deployment around the end of the decade.³

The technologies that will carry 6G have not all been settled. They will probably include a finer exploitation of millimeter and sub-terahertz frequencies, an integration of radar detection functions into the communications infrastructure, a native coordination with low-orbit satellite constellations. The use cases invoked, from remote surgery to autonomous vehicles, often cover promises already made for 5G, and only partially realized. It is reasonable to listen to these announcements with the same mix of attention and skepticism owed to any industrial communication carried by considerable economic interests.

It is around health that uncertainties concentrate most honestly, and where simplifications abound most massively. A few reference points are indispensable to understand what science has established and what it continues to examine.

In 2011, a working group convened by the International Agency for Research on Cancer classified radiofrequency electromagnetic fields in group 2B, possibly carcinogenic to humans, with indications of an increased risk of glioma and acoustic neuroma.⁴ An agent classified 2B by the IARC designates an agent for which an association with cancer has been detected and can be causally interpreted, without chance, biases, and confounding factors being able to be ruled out with sufficient scientific certainty. This classification means neither that exposure to radiofrequencies causes cancer, nor that it is harmless. It means that doubt has not been closed.

Since 2011, two large experimental studies, one by the American National Toxicology Program and the other by the Ramazzini Institute in Italy, have observed in rats excesses of certain tumors after prolonged exposure to radiofrequencies.⁵ Several epidemiological studies, in the opposite direction, have not identified a clear signal at the scale of human populations. Recent meta-analyses conducted by the teams of Martin Röösli at the Swiss Tropical and Public Health Institute in Basel and of Maria Feychting at the Karolinska Institute in Stockholm — two of the most recognized centers in radiofrequency epidemiology — confirm this absence of a clear population signal, while acknowledging the methodological limitations of a field where past exposure is difficult to reconstruct.⁶ The compatibility between these animal results and these human data remains one of the most debated questions in the literature. For this reason, an IARC advisory group has recently recommended the inclusion of radiofrequency fields in the priorities of the current Monographs program, with a view to a reassessment under the Agency's revised procedure. A formal reassessment is thus underway, without its conclusions being foreseeable today.

In France, the National Agency for Food, Environmental and Occupational Health Safety, in its updated opinion, concluded that it was unlikely that the deployment of 5G would be accompanied by new health risks compared to 3G or 4G.⁷ This conclusion deserves to be read with rigor. It does not say that radiofrequencies are harmless. It says that the addition of 5G, in frequencies already in use or in the 3.5 GHz band, does not introduce qualitatively new risks that existing studies would have failed to identify. The Agency itself emphasized that data on the 26 GHz band, not yet deployed in France, remained too scarce to allow a robust assessment.

The regulatory limits of public exposure, derived from the recommendations of the International Commission on Non-Ionizing Radiation Protection, were revised in 2020. They rest on the principle that the proven health effect of radiofrequencies is a thermal effect, observable at levels far higher than those of everyday exposure. The measurements conducted by the National Frequency Agency over the last decade observed give a concrete picture of this exposure. The average global exposure level in France has gone from around 0.68 V/m to around 1.2 V/m, with an average recorded in cities nearly three times higher than in the countryside; the measured levels all remain well below the limit values set by law, which vary between 28 V/m and 87 V/m depending on the frequency.⁸ This rise is measurable over time. It remains, at this stage, largely below the thresholds retained by the health authorities.

Several methodological controversies nevertheless run through this apparent consensus. The question of so-called non-thermal effects, the hypothesis that biological phenomena could appear without a measurable rise in tissue temperature, remains open in the literature. That of cumulative exposures and their influence on children, whose nervous systems continue to form, is the subject of abundant but contradictory studies. That, finally, of the independence of certain international protection bodies, foremost among them the ICNIRP, is the subject of a real debate in the literature — between those who document structural links between its members and the industrial actors of the sector, and those who emphasize the unavoidable narrowness of a restricted field of expertise where the same specialists find themselves on both sides of the bodies consulted.

One evidence must nevertheless be stated before closing. Connectivity is no longer today one technological choice among others, a consumer product one could adopt or refuse individually. It has become, over recent decades, the very substrate of the organization of contemporary societies. Cities, whether they present themselves as new or rearrange older fabrics, rest on data flows that regulate their transports, their energy, their public services, their security. Armed conflicts are now played out, in large measure, on the mastery of sensors, satellite communications, drones and digital command systems. Medicine, from remote care to implantable devices and synchronized imaging, relies on permanent data exchanges between patients, practitioners and machines. Space exploration and exploitation, which will determine a growing share of global strategic balances, rely on satellite constellations that are themselves communication nodes. Artificial intelligence, which has become in a few years the principal driver of industrial and strategic investment on a global scale, functions only through the permanent circulation of data between computing centers, users and connected objects; its most massive models as well as its most everyday uses depend entirely on the bandwidth, latency and resilience of the networks that carry them. 6G is, moreover, conceived in its very specifications to natively accommodate this load, integrating learning mechanisms into its radio architecture; the infrastructure and the machine it feeds now develop together, in a symbiosis that neither could undo. Down to the most everyday life, from payment to learning, which now passes through invisible infrastructures whose failure would immediately be felt as a collapse.

This irreversibility changes the nature of the question posed. It is no longer about deliberating whether we want a connected world, as one deliberates the widening of a highway or the closing of a power plant. This world is already here, and societies that tried to extract themselves from it unilaterally would not be retreating toward an earlier age — they would simply be marginalizing themselves within the present. The honest question thus becomes another one. It consists of asking how to inhabit an infrastructure on which we depend without having always chosen it, how to correct its excesses without denying its necessity, how to inscribe into its governance requirements that economy and speed tend to erase.

Public debate on 5G has often locked itself into a sterile opposition between progress and precaution. It would gain by recognizing that these two attitudes are not alternatives. The digital infrastructure that today connects territories provides real services, to professions that depend on it, to zones long poorly served, to an economy whose competitiveness now depends on the speed of exchanges. The health uncertainties that remain, minor as they may be in the literature, are no less real and call for proportionate vigilance, not blind consent.

This vigilance requires three simple demands. That measurement instruments adapt to the adaptive nature of contemporary networks, rather than settle for methods inherited from eras when the signal radiated uniformly. That long-term epidemiological studies, particularly among the most exposed populations, be funded with the ambition their importance requires. That local implantation debates, around antennas as around indoor equipment, be conducted in terms accessible to the populations concerned, without technical paternalism and without unnecessary amplification of fears.

The antenna that, from a rooftop no one looks at, weaves the fabric of our communications is neither a totem nor a demon. It is a technical object whose democratic mastery supposes that one first understand its function, then monitor its effects, and finally discuss its purposes. These three gestures, as 6G approaches, will lose nothing of their necessity. On the contrary, they will see it grow.
