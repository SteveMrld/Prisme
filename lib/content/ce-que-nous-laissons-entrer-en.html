There is a gesture we have been making for so long that we no longer see it. At night, before sleeping, we take the book from the bedside table, we open it, and we let someone enter the bedroom. A dead person, often. Someone who is not here, who cannot see us, who will never know that we read them tonight rather than anyone else. We welcome their thought, their sentence, their breath. We let it settle in this place where we let almost no one in. Then we turn off the light, and the sentence goes on resonating, for a few minutes, in the dark.

One could write the history of civilisations through that gesture. What they agreed to let in through their bedroom doors. What they refused. What they filtered, ordered, passed on, burned. Books, since they have existed, are the most intimate objects we carry all the way to our beds. We do not open them just anywhere. We do not lend them to just anyone. They travel through the rooms of the house without our noticing, they live beside us like a more patient presence than others, they age with pages that yellow and sometimes bear annotations only we understand.

That is perhaps why it has become so hard, today, to say what is happening in the world of publishing. Because the words we have to describe it — concentration, acquisitions, groups, assets — belong to another register. The register of business. And while we speak of business, something else is at work, more quietly, in a language we have not yet fully learned to speak.

A line we no longer saw

For a long time, there were doors. Thresholds, marked, visible, ritualised. One did not enter someone's home without knocking. One did not turn on the radio at just any hour. One did not open a book without taking the time to enter it properly. The house was a place where the world stopped at a certain line, and where we decided, sometimes explicitly, often without thinking about it, what could cross that line and what should stay outside. That decision, we made ourselves. It was a form of sovereignty that did not call itself by that name.

Screens dissolved that line. Gradually, without anyone really objecting, we agreed to let things enter our homes that we had not chosen. Images that appear without being summoned, voices that come on the moment a button is pressed, notifications that cross the wall as though the wall were no longer there. This is not a criticism, it is an observation. The very idea of a threshold has slipped. The house has become a porous place, traversed by flows, where what enters and what leaves no longer quite depends on us.

One thing remains, however, a single thing that we still choose. The books we put on our tables. For those, there is still a gesture. One must look for them, buy them, open them. A conscious movement is required. In a world where everything else pours in without being asked for, the book is the last door to which we still held the key.

This, everyone had forgotten. Or pretended to have forgotten. Until some began buying the houses.

They started with the houses

In recent years, in almost every great Western democracy, something has happened that has not had the echo it should have had. Businessmen have begun buying up, one by one, the historic publishing houses. Different men, different reasons, yet a common logic taking shape before our eyes. The great literary imprints, those that published the books which shaped our libraries, are changing hands. Founders, where any remain, are pushed aside. Editorial directors who have given a lifetime to a house are thanked and let go. Things are rationalised, reoriented, brought into order.

Habit invites us to read this as an economic matter. That is its visible side. Publishing houses, as enterprises, have structural difficulties that industrial groups manage better than independent publishers. Synergies with distribution chains, with the press, with digital platforms, make these acquisitions rational from a balance-sheet perspective. One could stop there. Many stop there, because it is the easy reading.

But one must pause on what is really at stake. When an industrial group buys a publishing house, it does not simply buy a catalogue. It does not simply buy a machine for producing books. It buys something more precious, which appears on no balance sheet: the right to decide, for years to come, which voices will have a paper body and which will not. The right to choose who will be published and who will not. The right, more deeply still, to define what, in the production of human thought, deserves to be taken the risk of printing.

That right is what is bought. And that right is a key.

The key to a door we had forgotten. The door through which a book enters a home, passes into a reader's hands, settles on a bedside table, becomes a presence. To buy the house that produces the book is to buy, upstream, the very possibility of that presence. It is to buy the right to enter people's homes without their knowing who, exactly, has decided the entry.

No one burns anything anymore

One must not mistake the nature of what is happening. We inherited, from previous centuries, a grammar of censorship that no longer describes what is taking place. Censorship, as we learned it, is a power that says no to a book. The Inquisition, the Index, book burnings, black lists, bans. A text exists, and is suppressed. An author speaks, and is silenced. That power had a brutal visibility. One knew who was censoring, one knew what was being censored, one could oppose it.

The stakes today are of a different nature. No one says no to a book. No political power, in Western democracy, comes to ban a title. The texts that someone would like to make disappear are not suppressed: they are simply not chosen. They do not enter the catalogues. Their authors do not find publishers. Their manuscripts come back with a polite letter. No fire, no ban, no trial. A silence, only. A silence discreet enough that one cannot plead against it.

That form is infinitely more effective than classical censorship. Because it cannot be seen. Because it creates no martyrs. Because it warns no one. A reader who has never heard of a book that was not published cannot feel its absence. They do not even know that something is missing. They live in a world in which the available books seem to be all possible books. They take the offer for the whole.

That is the door through which possession changes everything. When a few groups control the houses that decide which manuscripts become books, they censor no one. They simply shape the range of what can be read. They do not say what is forbidden. They say what is possible. And that difference, on the scale of a civilisation, is the one that shapes the thought of the generations to come.

A presence, not a product

To understand what is being lost, one must perhaps recall what a book really is. Not what it is worth on the market, not what it earns its publisher, not what it costs its reader. What it is, in the relation it establishes.

A book is not a quantity of information made available. It is an offer made to a reader by someone who has taken the time to write. An offer of company for hours. Of presence in the moments when one is alone. Of thought in the moments when one is searching. The book offers to accompany us, not to fill us. It does not claim to teach us something, it offers to think with us. That offer is an act of trust. The author gives themselves — the word is exact — to someone they will never meet. The reader accepts that offer by taking, in turn, the risk of being moved, displaced, transformed.

That relation has nothing to do with a product. It has to do with what other languages call presence. And presence is not something that is manufactured in series. It requires that, upstream, someone decide that a text deserves to be offered to readers. A publishing house is not a factory for books. It is a threshold between someone who writes and someone who will read. The threshold must be kept by people who know how to recognise what deserves to pass. There must, in other words, behind every book published, be a form of love of the text. A love that has been tested, worked, passed down from one generation of editors to the next, and that is not transmitted in quarterly review meetings.

When the houses become assets in the portfolios of industrial groups, we do not lose the material capacity to print books. We will print even more of them than before. What we lose is the long chain of transmissions through which a love of the text has been kept alive, from one editor to their successor, sometimes for over a century. That chain breaks in a generation. It is not rebuilt.

We no longer know how to inherit

One must then ask what it says, of a civilisation, that its books become assets. The answer is less flattering than we would like to believe.

We have ceased to understand what a book is. We see only an object in an economy. We no longer see the offer it carries, the presence it gives, the gesture it implies. We no longer see, in other words, that a book is not a product. And because we no longer see it, we accept that it be treated as such by those who, for their part, have never pretended to see it as anything else.

There is worse. We have ceased to feel that a last place of our own remained to us. With the screens having lost their frontier, we might have understood that books were the last thing whose entry we still chose. We might have defended that remainder. We did not. We let the door be bought without defending it, because we no longer saw it as a door.

And then there is this, more difficult to say. We have perhaps become people who no longer know how to distinguish between what is owned and what is passed on. Publishing houses, like universities, like theatres, like museums, are not goods. They are places of transmission. One does not own them, one inherits them, and one has the responsibility to pass them on. A society that treats such places as assets has lost the very notion of what an inheritance is. It lives in the present as though the present were enough.

The reader remains

One could, at this point, conclude on a note of resignation. Say that the movement is set in motion, that the groups are too powerful, that nothing will stop it. That would be false. Because there remains, in this story, one actor whom no one really talks about and who holds, nonetheless, the last word. The reader.

Those who buy the houses bet on our inattention. They bet that we will not tell the difference, on the shelf, between a book published because it deserved to be and a book published because it fitted a strategy. They bet that we will take what is offered, without looking for what is not on the table. They bet, in short, that we have forgotten that the choice of what we let into our homes still belongs to us.

That bet is not won. Because it depends entirely on us. We can, at any moment, take up again the gesture that every generation before us had — to choose, ourselves, what we let enter our homes. To look for the houses that still hold. To read the independent publishers, the small structures, the voices that do not pass through the great groups. To pass on to our children, by the simple gesture of placing a book on their table, that this book was chosen, that not all books are, and that the distinction matters.

We still hold the door. As long as we hold it, it is ours.
