The Silk Road, projection of power

From 2013 onward, China took a further step. The Belt and Road Initiative, the New Silk Roads, is the most ambitious investment program ever conceived by a state since the American Marshall Plan. More than one hundred and fifty signatory countries, trillions of dollars of commitments in infrastructure, ports, railways, and digital networks.

The logic is not merely commercial. Every port built by Chinese companies is a potential foothold. Every network deployed by Huawei is a vector of influence. Every debt contracted by a sovereign state to Beijing is a diplomatic lever. Sri Lanka, unable to repay its loans, ceded the port of Hambantota to a Chinese state enterprise for ninety-nine years. The geography of debt becomes the geography of power.3

The Belt and Road has also allowed China to export its model of digital governance. The urban surveillance systems deployed in Ethiopia, Zimbabwe, Ecuador, and Pakistan by companies such as Huawei, Hikvision, or ZTE are not simply commercial contracts; they are architecture transfers. States that adopt them import with them a conception of the relationship between power and data that is not neutral. This is what researcher Steven Feldstein has documented under the name of "authoritarian AI diffusion."4

The 15th Five-Year Plan, the decisive mutation

The Five-Year Plan presented to the National People's Congress in March 2026 marks a major paradigm shift. For the first time, Beijing no longer measures its technological ambition in terms of production, how many chips manufactured, what percentage of semiconductor autonomy, but in terms of deployment: the share of the digital economy in GDP must reach twelve and a half percent by 2030.5 Beijing is no longer chasing components. It is building the infrastructure that makes them indispensable.

Artificial intelligence is cited more than fifty times in the document. The objectives cover semiconductors, batteries, biomedicine, 6G networks, commercial aviation, and humanoid robots. The formulation adopted, "wage the battle for key core technologies," deliberately borrows military register. This is not a metaphor. It is a doctrine.6

Beijing is betting everything on AI and advanced manufacturing in a high-stakes rebalancing where the government is playing its economic future.

Andy Ji, analyst, ITC Markets, March 2026

The technology war, decisive front

American sanctions on semiconductors, tightened since 2022 and progressively extended to the entire technology ecosystem, have attempted to block this momentum. They have partially succeeded, delaying China's access to the most advanced chips. But they have also produced the opposite of the intended effect: by forcing Beijing to develop its domestic capabilities, they have accelerated a process of autonomization that was already underway.

DeepSeek, the Chinese artificial intelligence model presented in early 2025, demonstrated that frontier-level capability was attainable with constrained hardware. This is not a technical anecdote; it is a strategic rupture. It means that the American advantage in the most advanced chips is not necessarily synonymous with advantage in AI applications.7 China is also installing approximately ten times more industrial robots than the United States, making its production base structurally more efficient and competitive.8

The monetary ambition, keystone

Economic dominance will remain incomplete as long as the US dollar remains the world's reserve currency. This is why China is pursuing an internationalization policy for the yuan, still modest in volume but significant in direction. Commercial exchanges with Russia, Saudi Arabia, Brazil, and many African countries are increasingly denominated in yuan. Beijing has developed its own interbank payment system, CIPS, as an alternative to SWIFT. Chinese central banks are massively accumulating gold reserves, reducing their exposure to American Treasury bonds.

China has also announced its intention to maintain its competitive advantage in rare earths, those materials vital for AI chips, defense systems, and electric vehicles. The United States and its allies remain years away from autonomy in these supplies.9 This is a lever that Beijing does not hesitate to use in trade negotiations.

The vulnerabilities the strategy itself produced

China's rise has been accompanied by structural contradictions that the conquest strategy has itself aggravated. They deserve to be named, because they condition the trajectory of the coming decades as much as the successes do.

Demographics is the first fundamental challenge. The Chinese population has begun to decline, a first since the famines of the Great Leap Forward. The active-to-retiree ratio is deteriorating at a speed that has no equivalent in developed economies, except in Japan. The growth model based on abundant and cheap labor already belongs partly to the past. Industrial robotization partially compensates, but does not resolve the question of pension financing or domestic consumption.10

The property crisis constitutes the second front. The sector represented at its peak approximately 25% of Chinese GDP, counting direct and indirect activities. The collapse of Evergrande in 2021 was not an anomaly; it was the symptom of a development model based on a real estate bubble sustained by local finances. The forced deleveraging of the sector weighs on household confidence, consumption, and local government finances, whose budgets were massively dependent on land sales to developers.11

The third challenge is less visible but potentially the most structuring for technological ambition: the software ecosystem. Chinese companies themselves identify this in their financial reports as their main constraint. NVIDIA dominates the global market for AI GPUs not solely through its chips but through its software ecosystem CUDA, millions of lines of code, years of developer training, a global community. Replicating the chip is difficult. Replicating the ecosystem that makes it useful is a challenge of another order.12

The European response, between dependence and awakening

Europe occupies a singular and uncomfortable position in this Sino-American rivalry. It is China's largest trading partner and the United States' closest strategic ally, two roles increasingly difficult to maintain simultaneously as the rivalry intensifies.

Ursula von der Leyen's formulation in 2023, "de-risking" rather than "decoupling," attempted to name this median position: reducing critical dependencies without severing economic ties. This nuance quickly encountered its limits. On semiconductors, the American CHIPS Act compelled ASML, the Dutch company that produces the lithographic exposure machines indispensable for manufacturing advanced chips, to cease deliveries to China, a decision taken in Washington, applied in Europe. On electric vehicles, Brussels imposed compensatory tariffs against Chinese prices judged subsidized, exposing itself to commercial retaliation in sectors where Europe remains vulnerable.

The European question is not simply economic; it is strategic and political. Can Europe define itself as an autonomous actor in a world reorganizing itself into blocs, or is it condemned to choose sides? The answer to this question depends less on its economic strength than on its capacity to develop its own technological and industrial doctrine, something it has not yet done with the necessary clarity and coherence.13

What the Thucydides Trap reveals

Graham Allison has catalogued sixteen historical cases of confrontation between a dominant power and a rising power. War occurs in twelve of them. The question is not whether the Sino-American rivalry is real — it is, profoundly. The question is whether the two parties will find the mechanisms for a managed competition, or whether escalation will carry these mechanisms away before they can take hold.14

Prospective, What comes next

Three scenarios for the decade ahead

Managed competition. The two powers maintain sufficient diplomatic channels to avoid military escalation, while waging an intense economic and technological war. This is the most probable scenario in the short term, and the most costly for the rest of the world, forced to choose sides on every technological issue.

Accelerated decoupling. Sanctions, tariffs, and technology restrictions ultimately split the global economy into two distinct blocs, a dollar-centered ecosystem and a yuan-centered ecosystem. This scenario is silently being built, and its partial realization is already visible in supply chains.

Taiwan crisis. Bloomberg Economics estimates that a conflict in the Taiwan Strait would cost approximately $10.6 trillion to the global economy in the first year, roughly ten percent of global GDP. This scenario remains less probable than the first two, but its occurrence would permanently transform the international order.

The stake is not whether Beijing will succeed in dethroning Washington. It is the question of what form the multipolar world that is emerging will take, and how middle powers, including Europe, will find and hold their place before the places are taken.
