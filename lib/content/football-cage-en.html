Every sporting competition has its favorites and its outsiders, the kings it crowns and the princes whose birth it records. Five billion viewers should, between 11 June and 19 July 2026, catch at least a moment of the story that the twenty-third edition of the FIFA World Cup is about to write. A story in the image of contemporary football business, built on powerful certainties, geopolitical, commercial and financial, but still open to enough uncertainty for this sport to remain a sport.

Football, a major geopolitical object

Among the certainties, first of all, football has, more than perhaps any other sport, become a major geopolitical object. Club football is a barely contested instrument of soft power for Qatar (Paris Saint-Germain), the United Arab Emirates (Manchester City) or again Saudi Arabia (Newcastle). To host an edition of the World Cup is an assertion of power on a planetary scale. It troubles itself little with human rights, as it troubles itself little with the carbon impact of the geographies of its venues. The 2026 edition across three neighboring states is merely the appetizer for a 2030 World Cup that will be jointly hosted by Spain, Portugal and Morocco, but three of whose opening matches will be played in Argentina, Paraguay and Uruguay. The 2034 edition, for its part, goes to Saudi Arabia.

To satisfy certain ambitions, the leaders of contemporary football business come to burden themselves neither with the results of a competition, the final victory in the last Africa Cup of Nations was awarded to Morocco two months after Senegal had won the final on the pitch, nor with the basic rules of decency. The president of FIFA, Gianni Infantino, has for a decade devoted his mandate to entrenching his power at the head of world football and, to that end, does not hesitate to show himself the most absolute (and absolutely ridiculous) of the courtiers of the 47th president of the United States. He thus handed Donald Trump, in an impromptu ceremony, a trophy that seemed to have come straight out of a metaverse in which the Nobel Peace Prize had been turned into the FIFA Peace Prize. Never mind the obligations of neutrality that theoretically bind him. Nothing is worth more than the advantage of pleasing the supposedly strong man of the hour.

A telling submission of a financial and political giant to the whims of an American president who means to be omnipresent in the organization of the 2026 World Cup, which he dreams of making a success as remarkable as the United States edition of 1994. The most striking and unacceptable symbol of this submission is FIFA's inaction after the best African referee of 2025, the Somali Omar Abdulkadir Artan, was refused entry into United States territory, removed from the World Cup after several hours of detention at Miami airport despite a valid visa. This treatment is only the most extreme face of the humiliations imposed on several delegations. And yet FIFA's power today is measured as much by the number of its two hundred and eleven member national associations (more numerous than the member countries of the United Nations) as by its financial weight: its annual budget of 8.9 billion euros exceeds the GDP of nearly sixty countries in the world. Now the one thing Donald Trump did not manage to obtain for the competition was the replacement of the Iranian side (qualified) by the Italian side (not qualified), an idea put to FIFA by his adviser Paolo Zampoli, "special representative of the United States for global partnerships." Mexico, the other host of the edition along with Canada, agreed to welcome to Tijuana the base camp of the Tim melli, which will play its group matches in Los Angeles and Seattle.

The FIFA World Cup, an unsurpassable marketing showcase

If Mexico is represented by green on the competition's official ball, it has for several weeks already been decked in Coca-Cola red. The plane that brought the World Cup trophy into the country wore the colors of the American drinks giant. The hyperpower of Coca-Cola is present everywhere there, from the shelves of the tiendas to the giant billboards, from the meals of adults to those of children, from political careers to the battle for access to water. Coca-Cola accounts for 2.8 percent of the country's wage bill and 2 percent of its GDP. The group could not let this edition of the World Cup pass without entrenching, a little further still, its weight, indeed its supremacy, in the country.

The great sporting competitions are a major investment for the American giant. For Coca-Cola it is not so much a question of fame, according to Benoit Heilbrunn, on average, an individual, wherever he may be in the world, is never more than five minutes or fifty meters from the brand, as of omnipresence, to make certain that the consumption of its drinks goes without saying. The moment this self-evidence disappears, the brand finds itself singularly weakened. The Portuguese icon Ronaldo, on the sidelines of Euro 2021, triggered a 1.6 percent fall in Coca-Cola's share price on the New York Stock Exchange by pushing aside two bottles of the soda at a press conference.

Among the new rules of the 2026 World Cup, the double cooling break (one per half) turns the competition's matches a little more into advertising showcases of high potential. The minute of advertising it will allow in the middle of each half will not have the commercial scale of the Super Bowl spots, which in some years arouse more media attention than the sporting stakes of the NFL final. But this break symbolizes the soccerization of football, or to put it another way the Americanization of a sport that nonetheless barely existed in the United States a little more than thirty years ago. The demands of an entertainment saturating time and space impose themselves in part over those of sporting logic.

The genius of the game

There remains, happily, that dose of uncertainty that makes a sport a sport. That dose of uncertainty thanks to which, among the forty-eight countries that will contest this World Cup, will line up Curaçao (160,000 inhabitants, 450 km² in area) and Haiti, thanks to which too, in 2022 during the group stage, Saudi Arabia beat Argentina, and the Algerian Fennecs, forty years earlier, for their first World Cup, defeated West Germany.

In 2021, the announcement by twelve European clubs of the creation of a closed Super League, within which fifteen clubs out of twenty would each year be qualified automatically, had as its main aim to guarantee the richest clubs a financial annuity shielded from any sporting hazard. A sporting hazard that these twelve clubs undergo significantly, and so naturally, despite their financial power, as their fates in the Champions League since 2022 bear witness.

While this project, abandoned forty-eight hours after its announcement, had as its "official" vocation to answer the supposed desire of supporters to see more matches between "big clubs," in aggregate the twelve promoters of the Super League failed to qualify fourteen times over the last five editions of the Champions League, and let three of the last five titles slip away. Rather than pit themselves each year against clubs that are small financially but capable of proving great in sporting terms (Sporting Portugal, FK Bodø/Glimt, Feyenoord Rotterdam, Atalanta Bergamo, LOSC or Stade Brestois), it seems simpler to these twelve clubs to shield themselves from sporting hazard by sealing off their closed league. But football is not wrestling. It remains a sport and, to take up the words of coach Pep Guardiola in 2021, a sport is not a sport "when the relationship between effort and success, between effort and work, no longer exists," "when success is guaranteed" and "when it does not matter whether you lose."

The extreme financialization of club football is another lever for reducing sporting hazard. By authorizing the multiple ownership of clubs, UEFA now allows some of them to be turned into reserve teams for bigger stables. Their identities and their sporting journeys are royally ignored by their shareholders, who lend or transfer players and coaches without paying any heed to the human, whether on the pitch, in the dressing rooms or in the stands. The oligarchic excesses of football do not only weaken sporting merit. They can destroy what is for some merely a toy, but which in fact is a club, with a history, a training academy, lovers of the shirt and a true playing identity.

Financial power alone does not yet make football, however, even if it counts. Paris Saint-Germain won its first two big-eared Cups because the club adopted, with its Spanish coach Luis Enrique, a playing philosophy founded on individual sacrifice in the service of the collective effort. This philosophy has no price, but of course, having the best players in the world in almost every position (Nuno Mendes, Vitinha, Khvicha Kvaratskhelia, Ousmane Dembélé) has one. Will the 2026 World Cup offer runs as unexpected as that of Georgia at Euro 2024? Most likely. Will they amount to feats? Assuredly. Football is a sport that must be analyzed without naivety but that can still be watched with stars in the eyes.
