If Washington genuinely wanted to wage a war on drug trafficking, it would have had to intervene in Mexico. Where the fentanyl is manufactured. Where the cartels control entire regions. But Mexico is a major trading partner. And China, which provides the precursors, is a nuclear superpower.

Venezuela, for its part, possesses neither an army capable of resisting nor a nuclear umbrella. On the other hand, it possesses the world's largest proven oil reserves, ahead of Saudi Arabia, Iran, and Iraq. Trump is explicit about this. He announces that American companies will "exploit" these resources. In 2003, Washington invaded Iraq in the name of weapons of mass destruction that did not exist. Twenty-three years later, drug trafficking replaces chemical weapons. The objective remains.

Two centuries of exclusive hunting ground

In 1823, President James Monroe laid before Congress the principle that would structure two centuries of domination: America for the Americans. Meaning: Latin America for the United States. This doctrine, presented as a bulwark against European colonial ambitions, proved to be what it was: the claim of an exclusive preserve. The South American continent became Washington's "backyard." The expression says it all. One does not consult one's backyard. One does with it as one pleases.

Theodore Roosevelt would add his "corollary" in 1904: the United States arrogated to itself the right to intervene in the internal affairs of Latin American countries to maintain order. The "big stick" policy was born.

The catalog of interventions is striking. Guatemala 1954: the CIA overthrows Jacobo Árbenz to protect the interests of the United Fruit Company. Chile 1973: Salvador Allende dies under Pinochet's bombs, with Washington's blessing. Henry Kissinger encourages the Argentine junta in 1976 to "finish quickly" its dirty war. Operation Condor coordinates the intelligence services of six dictatorships to assassinate opponents across three continents. The School of the Americas trains 60,000 Latin American officers in the practice of the coup.3

What Trump is doing is not a rupture. It is the culmination of two centuries of Monroe Doctrine. The difference is that he no longer bothers to lie.

Republicans, Democrats: same logic

It would be wrong to believe that this brutality is Trump's exclusive domain. Obama supported the parliamentary coup against Dilma Rousseff in Brazil in 2016. Clinton looked the other way on the overthrow of Manuel Zelaya in Honduras in 2009. The continuity runs across party changeovers. What changes with Trump is only the staging: the trophy photo on Truth Social, the anti-military Creedence Clearwater Revival song played during videos of the operation. The irony is probably lost on him.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio confirms that Maduro will be tried in the United States, not by an international tribunal, but by American justice. Trump threatens Colombian President Gustavo Petro. He warns Cuba. The meaning is clear: the Monroe Doctrine does not concern only Venezuela. It applies to the entire hemisphere.4

International law was already dying

Article 2 paragraph 4 of the United Nations Charter prohibits the use of force against the territorial integrity of any state. It would be naive to believe that this principle collapsed on this particular night in Caracas. It has been eroding for decades.

Iraq in 2003, invaded without a UN mandate. Libya in 2011, where a humanitarian resolution was transformed into regime change. Drone-targeted assassinations in Pakistan, Yemen, Somalia. The annexation of Crimea. Each time, the same facade condemnations, the same impotence. The UN Secretary-General says he is "concerned." China speaks of "hegemonic behavior." Russia of an "act of armed aggression." The French Foreign Minister recalls that the operation "contravenes the principle of non-use of force."5

Trump is not burying international law. He is dancing on its grave. And in doing so, he reveals something we were pretending not to see: this law was already protecting almost no one. It protected states powerful enough to impose respect for it on others. For the rest, it was a hope.

An operation too clean?

One question remains that few analyses have yet posed. This operation unfolded with disconcerting smoothness. Over 150 aircraft mobilized, strikes on multiple strategic sites, a nighttime capture, helicopter exfiltration, and in the end, no significant resistance. Maduro and his wife "surrendered without resistance," according to General Caine. The streets of Caracas remained calm. The Venezuelan army, supposedly loyal to the regime, did not move. Vice President Delcy Rodríguez said she was "ready to cooperate" with Washington just hours after the strikes.6

For an operation against a head of state described as an entrenched dictator surrounded by an army of 120,000 men and loyal militias, all of this seems strangely smooth. Perhaps American intelligence pulled off a masterstroke. Perhaps the Venezuelan army simply chose not to die for Maduro. Or perhaps negotiations took place in the shadows, and this spectacular capture conceals an arrangement whose terms we do not yet know.

What this image says about our era

This photograph of Maduro, blindfolded and handcuffed, published as a trophy, is the Monroe Doctrine reduced to its simplest expression. Whatever one thinks of the man and his regime, what is at stake here extends far beyond his person. A head of state captured without international procedure, a country whose conqueror announces he will "exploit the oil," and the staging of force on social networks. No tribunal, no warrant. Just force.

Latin America has been paying the price of its geography for two centuries. Too close to the United States, too rich in resources, too weak to resist. The 30,000 disappeared in Argentina, the tortured in Chile, the victims of Plan Condor still haunt the continent's memory. Eduardo Galeano described in 1971 a continent bled for five centuries, stripped of its gold, silver, copper, and oil by powers that never ceased imposing their laws and their dictators.7 That book has not aged a day.

What Operation "Absolute Resolve" reveals, beyond Venezuela, is the actual state of the international system. For middle powers, Europe first among them, the question is no longer theoretical. In a world where force prevails over law, what is the value of a sovereignty that rests on no capacity to defend it? The answer to this question is not found in facade condemnations. It is built, or it is not built.
