There exists a geography that ordinary maps do not show. Not that of states, borders, or seas, but that of the resources without which no phone rings, no server runs, no electric car starts. This geography (underground and invisible) has become, over the course of a single generation, the theater of great-power competition. Whoever controls its nodes controls not merely raw materials. They control the cognitive and industrial infrastructure of the contemporary world.

Rare earths first. Seventeen elements of the periodic table (cerium, neodymium, dysprosium, terbium, among others) whose electromagnetic properties are irreplaceable in the manufacture of permanent magnets, screens, catalysts, and military guidance systems. Their name is misleading: they are not rare in any geological sense. Neodymium is more abundant in the Earth's crust than tin. What is rare is the capacity to refine them. And that capacity, China built methodically over forty years, combining state subsidies, the absence of environmental regulation, and long-term industrial strategy. The result: Beijing today controls 60% of global production and 85% of refining. This is not a geological coincidence. It is a political decision.

This monopoly did not remain theoretical. In 2010, following a diplomatic incident over disputed islands with Japan, Beijing abruptly reduced its rare earth exports to Tokyo. The Japanese archipelago, 90% dependent on Chinese supplies for its electronics industry, found itself in a situation of acute vulnerability. The episode revealed to the entire world the nature of the weapon China had just built. Since then, Beijing has systematically deployed this tool: export restrictions on critical metals in response to American semiconductor sanctions, special licensing requirements for defense-related rare earths, progressive nationalization of strategic resources.3

The logic is that of an asymmetric retaliatory weapon. China cannot compete directly with American military power. It can, however, deprive American and European industries of the materials without which their technologies cannot function. This is not an abstract threat; it is a demonstrated capability, and it applies to everything: from permanent magnets in electric vehicle motors to heavy rare earths in weapons systems.

Taiwan and the world's most dangerous concentration

If rare earths constitute the subsoil of digital power, semiconductors are its architecture. And here the concentration is of a different nature: not geological but industrial and intellectual, built not over forty years but fifty, with investment intensity that has no equivalent in modern industrial history.

TSMC, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, was founded in 1987 by Morris Chang, a Chinese-American engineer trained at MIT and Stanford. Chang invented the model of the pure-play foundry, manufacturing chips designed by others. This model enabled the emergence of an ecosystem of fabless (factory-free) designers who revolutionized the industry: Apple, Nvidia, AMD, and Qualcomm design their chips but entrust their fabrication to TSMC. The company today handles 92% of the world's advanced chip production.4

This concentration is the most documented and least resolved structural vulnerability in the global economy. An invasion, blockade, or even a major earthquake in Taiwan would instantly paralyze smartphone, AI server, automotive chip, and weapons system production for the entire planet. The term used by American strategists, the Taiwan chokepoint, says everything about how Washington perceives this dependency.

The American response, real but incomplete

American awareness accelerated with the semiconductor shortages of 2020-2021, when the shutdown of Asian factories during the pandemic paralyzed the American automotive industry. The CHIPS and Science Act, signed in 2022, committed $52 billion to relocate manufacturing to American soil. It is the largest American industrial policy program since the New Deal.

The results are real. TSMC is building several plants in Arizona; the first 4-nanometer facility has been in production since 2025, with yields matching those of Taiwan. Intel received billions in subsidies to restart its manufacturing capability. Samsung is investing heavily in Texas. Micron is building memory plants in New York and Idaho. In a few years, the United States is acquiring an advanced manufacturing capability it had not possessed for two decades.5

But this relocation runs up against a reality that money alone cannot resolve: the ecosystem. Semiconductor manufacturing is not reducible to a factory; it requires hundreds of specialized suppliers, precision chemistry subcontractors, and engineers trained in techniques that are learned over decades. This ecosystem exists in Northeast Asia. Moving it to the United States or Europe takes a generation, not a presidential term. TSMC's Arizona plants have encountered recruitment difficulties and cultural friction between American and Taiwanese engineers that concretely illustrate this challenge.6

Breaking China's monopoly on rare earths will take at least a decade, according to experts. The obstacle is not financial: China controls the entire chain, from mine to finished product.

The Chinese strategy over twenty years

Facing American pressure, Beijing is not improvising. Its strategy is the product of long-term industrial planning whose broad outlines have been known since Made in China 2025, published in 2015: achieve semiconductor self-sufficiency by 2030, dominate AI technologies, control global critical material supply chains.

American sanctions have accelerated this trajectory rather than slowing it. Deprived of Nvidia's advanced chips, China has invested massively in developing its own architectures. SMIC, the national foundry champion, has reached manufacturing levels that analysts considered impossible under sanctions. Huawei released a smartphone in 2023 integrating a 7-nanometer chip manufactured in China, a symbolic demonstration that restrictions are not impermeable.7

And DeepSeek showed that algorithmic ingenuity can compensate, at least partially, for the deprivation of computing power. What Chinese engineers accomplished under constraint (developing optimization techniques that drastically reduce computing requirements) could prove, in time, a competitive advantage rather than a handicap. Necessity has generated innovation that abundance might never have produced.

Europe between discreet monopoly and strategic absence

Europe's position in this war is paradoxical. Europe is absent from advanced semiconductor manufacturing: no European company produces chips below 22 nanometers at industrial scale. It imports 98% of its rare earths from China. It has no GAFAM equivalent, no AI champion, no clear doctrine on its technological sovereignty.

And yet it holds a lever that no one else possesses. ASML, a Dutch company born from a joint venture between Philips and a startup in 1984, is the world's sole manufacturer of EUV lithography machines. Without these machines, which cost up to €380 million each and require 100,000 components, no one (neither TSMC nor Samsung nor Intel) can manufacture the most advanced chips. This is an absolute monopoly over the most critical link in the chain.8

This monopoly gave the Netherlands, and indirectly Europe, an unexpected diplomatic lever. Under American pressure, The Hague progressively restricted ASML equipment exports to China, depriving Beijing of the machines necessary for advanced chip production. This is one of the rare cases where a European company sits at the center of a global geopolitical issue.

The rest of the European response is more modest. The European Chips Act of 2023 commits €43 billion to raise Europe's share of global production to 20% by 2030. The Critical Raw Materials Act sets extraction and recycling targets for rare earths. These plans exist, but they run up against the same reality as in the United States: industrial timelines do not accelerate by decree. And Europe has not yet resolved the fundamental question: does it want to be a sovereign actor in technology, or does it accept remaining a dependent market?9

What this war says about the next one

Resource wars do not end when prices fall or when a substitute technology emerges. They end when the balance of dependencies is redistributed. The oil war did not end with the arrival of renewable energy; it mutated. Tensions over rare earths and semiconductors will probably follow the same trajectory.

What has changed is the speed at which technology has become a first-order geopolitical variable. For three decades, states delegated this dimension to the market: companies sourced wherever it was cheapest, manufactured wherever it was most efficient. The result was a concentration of dependencies unprecedented in industrial history. Correcting this error will take as long as it took to accumulate.

For middle powers (Europe first among them) the question is no longer theoretical. In a world where mastery of critical resources conditions political sovereignty as much as military sovereignty, what is the value of a technological independence one did not have the will to build? The answer is not found in five-year plans or speeches about strategic autonomy. It is built in investment decisions, educational choices, industrial alliances: or it is not built at all.
