Tesla, SpaceX, Neuralink, Starlink, X — these companies do not constitute an ordinary portfolio. They embody a systemic vision of the world: remodeled by technology, freed from established institutions, oriented toward horizons their founder deems existential for the human species. With a fortune estimated at over $400 billion, Elon Musk is now less an entrepreneur than a geopolitical force in his own right, capable of influencing armed conflicts, national elections, and continental balances.

What his trajectory reveals is less an exceptional destiny than a particular era, one in which the concentration of technological and financial power in the hands of private individuals surpasses that of many states.

A childhood under the weight of apartheid

Elon Musk was born in 1971 in Pretoria, South Africa, in a country structured by an institutionalized system of racial segregation. He grew up in an environment of deep social tensions, protected by his membership in a privileged class but marked by difficult family dynamics. His father, Errol Musk, a prosperous engineer, passed on scientific and technical knowledge from an early age, but their relationship is described by Musk himself as authoritarian and emotionally violent.¹

Six major companies led simultaneously. Access to the US Treasury's payment systems. One man, an unprecedented concentration of power in a democracy.

At 17, he left South Africa to avoid the compulsory military service imposed by the regime. This departure was not merely geographical: it marked an explicit break with a social order he rejected. From adolescence, he found refuge in science fiction and developed his first programming skills; at 12, he sold a video game of his own design for $500.² These early years provide a frame for understanding certain constants of his thinking: distrust of institutions, attraction to alternative systems, and a fraught relationship with authority.

Asperger syndrome as an analytical variable

In 2021, during a Saturday Night Live appearance, Musk publicly revealed that he has Asperger syndrome.³ This disclosure illuminates several aspects of his public behavior: intense focus on technical problems, relative indifference to social conventions, and communications often perceived as blunt or impulsive. His collaborators describe a leader capable of immersing himself for days in a complex problem, at the expense of the human interactions around him.

This diagnosis does not constitute a sufficient explanation of his strategic choices, but it sheds light on the form his public decisions take, particularly on X, and the tensions they generate in his companies, where former Tesla and SpaceX employees have regularly denounced working conditions deemed untenable.⁴

X and freedom of expression as a political project

The acquisition of Twitter for $44 billion in 2022, rebranded as X, is not an ordinary financial operation. Musk presents it explicitly as a project to refound the digital public sphere, an agora freed from the moderation mechanisms he equates with a form of institutional control.⁵ The drastic reduction of moderation teams, the reactivation of accounts suspended for spreading disinformation, and the algorithmic amplification of conservative content transformed the platform into an ideological experimentation ground.

His defenders see in this a defense of critical thinking and a bulwark against censorship. His opponents consider that these choices produced an environment conducive to hate speech and organized disinformation. Both analyses coexist and say something real: the difficulty of measuring the effects of such a brutal transformation of an information infrastructure used by hundreds of millions of people.

The rejection of "wokism" between ideology and personal experience

Musk has asserted himself for several years as one of the most visible critics of currents he groups under the term "wokism," which he defines as the drift of an original social justice movement toward what he perceives as rigid identity conformism, incompatible with the principles of merit and innovation he places at the center of his companies.⁶

This critique is also rooted in personal experience. His estrangement from his daughter Jenna, who identifies as a transgender woman, is publicly interpreted by Musk as a direct consequence of what he calls "ideological indoctrination" by progressive institutions. This biographical dimension gives his discourse an emotional charge that transcends ordinary political debate. It also illustrates the way individual trajectories can fuel public positions with considerable collective effects.

From Democratic ally to Trump supporter

During the Obama presidency, Musk appeared as a natural ally of the Democrats: Tesla benefited from federal subsidies for the energy transition, SpaceX signed major contracts with NASA. This convergence rested on mutual interests as much as on shared values.⁷

The break began under Biden. Tesla's exclusion from a presidential conference on the automotive industry, in favor of union-backed manufacturers, was experienced by Musk as a symbolic sidelining. The administration's regulatory orientations on digital content deepened the disagreement. In 2024, Musk injected $270 million into Donald Trump's campaign and transformed X into a tool for the mass diffusion of conservative messages.⁸ This repositioning is interpreted, depending on one's reading, as acknowledged pragmatism or as the instrumentalization of private resources in the service of political interests.

Starlink, X, and influence on conflicts and democracies

In Ukraine, Starlink became a strategic actor in the conflict. The supply of thousands of satellite terminals restored vital communications for Ukrainian forces, but Musk also, on several occasions, restricted the network's use for certain military operations, in effect imposing his own rules in a war zone.⁹ This capacity to condition access to critical infrastructure in an armed conflict raises unprecedented questions about the legal and political status of private technology actors.

In Europe, Musk offered a platform to Alice Weidel, leader of the AfD, during a public conversation on X, provoking a major controversy in Germany weeks before federal elections. In the United Kingdom, his attacks on Prime Minister Keir Starmer sparked an unprecedented diplomatic response. In Brazil, his frontal opposition to a Supreme Court judge, accompanied by the reactivation of accounts suspended for disinformation, provoked a direct confrontation with Brazilian judicial institutions.¹⁰

These interventions trace a coherent pattern: a private actor using global technological infrastructures to weigh on national democratic processes, without electoral mandate or clearly established accountability framework. The question is less whether Musk is right or wrong on this or that issue, but rather what regime of legitimacy applies to this type of power.

A future without counterweights?

Elon Musk embodies an unprecedented configuration: a private individual whose decisions affect millions of people across domains as different as wartime communications, national elections, urban mobility, and space exploration. His projects, from the colonization of Mars to Neuralink's brain-computer interface, raise questions that extend far beyond his person.

The issue is less Musk's personality than the structure of power in the technological era. When an individual simultaneously controls a satellite infrastructure used in armed conflicts, the world's primary information platform, and access to implantable neurological chips, the political categories inherited from the twentieth century seem insufficient to account for it. The real question is not whether Musk is a visionary or a threat. It is the question of the regulatory, democratic, and institutional mechanisms capable of applying to concentrations of power of this nature.
