In most organizations, meaning at work is treated as a supplement to the soul. It is taken up once the demands of performance are met, rarely before. In an age marked by information overload, the acceleration of technological change and a professional exhaustion grown chronic, it passes for a luxury. This hierarchy is a misreading. At the intersection of psychology, neuroscience, medicine, sociology and the organizational sciences, one conclusion imposes itself with rare convergence: meaning is not an incidental comfort, it is a fundamental psychological resource, a measurable lever of performance and a determinant of physical health and longevity.² ³ ⁴

Meaning does not replace well-designed work or systems that function; it enriches them. But its presence or absence is not a matter of vague feeling. Two people may love or detest the same position, with the same tasks, depending on the value and significance they assign to them. Meaning and purpose are the products of a continuous process of construction, what researchers call sense-making, by which each person interprets his work and sustains his own motivation.¹

The argument holds in a single sentence: the need for meaning is fundamental to human functioning, it always has been, and it will not disappear. What changes today is that technological and organizational transformations weaken the markers that once gave meaning to work more naturally. Each person must now more actively connect his role, his efforts and his contribution to an intelligible purpose. An employee may see his occupation change several times within a few years, under the effect of restructurings, digital tools, automation or new expectations. This instability makes the construction of a durable professional identity more difficult. For organizations, to create the conditions of this interpretation ceases to be an option and becomes a decisive lever, at once psychological and structural.⁵ ⁶ ⁷

A constant need, shifting frames

The intellectual roots of the subject reach back to the Greece of the fourth century before our era. In the Nicomachean Ethics, Aristotle forges a concept that still structures contemporary research on well-being: eudaimonia, often wrongly translated as "happiness," and which refers more precisely to human flourishing.⁸ Where hedonic pleasure remains transitory and suspended on external stimuli, a reward, a bonus, a moment of comfort, eudaimonia rests on the realization of one's potential and on a life aligned with a purpose. Work may be demanding, even grueling, and remain a source of lasting well-being so long as it is perceived as meaningful. The work of Carol Ryff, in 1989, formalized this distinction by showing that eudaimonic well-being, oriented toward meaning, constitutes a state inscribed in duration, founded on growth, autonomy and contribution, irreducible to momentary pleasure.⁹

The relationship between work and meaning has continually redefined itself across history, even as the human need remained constant. In ancient societies, work and purpose were inscribed within stable collective frameworks; Aristotle held that human activities had to serve a telos, a purpose tied to the flourishing of the community. In the Middle Ages and then with the Reformation, work becomes a vocation: Martin Luther advances the idea that the ordinary task can carry a spiritual significance, an intuition that Max Weber would later connect to the structure of modern capitalism.¹⁰ Meaning, then, was largely transmitted by relatively fixed religious, social and cultural structures.

Industrialization introduces a rupture. The alienation described by Marx severs work from identity, from the collective and at times from its concrete result. The task becomes fragmented, standardized, impersonal. From then on, meaning can no longer simply be inherited from stable structures: it must be interpreted and rebuilt by individuals themselves. The upheavals of the twentieth century accentuate this shift. In Man's Search for Meaning, in 1946, Viktor Frankl shows that even in extreme adversity and the loss of markers, the capacity to construct meaning remains a fundamental resource.¹¹ Meaning ceases to be a bequest of society and becomes an active process of psychological construction. From the 1970s on, organizational research takes hold of the mechanisms by which work does, or does not, produce significance: the job characteristics model of Hackman and Oldham establishes in 1976 that certain structural dimensions foster this feeling;¹² Amy Wrzesniewski then shows that individuals actively modify their relationship to their work in order to transform its meaning;¹³ Michael Steger, more recently, makes it measurable through validated instruments such as the WAMI.¹⁴

The need to construct meaning thus remains remarkably stable; it is the frameworks that allow it that change. For a long time, societies provided relatively fixed narratives for interpreting work. Contemporary mutations make them shifting. Meaning can no longer be regarded as a datum transmitted by the organization through a mission or values: it becomes a process of permanent interpretation and reconstruction. It is precisely this that places the responsibility of organizations at the center.

What the brain makes of meaning

The human brain is goal-oriented. It is made to assign value, anticipate outcomes and sustain effort when it judges that its actions are connected to something that matters. Meaning engages the systems of motivation, reward and future-oriented behavior, in particular the prefrontal cortex, the anterior cingulate cortex and the dopaminergic circuits.¹⁵ When an individual connects a present effort to a future outcome he values, this link reduces cognitive load and makes sustained effort more bearable. Purpose produces measurable effects on persistence, attention and resilience to stress, which it modulates directly: people endowed with a clear sense of meaning present more regulated cortisol profiles and a more stable nervous system.³ ⁴

This capacity for construction is not a fixed trait. Wrzesniewski shows that orientation to work varies with the relational and cognitive context; Weick, that the aptitude for interpretation sharpens with experience and feedback; self-determination theory, that it strengthens with autonomy and connection to others, and atrophies in their absence. It develops when the conditions are favorable and weakens when they are lacking, which makes the design of work directly decisive.

The most robust framework for understanding intrinsic motivation remains self-determination theory, developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan in 1985, which identifies three universal psychological needs.¹⁶ Autonomy is the experience of choice and will, the feeling that one's actions are decided rather than imposed. Competence is the sense of efficacy and progression, without which effort is soon perceived as pointless. Relatedness is the experience of a real connection to others and of a contribution to a community, well beyond mere conviviality. These three needs directly govern the construction of meaning: autonomy makes it possible to interpret and adjust one's work, competence to perceive its impact, relatedness to feel its concrete reach.

When these conditions are met and the activity presents a calibrated level of challenge, the individual can enter the state that Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi described, in 1990, under the name of flow: a total absorption, the erasure of self-consciousness, the sensation that time is suspended.¹⁷ Flow corresponds to optimal performance and, paradoxically, to a deep form of restoration: one emerges from it more energetic than before. It is fostered by the structure of work, clear goals, a calibrated challenge, immediate feedback and sufficient autonomy.

A matter of health, not comfort

Purpose is not content with subjective effects: its absence or presence has measurable consequences for physical health, and this is medically documented. Several large-scale studies have linked a strong sense of purpose to a reduced risk of serious illness. Boyle and colleagues showed in 2010 that people with a high level of meaning presented a significantly reduced risk of developing Alzheimer's disease and mild cognitive impairment.³ The work of Kim and his team, between 2013 and 2019, establishes links with a lower incidence of strokes, a better uptake of preventive behaviors, a fall in all-cause mortality and in cardiovascular events.¹⁸ ¹⁹ ²⁰ The mechanisms run through neuroendocrine regulation, notably cortisol and inflammatory markers, through the autonomic nervous system and through behaviors themselves. Meaning lastingly modifies the way the body responds to stress.

For organizations, this shifts the frame of analysis. Wellness programs that treat the symptoms of stress without addressing the absence of meaning are at best incomplete, at worst costly responses to a poorly posed problem. At this scale, the effects of meaning on health confirm that creating its conditions is a structural responsibility, and not a comfort measure.

How meaning is constructed

The framework proposed by Amy Wrzesniewski in 1997 distinguishes three ways of relating to one's work: as a job, a career or a calling.²¹ The "job" orientation views work as a transaction: one carries out the tasks in exchange for pay, keeping a psychological distance. The "career" orientation banks on advancement, prestige and progression, an investment directed toward one's own trajectory. The "calling" orientation experiences work as socially useful and personally meaningful, as an expression of one's identity; Dik and Duffy showed that those who recognize themselves in it would go on doing a similar kind of work even if the financial incentives were to diminish.²² The model is descriptive and does not rank these orientations. Above all, Wrzesniewski establishes that they depend on the cognitive and relational context, not on the occupation practiced: two people with identical tasks, in the same organization, may experience them quite differently.²¹ The orientation, and therefore the experience of meaning, is evolving.

Job crafting describes the active process by which an individual transforms his role in order to increase its meaning, engagement and fit with his skills, without waiting for a reorganization handed down from above.¹³ It confirms that the construction of meaning responds to the conditions in which it is exercised, and that one can support someone in transforming his relationship to work when the context allows.

Adam Grant provided a striking demonstration of this. His argument is that prosocial motivation, the awareness that one's work benefits others, ranks among the most powerful drivers of sustainable performance.²³ With call-center operators tasked with raising university funds, a repetitive activity marked by a high rate of refusal, he arranged a five-minute meeting between the operators and a student who had benefited from the scholarships funded. Productivity, time spent on the phone and the amounts raised increased sharply, with no intervention on pay, training or management.²⁴ The only lever was the perception of human impact. Meaning can thus be activated by simple adjustments, upstream of any transformation of the position. Making visible who benefits from the work, and how, sometimes suffices to change the relationship to the same tasks. This visibility is itself a form of feedback, and feedback is at the heart of the construction of meaning.

Performance, and the paradox

The stake is not only psychological. The stakeholder theory of Edward Freeman, in 1984, contests the exclusive primacy of shareholders by recalling the firm's obligations toward its employees, its customers, its suppliers and society.²⁵ The concept of shared value creation, from Porter and Kramer, shows that solving societal problems can become a driver of innovation and a competitive advantage.²⁶ The empirical analyses of George Serafeim bring solid evidence: organizations whose employees are aligned around a purpose post better financial performance.²⁷ The 2025 Gallup report associates meaningful work with higher engagement, better retention and better results on most indicators, and convergent conclusions emerge from the work of McKinsey and of Harvard.²⁸ ²⁹

There remains a paradox that this convergence makes glaring. While philosophy, psychology, medicine and economics concur, a large part of employees perceives no meaning in their daily work. The gap between what the data show and what organizations live remains considerable. The job demands-resources model, formulated by Bakker and Demerouti, helps to understand it: it distinguishes demands, which call for sustained effort and generate physical and psychological costs, from resources, which make it possible to reach goals, cushion the demands and foster development.³⁰ Meaning belongs to these resources. When demands are high and resources sufficient, engagement holds; when demands remain high and resources are lacking, exhaustion sets in.

Feedback occupies a central place here, less as a steering tool than as the primary mechanism of the construction of meaning over time. It links action to its effects, and these effects to meaning. Weick showed that meaning is continually rebuilt through a cycle in which one acts, observes the consequences and adjusts one's understanding; deprived of information on the effects of one's actions, this cycle breaks off.¹ Hackman and Oldham make feedback a major determinant of the motivating potential of a position, whose absence strongly weakens the other dimensions.¹² Recent work shows that those who actively seek feedback perceive more meaning and display more initiative.³¹ Rare or low-quality feedback arrangements therefore constitute a structural obstacle. The quality of the interactions in which work is discussed and interpreted counts just as much: to be able to express one's experience, to be heard and recognized is a condition of motivational functioning, which self-determination theory and the work on perceived organizational support confirm. The quality of managerial and collective conversations thus becomes a direct lever: asking the right questions, reformulating, making interpretations explicit.

The responsibility of organizations

From this emerges an organization that takes on the task of creating the conditions of an active construction of meaning. This goes first through the job-crafting conversation. The work of Wrzesniewski and Dutton shows that with structured support, each person can identify the tasks that seem to him the most meaningful, connect them to his values and adjust the way he approaches his role.¹³ These exchanges find their place in regular reviews, provided they shift the focus from the tracking of objectives toward the understanding of what gives value to the work, starting from simple questions: which tasks give energy, which contributions seem the most useful, which aspects of the work accord best with the person's values.

It goes next through the visibility of impact. Grant's results establish that making perceptible the real effect of work on concrete people can transform the relationship to the same tasks.²⁴ Testimonies from beneficiaries, the reporting back of a project's effects, indicators of human impact in team reviews: the essential thing is to make it a regular practice, capable of nourishing the interpretive cycle described by Weick, rather than a one-off gesture.¹

It supposes, finally, collective times devoted to meaning, distinct from operational meetings, in which teams reconnect their work to a larger purpose, confront their interpretations and rebuild a shared coherence, particularly in phases of transformation. These moments call for no complex arrangements, but a clear intention and a regularity sufficient for the construction of meaning to become a habit rather than an exception. Where work evolves fast, these conditions form a durable competitive advantage: when employees know how to construct meaning themselves, they reorient in the face of change without waiting for the organization to redefine their role in their place.

The need for meaning is fundamental to human functioning; it always has been. The convergence of work from philosophy, psychology, neuroscience and medicine establishes that it weighs, in a measurable and lasting way, on cognitive functioning, health, motivation and performance. What changes is the context. The frameworks that once gave meaning to work, stable roles, linear paths, shared collective markers, are weakening under the effect of technological and organizational transformations. Meaning must now be constructed in a more active, more continuous and more individual way. Organizations that confine themselves to communicating a mission or values answer only part of the need; those that create the conditions of an active construction answer it fully. The need itself will not change. What can change is the place we choose to grant it.
