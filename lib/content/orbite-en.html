There is something vertiginous about looking at the night sky today. Those luminous points crossing the darkness in orderly procession are not stars. They are satellites, thousands, soon tens of thousands, silently redrawing the geography of global power. Low Earth orbit has become the new territory to conquer. And like all strategic territories before it, the battle is to occupy it before the rules exist.

This battle has a name in diplomatic and military circles: the race for mega-constellations. For now it pits two blocs, American and Chinese, against each other, with a Europe watching from the sidelines struggling to find its place. But behind the satellite launch figures and stock market valuations lies a far deeper question: who owns orbit? And what happens when it is full?

A resource that no one has declared finite

Low Earth orbit, between 500 and 2,000 kilometers in altitude, is not infinite. The radio frequencies needed for satellite communications are allocated by the International Telecommunication Union according to a simple and brutal principle: first come, first served. Whoever files their orbital coordinates first obtains priority. Latecomers must coordinate with existing occupants, or stand aside.

SpaceX understood this before anyone else. Starlink has deployed over 6,000 satellites since 2019, with authorization to launch up to 42,000. These figures are not merely commercial ambitions: they are a strategy of territorial occupation. By saturating certain frequency bands and certain orbital planes, Starlink mechanically makes any competing constellation harder and more expensive to establish.

"We are experiencing the same thing as during the age of maritime discovery. The powers that control the routes control trade, war, and diplomacy. Today those routes are in the sky."

Bleddyn Bowen, King's College London

The maritime metaphor says plainly: we are not in a technological debate. We are in a struggle for critical infrastructure, as decisive as the commercial routes of the fifteenth century, the telegraph cables of the nineteenth, or the oil of the twentieth.

The Chinese response

Beijing did not stand by and watch. In 2021, China officially launched Guowang, literally "national network," a state-planned constellation of 13,000 satellites, operated by China SatNet, a state enterprise created for the purpose. The launch cadence is accelerating. The objective is not only to rival Starlink on the civilian communications market: it is to guarantee China total strategic autonomy in the event of conflict.

For the war in Ukraine revealed something military staffs around the world have noted: Starlink has become a frontline military infrastructure. Ukrainian drones navigate via Starlink. Field communications run via Starlink. And on several occasions, Elon Musk has made unilateral decisions on activating or deactivating certain coverage zones, decisions with direct operational consequences on the battlefield.

No serious country can tolerate that its war-fighting capacity depends on the goodwill of a private American entrepreneur. China has drawn the logical conclusion: it must possess its own constellation, sovereign and unconditional.

In parallel, Beijing is developing Qianfan, an even more ambitious commercial constellation backed by Shanghai, targeting 14,000 additional satellites. China will not build one alternative to Starlink. It will build several.

Europe, absent at the decisive moment

History may record that Europe missed the orbital turn as it missed the digital platform turn in the 2000s. The reasons are the same: dispersed efforts, political timelines incompatible with industrial speed, reluctance to fund massively what does not pay off immediately.

OneWeb, the British constellation acquired after bankruptcy by Eutelsat, struggles to find its model. IRIS², the European sovereign constellation project, is accumulating delays. The ambition is there; the budgets are insufficient; and meanwhile orbit is filling up.

The result is paradoxical: the European Union, which has produced some of the world's most ambitious digital regulations, GDPR, DSA, AI Act, finds itself dependent on satellite infrastructures it does not control. In the event of a serious crisis with the United States, what sovereign access to space communications would Europe actually have?

Today, the answer is uncomfortable.

The Global South held hostage

This is where the question becomes political: in Global South countries. Entire regions of sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and the island Pacific saw Starlink arrive as a revolution: high-speed connectivity where no cable had ever been laid, no fiber deployed, no national operator capable of investing.

This is real. The benefits are real. But they come with a structural dependence that no one names frankly: these countries are entrusting their national communications infrastructure to a private American company, without service continuity guarantees, without bargaining power, and without a credible alternative in the near term.

China has understood this and makes it a diplomatic argument. In several African countries, discussions are underway to deploy Chinese satellite solutions under the Digital Silk Road. The message is clear: you do not have to depend on Washington. You can depend on Beijing. For states that have lived decades of Western conditionality, the offer has a particular resonance, even if it reproduces exactly the same logic of dependence.

No one is offering them a third way. An orbital infrastructure that is neither American nor Chinese. This gap may be the greatest missed opportunity of twenty-first-century space diplomacy.

The Kessler syndrome, or the threat hanging over everyone

In 1978, astrophysicist Donald Kessler described a scenario no one wanted to contemplate: if the density of objects in low orbit became too high, one collision could trigger another, then another, in an uncontrollable chain reaction. The debris generated would render certain orbits unusable for decades, even centuries. No more communications satellites. No more GPS. No more space weather forecasting. Much of the invisible infrastructure on which contemporary civilization rests, destroyed.

This is no longer science fiction. With tens of thousands of satellites planned by multiple competing actors, with thousands of existing debris pieces already in orbit, and without a binding international treaty on orbital traffic management, the Kessler risk has moved from the status of theoretical curiosity to that of a genuine operational concern.

The International Space Station regularly performs avoidance maneuvers. Collisions have already occurred. And each collision generates thousands of new fragments too small to track, too fast to avoid.

A war without declaration

What unfolds in low orbit is not yet a war. There have been no shots fired, no deliberate destruction of adversary satellites, at least not recently. But the anti-satellite tests conducted by Russia in 2021, by China as early as 2007, by India in 2019, say unambiguously that this option is on the table.

Meanwhile, the militarization of civil space advances. Starlink provides military communications in Ukraine. Commercial observation satellites feed the intelligence of several armies. The boundary between civilian infrastructure and instrument of war has never been more porous.

International space law, for its part, dates from 1967. The Outer Space Treaty was designed for a bipolar world with two state space powers. It does not anticipate private mega-constellations, dual-use satellites, or hybrid warfare by satellite proxy.

We are using twentieth-century rules to arbitrate twenty-first-century conflicts. The gap between the two grows every day.

What remains to be decided

The question is not whether Starlink is useful — it is. Nor whether China is wrong to want orbital autonomy — it is not. The question is whether humanity will allow Earth's orbit to be colonized by private and national interests without ever collectively deciding what it wants to do with it.

Space is, according to the 1967 Treaty, "the province of all mankind." It is a beautiful formulation. It does not long survive the reality of an orbital market functioning like all markets: those who arrive first take what they can, and the others adapt.

While diplomats debate, rockets lift off. Orbital slots fill. And what could have been a common heritage of humanity becomes, silently, the terrain of a competition that no one has officially declared open.

The sky belongs to those who settle in it. For now, they are the ones writing the rules.
