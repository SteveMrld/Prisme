On Sunday January 4, 2026, Nicolás Maduro woke in a cell at the Metropolitan Detention Center in Brooklyn. During the night, American special forces had burst through the reinforced doors of his residence at the heart of the Fuerte Tiuna military complex in Caracas. Operation "Absolute Resolve" had just overthrown a sovereign head of state without a declaration of war, without a UN mandate, without even the approval of the US Congress.¹

The European reaction to this unprecedented event since the invasion of Panama in 1989 deserves examination. Emmanuel Macron expressed satisfaction that "the Venezuelan people has been rid of the dictatorship of Nicolás Maduro." Friedrich Merz acknowledged that "the legal assessment of the American intervention is complex" while noting that Maduro had "driven his country to ruin." Keir Starmer recalled his attachment to international law, while clarifying that London would shed no tears over the end of the Maduro regime.²

In these reactions, what strikes is less the tepidness than the implicit logic. Europe accepts, in watermark, the idea that the United States possesses a moral right over its hemisphere. That the American neighborhood constitutes a natural zone of influence where Washington can act according to its own rules. The "Trump Corollary" to the Monroe Doctrine, formalized in the National Security Strategy of December 2025, thus becomes a diplomatic reality that Europe absorbs without contesting it.

France is no longer a great power in the classical sense, not yet an assumed regional power. It occupies an uncomfortable in-between.

Meanwhile, negotiations on Ukraine are advancing toward what increasingly resembles a de facto partition. Zelensky now speaks of demilitarized zones in the Donbas. The peace plan carried by the Trump administration envisages de facto recognition of Russian control over annexed territories, the lifting of sanctions, and Russia's return to the G8. Ukraine renounces NATO in exchange for security guarantees whose real solidity no one yet knows. Putin, who has refused any ceasefire without a global agreement, appears in a position to dictate the terms of peace.³

Meanwhile, off Australia, three Chinese warships conducted live-fire exercises on a heavily traveled flight path linking Australia and New Zealand, forcing forty-nine commercial flights to divert without sufficient warning.⁴ The Cook Islands have just signed a "2025-2030 Action Plan" for comprehensive strategic partnership with Beijing, including maritime surveillance, police training, and cybersecurity cooperation. The Solomon Islands have already hosted Chinese security personnel since the controversial 2022 agreement.⁵

A pattern is taking shape. Call it, for lack of a better term, Yalta 2.0.

Geography as destiny

The Americans are asserting their grip on the Western Hemisphere. Greenland has been under constant pressure for a year, to the point that Denmark has recalled its chargé d'affaires from Washington and increased its Arctic defense budget by $6.6 billion. Trump has named a special envoy whose declared mission is to "make Greenland part of the United States." He does not exclude the use of military force. Venezuela has just demonstrated that these threats are not rhetoric.⁶

The Russians are consolidating their grip on eastern Ukraine and Crimea, with the de facto blessing of an American administration that considers "95% of the problems resolved" and that territorial questions are a matter of detail.

There remains China. And it is there that the question becomes vertiginous for France.

France possesses three territories in the Pacific: New Caledonia, Wallis-et-Futuna, and French Polynesia. Together, they represent nearly 7 million square kilometers of Exclusive Economic Zone, representing 40% of France's total EEZ. French Polynesia alone possesses the largest EEZ in the Pacific at 4.8 million square kilometers, more than Japan. This immensity makes France the world's second maritime power.⁷

Consider a map. New Caledonia is 1,200 kilometers from Australia, in the heart of Melanesia, in the zone where China is multiplying security agreements with the Solomon Islands, Vanuatu, and Fiji. Nouméa is 7,700 kilometers from Shanghai. And 17,000 kilometers from Paris. These territories are in China's oceanic neighborhood, not Europe's.

Polynesia is more distant, lost in the middle of the Pacific, nearly 11,000 kilometers from Shanghai and from Paris alike. But this isolation protects it from nothing. For French Polynesia consists of 118 islands scattered across a space equivalent to the surface area of Europe. If one superimposes the maps placing Tahiti over Paris, the Marquesas end up in Sweden, the Australs in Sicily, the Gambiers in Romania. Truly securing such a space would require considerable means: an oceanic fleet, permanent aerial and satellite surveillance capabilities. We have none of this.

The Venezuelan precedent as signal

The geographical argument Trump uses for Greenland, China could replicate for New Caledonia. "This island is in our strategic neighborhood. It is more than twice as close to our shores as to Paris. By what legitimacy does France exercise sovereignty there?" ASPI, the Australian strategic think tank, documented in a 2024 report the interference activities of the Chinese Communist Party in New Caledonia: targeting of political and economic elites, use of the diaspora, instrumentalization of Chinese companies.⁸

New Caledonia holds approximately 25% of known global nickel reserves, a critical mineral for electric batteries. China already refines 70% of global nickel.⁹ An independent New Caledonia, indebted, weakened by the 2024 riots, could become a natural client for Chinese investment. The analogy with the debt trap deployed elsewhere in the Pacific is not fiction: Tonga, Fiji, and Vanuatu are already experiencing it.

If Europe accepts that Americans can capture Maduro because Venezuela is "in their backyard," if it accepts that Putin retains the Donbas because it is "his near abroad," on what basis would it refuse Xi Jinping the right to consider the Pacific his natural sphere of influence?

International law? We have just seen what remains of it when a great power decides to ignore it.

Historical legitimacy? The Chinese could recall that Zheng He was sailing these waters in the fifteenth century, long before Louis-Antoine de Bougainville landed in Tahiti.

France's solitude in the oceans

There is a cruel irony in our situation. France is the only European country with a significant military presence in the Indo-Pacific. Our bases at Nouméa and Papeete, our surveillance frigates, our Falcon Gardian aircraft patrolling these oceanic immensities constitute a major strategic asset. But this asset is only worth something if we are prepared to defend it.

What would we do if Beijing decided tomorrow to contest our presence? If Chinese "fishermen" multiplied incursions into our waters? If propaganda hammered that France is a colonial power that has no business in the Pacific? If bilateral agreements with our Oceanic neighbors progressively isolated us?

We could not count on the Americans. Trump has just demonstrated that his priorities are hemispheric. In a global bargain with Beijing, French interests in the Pacific would carry little weight. We could not count on Europe. Brussels has never considered French overseas territories as a European stake. The Union has neither doctrine nor means to project power 20,000 kilometers from its borders. As for NATO, let us not even think of it: Article 5 only covers territories north of the Tropic of Cancer. The Pacific, the Antilles, French Guiana do not fall within its perimeter.

We would be alone. As we already are, in a sense.

And there is more. For if the Monroe Doctrine in the Trump version applies to the Western Hemisphere as a whole, what of Martinique, Guadeloupe, French Guiana? These French territories lie in the heart of the Caribbean and South America. Martinique is equidistant from the coasts of Venezuela and Haiti. French Guiana shares its borders with Brazil and Suriname, thousands of kilometers from metropolitan France. By what logic would the Americans indefinitely accept a European presence in what they consider their zone of exclusive influence?

Nuclear deterrence and its limits

Against determined Chinese pressure, we would have only one possible response: nuclear deterrence. This is where, at bottom, the real question lies. Would France activate the atomic weapon to defend New Caledonia? Would it trigger nuclear war with Beijing over Wallis-et-Futuna?

Let us pose the question differently, with the brutality it deserves. Which President of the Republic, current or future, would risk a Chinese nuclear riposte on Paris, Lyon, or Marseille to retain Nouméa? Which leader would explain to metropolitan French citizens that they must accept death for distant territories that, whatever one says, do not hold the same value in their eyes as Strasbourg or Nice? France possesses a maritime empire, but it has never cultivated in its citizens the feeling of belonging to it.

I am among those who think the answer is no. That no French political leader, faced with this choice, would press the button. That nuclear deterrence, designed to protect metropolitan territory from a Soviet invasion, is not credible when it comes to defending islands located 17,000 kilometers from the Hexagon. And if deterrence is not credible, it deters nothing.

◆

The capture of Maduro is not merely a Venezuelan affair. It is the symptom of an epochal shift. The world of common rules, of equal sovereignty of nations, of international law as a regulator of conflicts — that world is dying. It is being replaced by another, older, more brutal: that of spheres of influence, of power relations, of empires dividing the map as at the Congress of Vienna.

In that world, France has a mortal weakness. It possesses territories it cannot defend alone against a determined great power. It enjoys a theoretical maritime power that its demography, its economy, and its political class cannot sustain.

What is the world's second EEZ worth when one has only a few patrol vessels and a handful of aging surveillance aircraft to cover 7 million square kilometers? The Americans possess the world's first EEZ. But they have the means to defend it. The US Navy fields 290 combat vessels, including 11 nuclear aircraft carriers. France, to watch over an almost equivalent EEZ, has a total national Navy of 120 vessels, only a handful of which are assigned to the Pacific.¹⁰

It is time to look at the world as it is, not as we would wish it to be. The Americans want Greenland and consider Latin America their backyard. The Russians intend to reconquer the lost territories of the former Soviet Union. The Chinese regard Taiwan as a rebel province and the Pacific as their natural sphere of influence. Each will take what it can take.

The only question that now matters is this: what is France prepared to do to remain a world power? And if the answer is "not much," then one might as well look the truth in the face. In the world of empires, the weak lose their possessions. This is a law as old as history. We have simply forgotten that it applies to us as well.

Key takeaways
01

The Venezuela operation is not an anomaly; it is the formalization of a world order in which great powers act freely within their sphere of influence, and the rest of the world watches.

02

France possesses territories in three oceans, without a coherent doctrine for defending or developing them. This is a major strategic vulnerability in a world that is becoming imperial again.

03

International law only protects states powerful enough to impose its respect on others. For the rest, it was a hope. That hope is fading.
