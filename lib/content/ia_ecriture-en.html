Open Albert Camus. The Stranger, first sentence: "Today, Maman died. Or maybe yesterday, I don't know." Two sentences. Twenty words. In twenty words, Camus establishes a narrator incapable of feeling grief for his own mother, an indifference to time that foreshadows the entire novel, and an emotional dissonance that will unsettle the reader to the very last page. Grammatically, the sentence is flawed; the temporal uncertainty is absurd. Literarily, it is irreplaceable. It is precisely because it is absurd that it is true.1

No generative artificial intelligence system would have produced that sentence. Not because it lacks command of French. But because that sentence does not come from a rule. It comes from a vision, a way of seeing the world that is Camus's and no one else's, built in the pain of colonial Algeria, in the experience of the absurd, in years of work on what had to be said and how.

What AI actually does

Generative writing systems work through statistical prediction. They learn, from colossal volumes of human texts, which words tend to follow which others in which contexts. They then produce sequences of words that resemble what a human might have written, because they are statistically close to what humans have written.2

This definition is not a criticism. It is simply a description. And it makes it possible to understand what these systems structurally produce: the probable. The expected sentence. Text that does not shock because it is the average of what has already been said. This is why AI-generated texts often have that particular texture, correct, fluid, without rough edges, that ends up sounding hollow. Not because they are bad. Because they are too good in a certain sense: too smooth, too predictable, too conforming to what the reader already expected.

Great literature does not say what the reader expected. It says what the reader was feeling without knowing how to formulate it.

Césaire and the untranslatable

Take Aimé Césaire. In Notebook of a Return to the Native Land, he writes:

"And the Antilles will dissolve in water, the sea, a swirl of sea, a burst of sea, a howl of sea..." Aimé Césaire, Notebook of a Return to the Native Land, 1939

The repetition is not an error. It is the very structure of the cry. Césaire uses anaphora like a drum; each "sea" strikes harder than the previous one, until the sea itself becomes a character, a force, a collective trauma. This is not a stylistic device applied to pre-existing content. It is the content itself that imposes its form.

An AI system trained to "write like Césaire" could produce repetitions, anaphoras, a lyrical register. What it cannot produce is the urgency that makes these repetitions necessary, because that urgency comes from a specific place: the experience of a man born in Martinique under French colonization, who lived in his very body the cultural and linguistic dispossession, and who chose to turn the language of the colonizer against itself with its own incandescent power. That biography is not an anecdote. It is the condition of possibility of the text.3

What writing does to the brain

The neuroscience of writing brings complementary insight. Writing by hand or formulating one's own thoughts in words coordinates several brain regions: Broca's area for language production, the hippocampus for memory, the prefrontal cortex for planning and word selection, the motor cortex for the gesture. This coordination is itself a form of thinking. One does not transcribe a pre-existing thought; one thinks by writing.4

To delegate writing to an algorithm is therefore to delegate part of the cognitive process itself. Not the ideas, which remain the author's, but the work of formulating them, of confronting them with the resistances of language, of refining them through that friction. This work is not a luxury or an archaism. It is where thought becomes precise, where blind spots reveal themselves, where the author discovers what they truly thought while searching for how to say it.

Uniformity as impoverishment

French literature owes its richness to the radical singularity of its voices. Hugo does not write like Flaubert. Colette does not write like Duras. Maupassant does not write like Zola. Each has their own way of constructing a sentence, choosing one word over another, placing a silence where another would put a comma. This diversity is not merely aesthetic; it is epistemic. Each voice sees the world in a way the other voices do not see, and literature is the ensemble of these accumulated and transmitted ways of seeing.

What generative AI structurally threatens is this diversity. Not because it will prohibit writing. But because it produces the probable, text that resembles the average of everything that has been written. And if the texts we exchange, read, and produce converge toward that average, something precious and irreplaceable is lost: the irreducible singularity of each gaze on the world.

Perhaps one day, with melancholic irony, this will be called the era of artificial genius, an age when technical competence reached its summit and true originality became the rarest thing of all.

What remains to be defended

Technology is not the enemy of writing. It has always been its companion, from Gutenberg's printing press to word processing. Each tool has transformed practices without erasing what is essential. What is at stake today is not the survival of literature; great works will continue to exist as long as there are human beings with biographies, pains, and singular ways of formulating them. What is at stake is the ordinary use of writing, those messages, letters, and everyday texts that, when they truly come from someone, bear the mark of that someone.

The authenticity of imperfections is infinitely more precious than the cold perfection of uniformity. A sentence that stumbles because its author was searching for words contains something no algorithm can generate: the trace of a presence. That of someone who wanted, perhaps clumsily, to say something that belonged to them.

This is what literature has always preserved, across centuries and successive tools. And this is what deserves to be protected, not against technology, but despite it, and through awareness of what it does not replace.
