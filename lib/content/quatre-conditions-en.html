My father arrived in Los Angeles in 1985. He worked in gas stations. He sold fruit from a truck, what today would be called a food truck. It was, in fact, his first American business. And thanks to that venture, he saved obsessively.

Nearly ten years later, he bought a house and built a car-rental company aimed at long-stay visitors trying to survive in a city without public transit. He had spotted the niche because he had lived the problem himself.

Looking back, I understand that my father's story was not simply made of courage and sacrifice. He had had the luck to arrive in a society that made four things possible: to work at once, to reach capital, to build a business despite the barriers of language and bureaucracy, and to exist in a gaze ready to see potential before proof. When he arrived, he held only a secondary-school diploma.

This last condition, the gaze, is the one no one talks about. Afghans in the America of the 1980s were seen through the prism of the Cold War, not through that of migratory panic. Attitudes were more open to him than to other minority groups. My father knew this owed more to luck than to justice.

Four conditions. That is what it took.

I think of this each time I meet founders like Rahaf Allymoni.

Rahaf fled Syria at the start of the war. She earned a master's degree in Spain while her fiancé spent all his savings to be smuggled into the Netherlands. No legal visa allowed them to live in the same country. They were reunited for the first time at the gates of a refugee camp in Amsterdam.

With the support of Forward·Inc, they built together Daffee, a caffeine-free coffee alternative made from recycled date pits, now funded by Rabobank and WingInvest.

The company is real. The investors are real. The obstacle was Europe.

Or Raafat Hantoush, a Syrian data scientist in Berlin, who designed Bote, a connected insole born of a childhood memory, that of having got lost in a market. He had the engineering, the prototype, a verbal agreement with one of the largest German geolocation services. What he lacked was the invisible infrastructure that local founders inherit at birth: networks of investors, warm introductions, mentors who call back. He found his way thanks to SINGA, because the mainstream ecosystem had not been built for him.

Or Khaled Shaaban, who founded Subul from an asylum-seekers' center in The Hague, a technology-outsourcing company that connects skilled refugees to the global digital economy, and solves the labor shortages that European employers say they cannot fill.

These founders are not exceptional because they are migrants. They are exceptional because Europe set before them obstacles that most local founders never see.

As of today, 2.2 million entrepreneurs born outside the European Union are already active in the single market, each creating on average 0.2 additional jobs in the host economy. Mixed founding teams are 44 percent larger and 35 percentage points more likely to raise funds in their first three years than teams made up solely of natives. Europe does not have a shortage of entrepreneurial talent. It has a shortage of infrastructure for those who are already here.

It is in this context that the European Commission published, on 18 March 2026, its EU Inc. proposal: to register a company in forty-eight hours, for 100 euros, with no minimum capital, no notary, a single legal structure across the twenty-seven member states. EU Inc. solves one of the four conditions, the one that consists in building a business without drowning in fragmentation. The Commission's own estimates put the administrative savings at between 328 and 440 million euros. It is real and it is welcome.

But one condition is not enough. EU Inc. leaves migrant founders hanging on the other three.

The right of residence

EU Inc. allows non-European nationals to register a company in Europe, but not to live there to run it. For a founder, company law and immigration law are inseparable: one does not build a business in a country where one has no right to reside. Yet the proposal treats these two domains as entirely separate.

Where startup visas exist, they remain fragmented across twenty-seven national systems, with different definitions of innovation, distinct financial thresholds, and unequal mobility rights. A founder holding a Danish startup visa cannot operate in France. A visa granted in Estonia has no value in Germany. The single market stops at the immigration office.

Access to capital

Research conducted in seven European countries has brought to light persistent gaps in the granting of bank loans, correlated with the perceived ethnic origin of applicants. Migrant founders move within systems statistically less inclined to trust them. There is also a quieter barrier: EU Inc. relies on national electronic identifiers, which founders cannot obtain without a residence permit. Most of the organizations supporting migrant entrepreneurs cite access to capital as one of the principal obstacles.

The gaze

This one cannot be legislated. But it shapes everything else. Migrant founders are still treated as risks or as case files, not as entrepreneurs. This perception determines who gets meetings, funding, and second chances.

The legislative agreement on EU Inc. is expected by the end of 2026. Four things must happen before that window closes.

EU Inc. must resist the temptation to confine itself to high-growth startups alone. The European fabric of SMEs employs 90 million people across 26.1 million companies. If EU Inc. becomes a vehicle reserved for future unicorns, it will miss the economy that actually exists. This narrowing is already under way in the regulation: the simplified insolvency procedure is reserved for "innovative startups," a definition that excludes other companies vital to the European economy. For a migrant founder, this exclusion sustains vulnerability: if his company fails, a costly exit puts an end to the story. A clear insolvency procedure is what makes risk-taking possible. It must apply to all EU Inc. companies.

Europe needs a genuine founder visa, renewable, tied to a real activity, without national quotas, and allowing the family to come from the first day. A founder digital identity must be built into the eIDAS 2.0 framework now, not bolted on after the fact.

The recitals of the regulation must explicitly reference the European Agenda for Inclusive Entrepreneurship. This is nothing symbolic. The recitals determine how the authorities, the courts, and the Commission will interpret what follows. Without this reference, the accompanying measures that would make EU Inc. usable in practice, for Rahaf, for Raafat, for Khaled, will have no legal anchor on which to rest.

But EU Inc. is neither the first nor the last moment when Europe will face this question. The four conditions I have listed existed before this regulation and will outlive it. They will apply to the next legislative wave, and to the one after. As long as a foreign founder must prove his legitimacy before existing, as long as bank counters read his nationality before his project, as long as the public gaze sees him as a case file before seeing him as an entrepreneur, the problem will not be solved.

My father had the four conditions not because the system was just, but because the America of that time saw in him a possibility rather than a threat.

Rahaf, Raafat, and Khaled are more educated, more connected to the world, and technically more sophisticated than he was forty years ago. What they lack are the conditions he had.

The founders are already here. The question is whether Europe will build a system that lets them build too.
