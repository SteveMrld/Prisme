His name is Edgar Nahoum. He is born on July 8, 1921, at 10 rue Mayran in the 9th arrondissement of Paris, the son of Vidal Nahoum and Luna Beressi, Sephardic Jews from Salonika. It had been planned to give him the names of his two dead grandfathers. His parents, frightened by this morbid tradition, change their minds and rename him Edgar, a Frenchified first name referencing a figure admired in the family, Edgar Quinet, a major figure of republican socialism.

From the moment of entering the world, a lesson imposes itself: identities are negotiated, names carry battles, and nothing is simple.

He loses his mother at ten. He will say later that his entire body of work is inscribed in that bereavement. His first major book, published in 1951, is titled L'Homme et la mort (Man and Death). He is thirty years old.

Nahoum becomes Morin

His parents came from Jewish merchant families in Salonika who arrived in France in the early 1910s. His father ran a wholesale hosiery business in the Sentier district of Paris. Edgar grows up in the Paris of the 1930s, between the Sephardic heritage and the French Republic, between an immigrant community and a nation beginning to falter under the rise of fascism.

In 1940, he withdraws to Toulouse, where he makes connections with many left-wing intellectuals: Vladimir Jankélévitch, Georges Friedmann, Clara Malraux, Julien Benda, of whom he will serve as secretary for several months. It is in this intellectual ferment during wartime that a thought takes shape which will never cease connecting what others separate.

In 1943, he joins the communist Resistance, co-opted by André Ulmann into the Mouvement de résistance des prisonniers de guerre et des déportés. After the merger of his movement with that of François Mitterrand, he becomes one of the heads of the Paris committee, responsible in particular for printing and distributing clandestine tracts.

This is where Edgar Morin is born. As a member of the Resistance, he adopts the pseudonym Morin following a mix-up at a meeting in Toulouse: he had introduced himself as Edgar Manin, in reference to a character from Malraux's La Condition humaine. His comrades misheard and called him Morin. The name stuck.

This wartime name becomes his name for life. Edgar Nahoum officially disappears. But the man keeps everything. He describes himself as having a neo-Marrano identity: those Iberian Jews who traversed the centuries carrying multiple identities, never reducible to a single belonging. This is his entire philosophy in embryo: contradiction is not a defect of reality; it is its deep nature.

A saga "fabulous, magnificent, filthy, atrocious"

Having entered simultaneously, at twenty, into resistance and communism, Edgar Morin begins to doubt the latter from the Liberation onward, then through successive ruptures and disillusions, at the time of the trials and purges of the Stalinist "second glaciation," before mutual rejection in 1951. His membership in the Party had lasted ten years, during which he had seen how the Apparatus could transform a brave man into a coward, a hero into a monster, a martyr into an executioner.

He does not emerge silently. In 1959, he publishes Autocritique, a reflection on his adhesion to the Communist Party and on the dangers of ideology and self-blindness. The title is a radical hijacking: where the Party demanded self-criticisms to better crush its members, Morin appropriates the form to think freely. It is one of the first works in French intellectual history to examine so frankly how an intelligent man can believe in the absurd.

He will describe communism himself as "a fabulous, magnificent, filthy, atrocious, and farcical saga." No retrospective hatred, no convenient amnesia.

Heidegger in Freiburg

In the aftermath of the Liberation, as a lieutenant in the First French Army, he finds himself in occupied Germany. Having read fragments of Heidegger, he learns that the philosopher is living in Freiburg under restrictions because of his Nazi past. He decides to go see him. Heidegger and his wife are frightened to see a French officer arrive: they fear new prohibitions. They are surprised when Morin tells them: "I would like to talk with you."

A son of Sephardic Jewish immigrants, a lieutenant of the army that has just liberated Europe, knocks on the door of the philosopher compromised with Nazism. He comes to engage him in dialogue, not to punish him. Complex thinking is not an abstract method. Morin embodies it before theorizing it.

No one is a prophet

From the 1950s onward, Morin joins the CNRS and begins his traversal of disciplines. Cinema, sociology, anthropology, biology, philosophy: he refuses to choose. He holds no doctorate, only two undergraduate degrees, in history-geography and law. In a French academic system obsessed with credentials and schools of thought, this is almost a provocation. He nonetheless produces a body of work that will be studied in the greatest universities in the world.

The saying "no one is a prophet in their own country" applies to Morin in France with cruel precision. His contributions to education, political thinking, and ecology appear not to be recognized in this country commensurate with the acclaim he receives on a planetary scale. Less known in the English-speaking world due to the limited number of translations of his more than sixty books, he is celebrated in the French-speaking world, in Southern Europe, and in Latin America. His influence now extends as far as China and Japan. Everywhere except where decisions governing the world are made.

The South understood him before everyone else

Morin's rapprochement with Latin America dates from the 1960s, when "mestizo thought" and the great myths of pre-Columbian civilizations begin to fascinate him. He spends two years on the continent, teaches, observes, learns. In 1990s Brazil, groups reflecting on complex thinking form inside and outside universities, without academic France paying attention.

Why did the South understand Morin before the West? Because his work is received there as the adventure of a maverick seeking to connect scholarly knowledge with the legendary knowledge of lived experience. A laboratory of complexity par excellence, Latin America demonstrates that hierarchies of knowledge are power games in which the victors impose their mark. Morin himself suggests that it is in the South (crucible of mixing and integration of different cultural inheritances) that the seed of the reform of thought will germinate.

The man who invented the word of our era

In 1993, while chancelleries are celebrating the end of history and the definitive triumph of the liberal West, Morin publishes Terre-Patrie with Anne-Brigitte Kern. It is there that he introduces for the first time the term "polycrisis," a situation in which multiple environmental, social, economic, and political crises interconnect and mutually amplify one another.

No one listens. The word sleeps for thirty years.

It is the historian Adam Tooze who popularizes it at Davos in 2023 to describe the convergence of the pandemic, the war in Ukraine, and the energy and climate crises. The world then discovers that the term best describing our era had been forged thirty years earlier by a philosopher no one had deigned to translate into English. It was only in 2022 that his work became more accessible to the English-speaking world, with the publication of The Challenge of Complexity, which compiles 32 essays, some translated into English for the first time.

Marrakech, 2024

On February 10, 2024, before a packed room at the African Book Festival in Marrakech, his eyes bright and his voice sharp, the 102-year-old philosopher takes the floor. He denounces Israel's bombardments of Gaza, those carnages accompanied by persecutions in the West Bank that have reawakened the dormant Palestinian question. He deplores the world's silence in the face of what he calls "a horrible tragedy."

What makes this speech particularly significant is what he is: a son of Sephardic Jewish immigrants from Salonika, a resistance fighter against Nazism, who refuses in the very name of his own history to be silent in the face of the oppression of another people. This is not a betrayal. It is the same moral compass that has guided him since 1943: refusing Manichaeism, even when it costs.

"Is it not midnight in our century?"

In January 2024, in Le Monde, he revives the formulation of Victor Serge, published in 1939, the year of the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact: "Is it not midnight in our century?" He observes the war in Ukraine, the tightening of a Russia-China bloc, the Global South's increasing autonomy from the West, the wars in Gaza. He sees all of this not as separate crises but as manifestations of the same systemic disintegration: the polycrisis he had described thirty years earlier. It has arrived as he announced. He draws no satisfaction from this.

In 2024, he publishes Cheminer vers l'essentiel with Albin Michel. In 2025, Y a-t-il des leçons de l'Histoire? with Denoël. The title of this last book is a question. Not a reassuring affirmation. A question suspended in the air, posed by a man who has seen everything and who still doubts humanity's capacity to learn, never his own.

Honorary doctor of twenty-seven universities around the world, translated into dozens of languages, his work crosses the borders he always refused to draw between disciplines.

He was born Edgar Nahoum, son of Salonika. He became Morin in the Resistance. He traversed communism, anti-communism, structuralism, globalization, Covid, the wars of the twenty-first century, without ever yielding to simplification. At 103, he is still publishing.

Thought does not surrender. It waits for the world to be ready.
