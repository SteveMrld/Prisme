It is undeniable that technology in the service of human inventiveness has brought immeasurable benefits to our lives and opened the way to a better future in many parts of the world. But the pervasiveness of technology and its impacts on the lives of billions of individuals now demands several crucial questions: what place for humanity in a world dominated by technology? What directions of progress do we want, with what goals and what means?

Technological innovation has substituted itself for the notion of progress. Worse: it has become its only admitted manifestation. This is an intellectual abdication as much as a political problem.

In his work To Save Everything, Click Here, published in 2014, Morozov offers a clear definition of technological solutionism. For him, the development of new technologies has been accompanied by two ideologies: solutionism, erected as a totem by the champions of Silicon Valley, for whom all aspects of our lives can be improved by technology; and webcentrism, a new fantasy that has made the digital the center of gravity of the world. Forced digitization is not without consequences: growing disinformation, data breaches, advertising phishing, identity theft. If platforms were once considered the site of contemporary utopias, they have fostered the emergence of a voracious individualism, far from the spirit of the internet pioneers born of the libertarian counterculture of the 1970s.1

Far from the disputes between technophiles and technophobes, whose oppositions are too binary to be accurate, I have observed for years how new technologies shape our lives daily and preempt the long term. Today everything moves fast, too fast, without frightening our political leaders and without any genuine reflection on the social, environmental, and ethical consequences. Technological innovation has substituted itself for the notion of progress; worse, it has become its only admitted manifestation.

Rare earths, a race without end

The frenzied quest for rare earths, necessary for the functioning of our smartphones, computers, and electric cars, degrades land and marine environments. From lithium extraction in the Atacama salt desert in Chile to cobalt mining in the DRC, to polymetallic nodules on the ocean floor, humanity destroys ecosystems daily to satisfy its technological appetite. It is now on the Moon that the great powers are racing to extract usable metals, particularly helium-3, considered the energy of the future.

Supposedly decarbonizing the economy, the green revolution runs up against an implacable reality: producing so-called renewable energies requires rare metals. In The Dark Side of Green Energy, Guillaume Pitron shows that every green technological innovation inevitably generates pollution transfers.2 For Aurore Stéphant, a mining geologist, it is imperative to rethink digital transition approaches and identify levers to drastically reduce their impacts.

Hidden in the Cloud are servers, rare earths, and waste. According to researchers at the University of Massachusetts, training neural networks can consume as much energy as five cars over their entire lifecycle.3 The Netherlands recently discovered the staggering consumption of Microsoft's datacenter: 84 million liters of water, when the company had declared only 12 to 20 million.4

The end of Moore's Law

The endless technological race, sold as a categorical imperative, is at the heart of geopolitical stakes and the Sino-American rivalry. While 5G is barely beginning to roll out, Chinese satellites are already testing 6G. Intel, Qualcomm, Google on the American side; Huawei, Samsung, NTT Docomo on the Asian side; Nokia, Ericsson, Orange on the European side: all are sharpening their weapons to win this battle.

"In an extremely mobile society where everything is disposable, the psychological need for security and stability is often perversely exploited by the industrialist, the advertising agency, and the merchant." — Victor Papanek

The principle of Moore's Law, according to which computing capabilities double every two years, enabled the emergence of smaller and more powerful devices. But this logic, conducive to planned obsolescence, pushes ever greater consumption, making any fight against digital consumerism illusory. These objects of subordination and dependence were supposed to make us more free.

A technological solution for every problem

Far from reflecting on the origins of problems, states and institutions default to technological solutionism as a first reflex. This is the case for migration flows, managed through identification and surveillance technologies, chips in visas, biometric identifiers, cameras, which belong to a classic reactive system rather than a proactive policy.

"Every science, having become experimental, depends on the technique that alone makes it possible to technically reproduce phenomena." — Jacques Ellul

Technological progress has become a formidable alibi for absolving ourselves of our errors. While we know that the growing use of air conditioning aggravates global warming, Dubai invested billions in energy-hungry towers before investing considerable sums in geoengineering technologies to make it rain. Climate manipulation could have a catastrophic impact on the balance of the atmosphere and oceans.

The Metaverse became the avatar of a dystopian world where digital twins promise to synchronize the physical and the virtual. The Tuvalu islands, threatened by submersion, are considering creating a virtual version of their archipelago before its disappearance. While the applications are real, Raja Koduri at Intel affirms that creating a metaverse for hundreds of millions of users requires computing power a thousand times greater than the current level.

The age of surveillance capitalism

The digital era has brought many advances and with it its share of disillusions. One of the greatest dangers comes from the way tech giants collect and use our data. According to academic Sarah Spiekermann, "some companies hold up to 30,000 data points for each individual they track."5

Initially, the Internet was designed as a decentralized network. This design rested on the conviction that decentralization would make it resistant to censorship. Regulators — CNIL, GDPR, CCPA — constitute firewalls but do not prevent all abuses. The collection and transfer of medical data of millions of French citizens to the giant IQVIA is proof: the American company holds, with CNIL's assent, pharmaceutical information on millions of people. Even if the data is pseudonymized, the operation remains reversible.6

The pervasiveness of algorithms

In The Age of Surveillance Capitalism, Shoshana Zuboff denounces the power of intelligent machines, a power that depends not so much on their present interactions as on their capacity to predict and orient our future behavior. By automating existing discriminations, the digital transformation merely amplifies them. Researchers at the AI Now Institute at NYU raised the alarm: "It is becoming increasingly clear that in various domains, AI amplifies inequalities and places the means of control in the hands of those who already have power."

Timnit Gebru, one of the leading American researchers according to Nature, was fired from Google for criticizing the biases of artificial intelligence systems. In Weapons of Math Destruction, Cathy O'Neil, a mathematics doctoral graduate from Harvard, demonstrates how Big Data increases inequalities and threatens democracy.7

Social credit, the world ahead

Personal data represents precious and strategic resources fought over by Chinese and American giants. Countries such as Sri Lanka, Cambodia, and Poland have expressed interest in the Chinese social credit model. In France, some elected officials are campaigning for a French-style social credit. Caught between the temptation of social credit and the arbitrary censorship of American platforms, our democracies appear quite fragile.

Palantir Technologies, financed by In-Q-Tel, the CIA's venture capital arm, was used by New Orleans police to predict which individuals were likely to commit crimes. The experiment revealed numerous errors and major biases, reinforcing social, racial, and economic prejudices.8

An urgent need for regulation

"In the field of health, ethical Progress aims to repair humanity; unethical progress works to augment humanity." — Axel Kahn

In Europe, the Digital Services Act and the Digital Market Act attempt to establish common rules. The European AI strategy presented in 2021 aims to make the EU a world-class hub. However, the sums allocated are clearly insufficient: the Commission plans €20 billion over the decade, while the Chinese and Americans plan to spend three to four times more.

Digital pharmakon

The new technological paradigm — facial recognition, the Internet of Things, mass surveillance, the tyranny of algorithmic biases — brings our societies closer every day to the dystopia of 1984. This headlong rush, widely denounced by Jill Lepore, professor of history at Harvard, must not make us forget that unlike progress, which seeks an improvement of the human condition, innovation merely creates new products, leaving the legislator to manage the ethics.

"The technology already exists. It's only the will we're lacking." — Cathy O'Neil

CRISPR genome editing technology could make it possible to cure the most serious genetic diseases. The Neuralink brain implant promises to help people with disabilities and open new prospects against Parkinson's and Alzheimer's. Unless these technologies open the Pandora's box of transhumanism. For while technology is neutral, the ethics of the user and the designer are not always so.

Between the techno-solutionists who have made technology an ideology and the resisters trying to flee the hell of chips and robots, there exists a path. The Low Tech movement, relocalism, the growing questioning of our relationship to consumption, the decarbonization of the supply chain: not everything is lost if these initiatives are filtered through reason and common sense.

To those who, after reading this article, are still asking whether technology constitutes the answer or the problem, I believe, like Bernard Stiegler, that it is a Pharmakon: both the poison and the remedy.

Steve Moradel teaches strategy at ESSEC and is founder of Soara.
