Fresh water represents less than 3 percent of the water present on Earth. For two centuries, our societies have nonetheless treated it as an inexhaustible resource, whose rivers would regenerate on their own and whose aquifers would recharge by themselves. The historic drought of 2022, the floods of 2023 and NOAA's confirmation of a powerful El Niño for 2026-2027 demonstrate the contrary, territory after territory.

Charlène Descollonges grew up in Dijon, by the watercourses she roamed with her brother, a fly fisherman. She was about ten years old when she first saw what pollution, pumping and dams do to rivers, and never again looked at water the same way. A double master's degree in hydrology, an engineering degree from the Mines, five years spent in the territorial civil service managing the sharing of the resource in the Arve basin, in Haute-Savoie, then the work of consultant and lecturer: her path is that of a scientist who understood very early that technology would not suffice, and that the problem of water was first of all cultural. In 2022 she cofounded the association Pour une hydrologie régénérative and published Living Waters (Actes Sud, 2026). She is preparing a book on the link between water and women, which examines the way we have constrained rivers as we have constrained women.

She defines herself as a diplomat of waters and rivers, and believes in cooperation rather than confrontation, including with those who degrade what she spends her life protecting.

We hear about water everywhere. Droughts, mega-basins, conflicts over use, water tables at their lowest. But at bottom, do we really measure what is at stake?

Offhand, no. We have not grasped the scale of the effects of climate change and human activities on hydrosystems. By hydrosystems, I mean the whole of the rivers, the streams, the very small watercourses connected to one another, connected too to the wetlands, the forests, the aquifers, all these stocks of natural water. We are not fully aware of having profoundly altered these hydrosystems, nor that climate change will alter them still more. The distribution of available water across the seasons is going to be upended.

We suffer from a kind of amnesia of water, the opposite of the memory of water that some defend without its being scientifically verified. Our collective memory of water, if indeed it exists, remains very fleeting. This year, a very rainy winter, thirty-two days of nearly continuous rain, caused major flooding in France. And we are already sliding toward a flash drought, which will no doubt set in over the summer, with its probable share of conflicts over use. Having received a great deal of water this winter changes nothing, and that remains largely absent from our minds.

There is a lack of information, of pedagogy, of awareness-raising. For a very long time, we considered that the lack of water did not concern France, that it was in other countries, far from us. Yet we are perhaps the most vulnerable to these conflicts over use, precisely because we are used to having plenty of water. Our way of thinking was not built around scarcity. That is very dangerous: not to see the danger is not to be ready to share water among all uses, and with the natural environments.

Is it a lack of acculturation? Must we change paradigm and rethink our relationship to water, right down to the schools and the media, around its growing scarcity and the dangers to come?

Yes, there is a need for pedagogy, to understand the stakes of water and, as far as possible, to territorialize them. Our school programs address these questions in a very global way, which is useful, but to stop there is to miss the local stakes. If one does not speak directly to the inhabitants and to the local realities of water, one misses something essential.

The south of France, for example, depends entirely on the water resources of the Durance, that tributary of the Rhône which rises in the glaciers of the Hautes-Alpes. Immense hydraulic works diverted its waters as far as Marseille, Aix and the great coastal cities, which became wholly dependent on them. But it is an artificial dependence. If the inhabitants of Aix-Marseille do not learn it, do not take it in quickly, they will not be able to adapt to the effects of climate change that are the accelerated melting of glaciers and the decline of snow cover, hence a diminution of the available resource. It is one example among many others.

"The illusion that technology will save us."

To the illusion of an abundant water is added a third factor, peculiar to our modern societies: the illusion that technology will save us, that we will always find solutions to store water, to reuse it in closed circuits ad infinitum. It is the paradigm of the engineer who controls water and believes he can extract himself from the reality of a territory with defined and limited resources.

According to the United Nations, only 12 percent of the world's water serves domestic uses. The remaining 88 percent go to agriculture and industry. The great battle over water is not waged at our taps. Why does this reality remain so little known?

These 88 percent are economic water. The bulk of our uses serves economic growth, and the need for water keeps rising with it. There is a real correlation between economic development and the use of water. To this is added a question of price. Water is cheap, it is one of the least costly commodities and one of the least burdensome for economic sectors, unlike energy. Now what is cheap is considered to be without value.

Does the low cost of water proceed from a feeling or from a reality? Does the price vary by territory?

It is factual, with two exceptions: the systems of financialization of water that appeared in the United States, first in California, then in Australia. This model is not acceptable on the ethical level. Financialization follows market logics: when the resource grows scarce, the price rises, while needs rise too. This speculation can lead to farmer suicides, when they prefer to sell their water rather than use it, because it is more profitable. The effect on agricultural systems is very deleterious. It is not the path to follow, and it is the only exception that contradicts the idea of a cheap water.

In France, the average price of drinking water, domestic water, runs around four euros per cubic meter, fairly close to the European average. Some countries pay more, others less. These four euros concern only a share of fresh-water withdrawals, on the order of 20 percent, to be verified, which corresponds to domestic use. It is the only water paid for at this rate. The other uses, energy, agriculture, industry, come under other systems, fairly complex ones.

The price thus remains low, which produces a perverse effect: since water costs little, we do not invest sufficiently in infrastructure, we do not renew it to prevent leaks, we do not modernize the networks or the treatment systems. The water economist Esther Crauser-Delbourg, who devoted her thesis to the water footprint and the price of water, broadly confirms this observation. In French agriculture, water can be free, or cost two to three euros per cubic meter. It is highly variable.

All the great civilizations were built around water. Mesopotamia between the Tigris and the Euphrates, ancient Egypt around the Nile, the Aztecs on Lake Texcoco. Then the modern civilizations decided to master water rather than organize themselves around it. Is that where everything tipped over?

Yes, all were built around water, with the necessity of mastering it to produce their food. We know these ancestral cultures that diverted water into canals, that infiltrated it. Research has worked a great deal on this subject. Certain hypotheses, and I am not a specialist on this question, advance that these ancient civilizations declined because of climatic catastrophes, enormous droughts that would have dried them out.

One sees it clearly: as soon as there is no more water, or on the contrary when there is too much of it, when it floods and destroys, populations run real risks. We have forgotten our dependence on water and its dangerousness. We depend totally on its availability, in quantity as in quality, for a pollution renders it unusable. And we remain vulnerable when intense rains fall on a territory and provoke floods.

Our modern societies were built around these great rivers, these water points, these aquifers, then they developed immense hydraulic infrastructures, for agriculture first, then for the cities. That is how they lost sight of this relationship of interdependence and of the danger water represents. They tipped into the illusion of having controlled everything, of being out of danger. Paradoxically, that made them more vulnerable.

An example: in France, many dikes were built to contain the rivers and prevent their overflows. In the past, as with the Nile, these overflows were beneficial, for they deposited fertile silts. Our modern societies judged that they no longer were, and goods and people also had to be transported. So the rivers were diked, and behind the dikes were installed industrial zones, housing, all sorts of stakes. But the dikes are very costly to maintain, and during particularly intense events, like this winter on the Garonne, they give way. Their rupture then provokes a surplus of flooding, greater than it would have been in their absence. There is the whole paradox: to want to control, and to become more vulnerable.

You are a hydrology engineer by training. What is the precise moment in the field that definitively changed the way you look at water?

I have two great moments in my career, at the time when I was a civil servant in a local authority that dealt with these questions of water sharing. The first is an exchange with the president of the body, an elected official, the mayor of a village. He told me that the problem also came from the fact that water no longer had time to infiltrate the soils, and that the soils no longer retained it. He had the intuition that there were more droughts because water runs off instead of infiltrating. That triggered a first awakening, for I had sensed it without having put it that way. It pushed me to seek out the data, the studies that corroborated this intuition. It is from there that regenerative hydrology was born, which aims to slow water down and to infiltrate it.

The second trigger, the one that definitively made me leave the civil service, came as I was measuring the flow of a river, in the field. One of those I was studying. I was literally watching it die before my eyes. The water was very warm, there were no more fish, entire zones were dried out. I felt profoundly powerless and useless. That feeling of uselessness made me leave: I told myself that I had a great deal of energy, that I could put it elsewhere, and that I would rather heal rivers on a far vaster scale, by informing, raising awareness, setting changes in motion at the national level.

Is it a commitment of public utility?

Yes, a deep commitment. I am very attached to the general interest, to public utility, since my student days. I attended an engineering school but I never worked in a design office: I always worked in the public sector. The values of the commons, their organization, fully inhabit me. I do not see why an element as vital as water could be privatized or monopolized for certain uses. It is something I cannot manage to understand, even intellectually.

You grew up by the rivers, you have devoted your life to them. Does water still move you, or has the expert's gaze ended by taking over completely?

Both, clearly. During my studies and my first years as an engineer, it was the expert's gaze that prevailed, except in the field. In contact with the rivers, I was overwhelmed by emotion, but a rather negative emotion, for I was sad to see that everyone was losing interest in the rivers, that they were literally drying out and dying. There, my emotions caught up with me.

The engineer's paradigm, for its part, remains totally disconnected from emotions, which it is rather frowned upon to express. One would have to be unfeeling, perfectly rational. I am deconstructing that today, notably thanks to ecofeminist thought, to the philosophers who have worked at length on this question. It is not exclusive, it is not one or the other: it is one and the other. We need both.

Is this distrust of emotion above all Western, as if a cold, almost mathematical approach were alone judged credible?

That is exactly what I observe. And it is precisely by putting emotion and the human back into it that we restore value to what we do.

In Living Waters, you write that our understanding of the water cycle is fundamentally mistaken. What do we think we know, and completely ignore?

The first thing we ignore is green water. It is the water contained in the first few meters of the soil, captured by the vegetation, which evapotranspires it. Evapotranspiration is the passage of liquid water from the first few meters of the soil to gaseous water taken up into the atmosphere. Many agronomists and hydrologists consider this evapotranspired water as lost water. Yet, and this is what we are not taught sufficiently, or even ever, this green water is not lost. It is put back into circulation within the continents. It becomes a source of rain for other regions. The water evapotranspired into the atmosphere is carried by the atmospheric currents, by the prevailing winds, and falls again elsewhere as rain. We realized it by deforesting the Amazon and the Congo, which affected the rains of the neighboring regions.

That, we cannot manage to integrate into our representations of the water cycle. We have the impression of one great cycle, the ocean and the continents, and nothing else. We do not represent the small cycles within the great one. Yet they are fundamental, for certain countries depend entirely on the forests and their evapotranspiration. It is a first shift that we seek to bring about through regenerative hydrology.

Then comes the question of hydrosystems, these connected rivers. Often, when we look at a river, we imagine it fixed. It would not move, it would be perfectly straight, lined with a few small, neatly aligned trees. The river is not that. Above all, we no longer leave any wood in it. There is a kind of fear toward wood in rivers, whereas they have coevolved with forests and wood for millions of years. Why is this important? Wood holds back the sediments and the blocks carried by the river, and allows it to keep itself at an acceptable level. At the rhythm of the floods, it goes to seek wood, sediments, it moves. If we shut it into a canal and remove the wood from it, it incises itself progressively: with each flood, it digs deeper, enters a spiral of incision, and disconnects. In our perception, the river is something very fixed, whereas it is very moving, very alive.

If we do not change our gaze on these rivers that move, that need wood and space, we will make ourselves vulnerable once again. It is these two changes that we seek to bring about with regenerative hydrology: to recover green water, that of the forests, the wetlands, the meadows, and to recover free rivers, that have space and can take it.

The regenerative hydrology you defend already exists in India, Kenya, Australia, Mexico. What have these countries understood about water that France still refuses to admit?

Other countries already practice it. As I explain in the book, it is not a new concept in itself. We put many different words behind it. In the United States, they speak of conservation hydrology, the one that makes it possible to keep water in the soils.

India is a shattering case, by the gravity of its situation. It is a country that concentrates immense economic stakes and that figures among the most exposed to the danger of water, between the melting of glaciers, the monsoons, the droughts and the salinization of the land. In one of its regions most affected by farmer suicides, the Paani Foundation, the water foundation, led a vast program to recreate a sense of community around water and to support the farmers. The inhabitants were trained, people went into the villages, and a kind of competition was organized to dig the most swales, infiltration basins, storage zones. Thousands of people were swept up in this dynamic. It made it possible to keep water in the soils, to slow it down, to reinfiltrate it into the aquifers. The inhabitants also learned to measure the level of the water tables in order to ensure a good recharge and the capacity to irrigate the land. The next step consisted in supporting the farmers toward agroecological practices, to free themselves from chemical inputs, both for their health and for the crops they eat or sell.

That inspires us enormously, in technique as in the way of conducting change. These transformations do not rest on the shoulders of farmers alone: it is a whole community that supports them. Kenya inspires us in the same way, like the green belt projects in Africa. There is there a sense of belonging to the territory, the idea that in taking care of water, one takes care of the land, of the other living beings on which one depends, and of the survival of the community. That is what we lack in France, and more broadly in Western Europe. We seek to recreate this bond of belonging, and all the projects we carry out go in this participatory direction.

France has channeled its rivers, removed its meanders, concreted over its wetlands, all in the name of progress. Can we still repair what we have undone, or must we learn to build on something else?

We have not measured the economic value that the destruction of hydrosystems covers, the artificialization of the soils and the rivers, the disappearance of the wetlands. We still consider what surrounds us, what is called the environment, or worse nature, as a reality external to humans, devoid of value, from which we could draw and which we could simplify in the name of economic growth, itself held to be synonymous with progress, stability and security. It is a delusion. We do not see to what extent we are making ourselves vulnerable to hydric extremes, to heavy rains as to droughts, which climate change will amplify.

Climate change acts as a trigger of awareness. The idea is not to destroy all the dams, all the dikes, and to live otherwise from one day to the next. We are not ready for that, just as we are not ready to give up nuclear power in France, so anchored is it in our engineering culture. Where there is space, where it is possible, we let the water come, we let it do, we accompany it in a process of regeneration. That demands a great deal of diplomacy, of dialogue with the landowners, the farmers. It is this culture of bond and dialogue that makes us change paradigm, rather than the engineer who lays down a structure and relies on maintenance. If we let the beaver, the wetlands and the rivers do their work, we must accept to compose with uncertainty, and adjust our space of activity.

You define yourself as a diplomat of waters and rivers. In a country where conflicts over use among farmers, industrialists and local authorities are intensifying, what does that mean concretely?

The status of water diplomat does not yet exist, to my knowledge, and I know very few people who truly are one. It is to manage to understand both parties. I understand the hydrosystems, the rivers and their needs, and I also understand the economic interests that seek space to cultivate, to produce energy, goods and services. One must adopt this posture of listening to grasp the interests of each. In France above all, and that is my prism, we have so encroached on the rivers' space of freedom and on the aquifers that the margin for progress is enormous. My objective is to give back this space of freedom to the rivers and to relieve the aquifers as much as possible. I am no doubt more advocate than diplomat at times, but I manage to speak to all the economic actors that depend on water. To be a diplomat, in a word, is to put oneself in a posture of listening and to carry the voice of entities we do not hear, that do not have the same voice as we, humans, do. It is a very difficult ridge line.

The American National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, the world's reference federal agency on climate forecasting, has just confirmed an 82 percent probability that a powerful El Niño will form this summer and persist until 2027. What does that mean for our rivers, our aquifers, our territories?

I do not have the exact figure in mind, I had rather placed it around 85 percent, so we are there. I am not a meteorologist. From what I have been able to read and hear, we are entering this very summer into this El Niño phenomenon, cyclical and natural, but in a world transformed by climate change, its amplification is all the stronger. It will probably be a super El Niño, of an intensity greater than those of the last decades. Climatologists and paleoclimatologists compare it to far older episodes, which are thought to have provoked heavy mortality and famines.

In Europe, the effects are less direct. One can nonetheless expect, around winter, very abundant rains. Above all, most experts estimate that the effects will be felt from 2027. 2026 marks the beginning, 2027 will see more significant effects. For companies with a globalized model, it is a real subject: Europe will perhaps be less affected, but certain countries will suffer, with heavy mortality during very powerful heat waves and heat domes, monsoons and very intense rains, floods. These phenomena are well tracked and documented. In the event of major destruction or shortages in certain places, industries entirely dependent on the availability of water will pay the price.

It will no doubt be the moment of truth for this awareness. I do not know how far it will translate into acts, for the awareness, we more or less all have it. For the last two years, a movement of ecological backlash in France has relegated these subjects to the last rank, with significant setbacks, notably on the CSRD. I hope nonetheless that it will call for a stronger international solidarity.

You believe in cooperation rather than confrontation, including with those who contribute to degrading water. What convinced you that it was the right path?

I have carried that within me since childhood. I have a very conflictual family history, and my little sister already used to say that I was the diplomat of the family. I grew up with conflict, sometimes with chaotic situations, and I built myself around this identity. It was my survival strategy. So much for the personal part.

What convinced me about water is, first of all, the history of water governance in France, a beautiful history that has been building for more than sixty years and that is dear to me. Then, when I set foot in the public structures of water management, in the water parliaments, I was fascinated to see people with very divergent opinions, of very different political colors, able to sit around a table to talk about water, agree on projects, make a shared effort among farmers, EDF, drinking water, elected officials and industrialists. I told myself that water was a subject of bond and cooperation. We often speak of it through conflicts and wars, very real ones, and war, in turn, strikes water. It is said, moreover, to be both victim and weapon of war. I like to think that, behind the conflict, that is where everything is at stake: how do we create common ground, consensus, cooperation, how do we restore a culture of peace around water.

In fifty years, what will France look like if we have changed nothing in our relationship to water? Is there still time to do otherwise?

In fifty years, in 2076, I do not know if I will still be here. These questions are dizzying, and that is precisely their interest: they make it possible to think on the scale of great structural changes, to project oneself over a little more than half a lifetime. For someone who is twenty today, it is the horizon of their children and grandchildren.

If nothing changes, we are heading toward a reinforcement of the technological paradigm, the one that consists in controlling water ever more. In France, that would no doubt mean more nuclear plants, more large storage, these mega-basins for agriculture, but also storage for artificial snow and for hydroelectricity. The engineer's paradigm would pursue its momentum. More data centers, hence zones in great difficulty and conflicts. A priority given to the economy, to the point where water would lose its status as a common, as a matter of general interest and public utility, to become a product in the service of growth. More conflicts, no doubt wars, zones devastated by floods and droughts, migrations. It is a very probable scenario if we change nothing, and I deliberately put it forward as a foil, to recall that we are not condemned to take it.

Another path is possible, the one we seek to set in motion with regenerative hydrology. A desirable path, on the aesthetic and scenic level: where there is water and greenery, as in the place where I live, in the Chartreuse, one feels good. It is a question of living well. We are not condemned to sink money and energy into infrastructure, we can do otherwise. I am optimistic by nature, and I believe it entirely possible to spread this culture of the care of water, so that it is slowed, that it spreads and that it stays as much as possible in the territories, for life.

That is the most important thing. What guides us is life, not money or growth. Without life, there is neither money nor growth. We can endlessly build closed circuits and infrastructure, but if there is no more water in the rivers and the aquifers, no more pollinators, no more beavers, no more forest, no more growth is possible. It is this path that must be opened, to give rise to this sense of the common and of interdependence.
