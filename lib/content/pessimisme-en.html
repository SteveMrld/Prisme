The outrage economy, or how media amplifies the bias

Negativity bias would be manageable if our information environment were neutral. It is not. Traditional media have long understood that bad news sells. This phenomenon predates the internet: nineteenth-century newspapers had already theorized that blood, crime, and catastrophe drove sales. But the algorithmic logic of social networks has taken this principle to an unprecedented scale and intensity.

Recommendation algorithms, YouTube, Facebook, TikTok, X, are not designed to inform. They are designed to maximize time spent on the platform. Research shows that content eliciting strong emotions, anger, indignation, fear, disgust, generates more engagement than neutral or positive content.4 A tweet that outrages gets shared six times more than a tweet that calmly informs. The result is mechanical: algorithms preferentially select and amplify the most anxiety-inducing content, creating a spiral in which our perception of the world aligns with what the platforms judge most profitable to show us.

If information were a drug, studies on its cognitive effects would lead us to strictly limit the dosage.

Rolf Dobelli, Stop Reading the News, 2020

To this is added what researchers call the availability heuristic, formalized by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky: we estimate the probability of an event from the ease with which we can recall examples of it. A terrorist attack, covered continuously for forty-eight hours by dozens of news channels, becomes salient in our memory and colors our perception of risk, even though statistically terrorism kills infinitely fewer people than cardiovascular disease, which never leads the evening news.5

Progress is real, invisible, and boring

The central paradox is that the most significant advances in human history are, by nature, slow, diffuse, and narratively unremarkable. Child mortality does not fall overnight with a news flash. Poverty does not hold a press conference when it retreats by two percentage points. Life expectancy does not make the front page when it gains three months in an emerging country. These deep trends, extraordinary on a historical scale, remain invisible in a media flow that feeds on punctual and dramatic events.

Harvard psychologist Steven Pinker has devoted two books to documenting this gap. In The Better Angels of Our Nature (2011) and then in Enlightenment Now (2018), he shows that almost all indicators of human well-being, violence, famine, illiteracy, mortality, poverty, are improving over the long term, and that our collective perception of this movement is systematically inverted.6 His theses have sparked legitimate debates; he has been accused of minimizing existential risks such as climate change or weapons of mass destruction. But on the basic observation, the gap between statistical reality and perception, his demonstration remains difficult to refute.

Why this gap is politically dangerous

The pessimistic distortion is not a psychological curiosity without consequences. It directly shapes the political landscape. An electorate convinced that everything is getting worse is an electorate receptive to radical rupture narratives: "the system is broken, we need to start over." Populist movements of every stripe have perfectly grasped this dynamic: their rhetoric relies less on precise alternative proposals than on amplifying and validating the feeling that things are catastrophic and that elites are concealing it.

Distrust of institutions, media, science, representative democracy, takes root in the same soil. If the world is objectively doing so badly, then the experts who claim to manage it are either incompetent or lying. The spiral of pessimism feeds distrust, which feeds radicalism, which feeds pessimism. This cycle is not inevitable, but escaping it requires a conscious effort.

Lucid optimism, a discipline

The answer to this diagnosis is not the blissful optimism that denies real problems. Climate change is a serious threat. Inequality is growing in many countries. Geopolitical tensions are real. The mental health of younger generations is deteriorating in wealthy countries. These facts deserve attention and action.

But acknowledging these challenges does not require separating them from simultaneously achieved progress. Philosopher Philippe Gabilliet puts it this way: one must cultivate an optimism of ends, the conviction that things can improve and that human action can contribute to this, combined with a pessimism of means, lucidity about obstacles, risks, and the complexity of reality.7 This is the inverse of what our biases spontaneously push us to do, which is to cultivate a pessimism of ends, the conviction that nothing can change, and an optimism of means, the illusion that problems will resolve themselves if nothing is done about them.

Countering negativity bias is not a question of temperament or naivety. It is an intellectual discipline that begins with clear awareness of one's own cognitive mechanisms, and with deliberate informational hygiene. Reading long-term data rather than news feeds. Distinguishing structural trends from punctual events. Calibrating one's attention to risks according to their actual probability rather than their media salience. And remembering, regularly, that the world of tomorrow will not necessarily resemble the world of yesterday, because the world of yesterday did not resemble, despite all the Cassandras of every era, the world of the day before.
