In the almond orchards of California's Central Valley, the trucks arrive before dawn in February. They carry hives. Millions of hives, brought in from across the country, rented from professional beekeepers for three weeks of work. Without them, there is no harvest. California produces 80% of the world's almonds, and almond trees do not pollinate themselves. This colossal logistical operation, repeated every year, has become so routine that no one finds it remarkable. It ought to be deeply disquieting. It is the sign that something, in the natural functioning of the world, has broken.

The honeybee has existed for 100 million years. It has survived five mass extinctions, the drift of continents, successive glaciations. It has coevolved with flowering plants in a relationship so ancient and so precise that it reads as one of the fundamental architectures of the living world. What we are doing to it now has no precedent in its history. And very little in ours.

What the figures do not say loudly enough

Between October 2022 and April 2023, American beekeepers lost 48% of their colonies. In Europe, average winter losses have exceeded 20% per year for a decade. In France, certain départements have recorded mortalities of 80% in some years. These figures circulate, are cited, and then forgotten. They do not provoke the dread they should.

Perhaps because they are always presented as an environmental problem: and environmental problems have become an abstract category, filed away in a mental compartment separated from everything else. The stakes here are not environmental in the narrow sense. It is alimentary. 75% of the world's food crops depend, entirely or partially, on animal pollination. Bees alone ensure the reproduction of more than 20,000 plant species. Their disappearance is not an impoverishment of biodiversity in the aesthetic sense of the term. It is a direct threat to the earth's capacity to feed nine billion human beings.

The bee is not an insect among others. It is infrastructure. And like all infrastructure, its importance is measured only at the moment it gives way.

A memory lost in a single generation

Every agrarian civilisation has maintained a particular relationship with pollinators. The Maya venerated Ah-Muzen-Cab, god of the stingless bees, guarantor of the abundance of harvests. The Egyptians of the Old Kingdom used the bee as a royal symbol: it appears in the cartouches of the pharaohs as a sign of sovereignty over Upper and Lower Egypt. In medieval Europe, apiaries were placed under the protection of saints, and the theft of a hive was punished more severely than the theft of a horse. This is not superstition. It is peasant knowledge encoded in ritual: these societies knew, empirically, that their survival depended on these insects.

This knowledge took millennia to accumulate. It disappeared in fifty years. Not because people became stupid: because the chain of transmission between the generations who cultivated the land and those who consume it has been severed. Industrial agriculture did not only change the way we produce. It cut the cord of memory between the human and the cycles of the living world on which it depends.

Neonicotinoids, or the silent crime

The causes of colony collapse are multiple and entangled: habitat destruction, the impoverishment of wild flora, parasites such as varroa, viral diseases. But at the centre of it all, there are systemic pesticides, and in particular neonicotinoids. These molecules, introduced in the 1990s, have a remarkable property: they are absorbed by the entire plant, including the pollen and the nectar. A bee that forages in a treated field is not killed on the spot. It returns to the hive with contaminated pollen, gradually losing its ability to navigate, to remember, to communicate. It forgets the way home. The hive collapses from within, slowly, with no apparent corpse.

Bayer brought imidacloprid, the first mass-market neonicotinoid, onto the market in 1994. In 2018, Bayer acquired Monsanto for 63 billion dollars, becoming the world's leading agrochemical group. The same group therefore sells the pesticides that decimate pollinators, the seeds of the monocultures that impoverish wild flora, and is today developing alternative solutions to natural pollination. This vertical integration is not a conspiracy. It is simply the logic of industrial capitalism applied to the living world: create the problem, then sell the solution.

The drone arrives: but not from where we think

At Harvard University's Wyss Institute, the researcher Robert Wood has been working for more than a decade on what he calls RoboBees. Robots half the size of a paperclip, weighing less than a tenth of a gram, whose wings beat a hundred and twenty times per second through piezoelectric actuators: ceramic strips that contract under the effect of an electric field. Some models can fly, swim, and perch on surfaces by static electricity. The project is divided into three axes: the body, the brain, and the colony. That last word is the most revealing. The ultimate objective is to coordinate swarms of autonomous robots so that they act as a single unit. The bee is not being replaced. The hive is being replicated.

This work is legitimate science. It explores the limits of biomimicry and opens real prospects in environmental monitoring and in search and rescue. In 2019, the first RoboBee performed an autonomous flight without a power tether. Robert Wood, asked about this breakthrough, said that it was precisely because his laboratory's recent advances in fabrication, materials and design had made it possible. The rigour is there. So is the intellectual honesty.

But science does not operate in a vacuum. In 2018, Walmart filed six patents for autonomous pollinator drones intended for industrial agricultural use. The same year, the Israeli startup Arugga raised several million dollars for pollinator robots in tomato greenhouses. Dropcopter was already offering commercial drone pollination services for Californian cherry and apple orchards. These are not researchers. They are economic actors positioning themselves in a market under formation, the size of which depends directly on the scale of the natural collapse.

The distinction is not trivial. On one side, a university trying to understand the flight of insects in order to build machines capable of going where humans cannot. On the other, companies patenting pollination as a marketable service before the problem is even officially recognised as irreversible. Science is not the problem. What is sometimes done with it, and by whom, is.

These technologies function at a small scale, under controlled conditions. They will not replace the bee: a single hectare of cultivation requires millions of acts of pollination per day, and no existing fleet of drones can rival that volume at a reasonable cost. But they do not need to be a complete solution in order to be lucrative. It is enough that they become indispensable in certain segments (high-value crops, industrial greenhouses, zones where pollinators have disappeared) for the model to hold.

The shape taking form is not artificial pollination as an emergency plan B. It is pollination as an economic asset, billable, patentable, the access to which will depend on the capacity to pay.

The enclosure of the last commons

For millennia, pollination was an absolute commons. Free, universal, invisible. A peasant in Bihar, a smallholder in Chiapas, a subsistence farmer in Mali did not need to understand the mechanism in order to benefit from it. It was enough not to destroy it.

India has 150 million smallholders whose crops depend on wild pollinators for more than 60%. Ethiopia derives 70% of its food security from a subsistence agriculture entirely dependent on pollinators. Sub-Saharan Africa as a whole, Southeast Asia, much of Latin America: these regions have neither the financial means nor the technological infrastructure to deploy fleets of pollinator drones. If natural pollination collapses and artificial pollination becomes the only alternative, a new fracture opens. It no longer runs along the political North-South line. It runs between those who will be able to purchase the vital function that the living world provided for free, and those who will not.

This is the real issue. Not the disappearance of an insect. The transformation of a fundamental ecological function into a privatisable financial asset. This process has a name in economic theory: enclosure. It was applied to the common lands of England in the 18th century, to peasant seeds at the end of the 20th century with patents on life. It applies today to pollination.

What resists, and what gives way

France banned neonicotinoids in 2018. Then granted derogations in 2020, 2021, 2022 for the sugar-beet sector, under pressure from industry. The European Union confirmed the ban on three major neonicotinoids in 2018, then continued to authorise emergency uses in several member states. The United Kingdom, post-Brexit, re-authorised thiamethoxam in 2021. These reversals are not failures of governance. They are the result of a balance of power between organised economic interests and an ecological demand that does not command the same institutional weight.

Serious alternatives exist, however. Regenerative agriculture rebuilds pollinator habitats by maintaining flower strips, hedgerows and fallows. Some French agricultural cooperatives have reduced their hive mortality by 60% by changing their practices, without significant loss of yield. Ecological corridors, put in place in Switzerland and the Netherlands, show that it is possible to recreate conditions favourable to pollinating insects even within intensive agricultural landscapes. These experiments exist. They do not make the front page.

The bee does not need to be saved in the romantic sense of the word. It needs us to stop destroying what it needs to live. Those are two very different things. The first mobilises feelings. The second requires precise political and economic decisions, against precise interests, at precise costs. That is why it is harder.
