There is a scene at the end of The Plague that is rarely quoted. Oran is celebrating, people are embracing in the streets, the quarantine has been lifted. But Dr. Rieux, from his terrace above, knows what the crowd ignores or pretends to ignore: the bacillus never dies nor disappears, it can lie dormant for years, and the day might come when, for the misfortune and instruction of men, the plague would rouse its rats and send them to die in a happy city. Camus wrote this in 1947. No one chose to read the ending.

Six years after the start of the Covid-19 pandemic, it is not so much the virus that merits reflection as what it revealed about our societies: their fragility in the face of fear, their docility before power, and their inability to tolerate doubt at the precise moment when doubt was the only intellectually honest exercise.

Western democracies underwent, between 2020 and 2022, the greatest experiment in the voluntary suspension of fundamental freedoms since the Second World War. Successive lockdowns, curfews, exemption certificates, closure of places of worship, prohibition of visiting dying parents, and finally vaccine passes conditioning access to social life on a medical act. Each of these measures was accepted, often demanded, by populations who nonetheless define themselves as heirs of the Enlightenment and human rights. This paradox was not sufficiently interrogated. It should have been.

Tocqueville had sensed it in the nineteenth century with a lucidity that is vertiginous. The despotism of modern democracies, he wrote in Democracy in America, will not resemble ancient tyrannies. It will not be violent. It will be tutelary, provident, mild. It will not break wills, it will soften them. It will not compel men to act, it will prevent them from acting. It will not destroy, it will prevent things from being born. Rereading these pages while thinking of the pandemic years produces a troubling effect of recognition. This is not an excessive comparison. It is a warning that lay before us and that we failed to recognize.

For what played out during those two years cannot be reduced to more or less competent health management. Something deeper was at stake: the question of what a democratic society is prepared to sacrifice in the name of its security, and above all who decides on this sacrifice and in whose name. Care homes remained closed while shopping centers reopened. Children were deprived of school while adults debated the right to drink on a terrace. Exhausted caregivers were applauded at windows in the evening by people who, two years later, were tearing each other apart over vaccination. The pandemic fractured Western societies not along a traditional political line but along an older and deeper one: the line separating those who trust institutions from those who no longer do. And that fracture predated the virus.

Hannah Arendt, analyzing the mechanisms by which ordinary societies accept extraordinary logics, insisted on a point too often summarized by the convenient formula of the banality of evil: it is not monsters who do evil, it is ordinary people who stop thinking. The suspension of critical judgment, obedience to legitimate authority, the total delegation of decision to experts whose word cannot be questioned: these are the mechanisms that, in an infinitely less tragic version than the one Arendt had observed, characterized part of the Western response to the pandemic. Not malevolence. Intellectual abdication.

For it is there that the most lasting wound lies. Scientists, epidemiologists, economists, and philosophers were marginalized, silenced, or disqualified not because their arguments were refuted but because they disturbed the narrative governments had chosen to maintain. Scientific debate, which is by nature a contradictory debate, nourished by uncertainty and revision, was presented as settled when it was not. Questioning a public health measure became morally equivalent to endangering lives. This is a serious intellectual confusion, and its effects on trust in science and institutions are still being felt.

Meanwhile, thousands of kilometers from European terraces, entire countries waited for a vaccine that was not coming. The COVAX facility, meant to guarantee equitable access to vaccines for low- and middle-income countries, was a failure. Wealthy nations had pre-purchased doses sufficient to vaccinate their populations several times over while sub-Saharan Africa struggled to reach 10% vaccine coverage in 2021. This asymmetry, though fundamental, barely existed in Western public debate. We were too busy debating the vaccine pass.

Rieux, at the end of The Plague, decides to write in order not to be among those who remain silent. To bear witness in favor of the plague-stricken, to leave at least a memory of the injustice and violence done to them. Six years later, the question is not whether the measures taken were just or unjust, effective or ineffective. The question is whether we had, collectively, the courage to examine honestly what happened. The answer, for now, is no. And that is perhaps the most alarming sign for the next time. Because there will be a next time.
