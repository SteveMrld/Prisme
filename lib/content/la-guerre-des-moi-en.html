The greatest adversary of this world is not war. Not money. Not even illness, that blind reaper the centuries have never managed to tame. No. The greatest adversary of humanity acts in broad daylight, with the sovereign insolence of one who has nothing to fear, for he is the one others would fear. He is born of our own darkness and yet he walks in the sun, greeted, adored, fed by hands that do not see they are feeding their own jailer. He is called: ME.

Not me, your humble author behind his keyboard, that ME, I hope, has learned to hold its own leash. I am speaking of the countless ME scattered across the whole surface of the earth, those billions of absolute entities that, reunited, make up this discordant and dissonant humanity, a kind of grandiose orchestra, well trained even, well equipped, schooled in the finest scores, yet playing with so many false notes that it ends by casting doubt on the very point of the music. And yet. How beautiful the music would be, without that obstinate, vengeful, insatiable ME.

When we arrive on earth, it is so small, this ME, that we do not notice it. It does not yet feel its own presence, feels no need to signal it. Look at a newborn in its mother's arms, it does not yet know where it ends and where she begins. It is porous, open, crossed by the world like a window without glass. It demands nothing, this tiny and original ME. It is almost vegetarian, almost peaceful, almost innocent. But the world, for its part, demands. The other ME, fed on the hormones of the world, insist that it come out of its cage. They force-feed it, they educate it, they equip it. For unlike the animals in captivity, we want this beast to be free, free to go and devour everything that passes within reach of its claws and its teeth. The original ME is vegetarian, it must become carnivorous. That is the rule. That is the tradition. That is what we call, with a suspect tenderness, growing up.

And the insignificant ME grows. It swells in silence, gathers experience the way one gathers venom, drop by drop, painlessly, with no one alarming anyone. Then one day, it utters the magic phrase, the one that answers to the magicians' "Abracadabra," to Ali Baba's "Open Sesame." This phrase is a single word: I. Ah, I! It is the absolute tipping point, the singularity of the ME, when it comes out for the first time, the ME does not explode, it implodes, folding back on itself like a star that collapses the better to shine. From now on there is only HIM. HIM. HIM. "Freedom! Freedom!" cries this ME at last set free, and before him stretches a horizon gorged with possessions to conquer, territories to mark out, whole worlds to reduce to his measure.

Like any conqueror worthy of the name, ME is patient. It begins with small conquests, almost touching in their innocence: "MY toy," "it belongs to ME." These two syllables are nonetheless already geopolitical acts. They designate a narrow space from which all who are not ME are excluded by definition, that is to say: everyone. As the body in which this tyrannical ME resides grows, so too does this space, or at least its possessions multiply: MY pen, MY notebook, MY book, MY school. Each new good is one more dose of fuel. And gradually, imperceptibly, this ME anchors its fundamental belief, the most deeply rooted, the most dangerous of all: I am the center.

For the true god of this world is neither Jesus, nor Allah, nor Yahweh. These names are only costumes cut to measure to clothe the same nakedness. Behind each of them hides the same face: ME. Not faith, faith, in its purest form, is perhaps the only moment when the ME agrees to kneel before something greater than itself. But faith confiscated, instrumentalized, turned into a banner of war: that one belongs entirely to the ME. It is ME that one protects when one kills for god, since one is defending MY faith, MY version of the divine, MY reading of the sacred text. The ME does not protect God, it protects only MY god, which belongs to it like all the other goods accumulated since that first triggering "I." And very often, before conquering the divine, the ME has already colonized MY studies, MY diploma, MY work, MY money, MY wife, MY country. For some of these trophies, it has betrayed, plotted, lied, cheated, or worse still, and history knows something of that. MY god is, at bottom, only the latest ornament in the now well-stocked wardrobe of the triumphant ME.

The world is faring badly, or, for those who prefer the glass half full, the world is not as harmonious as it ought to be, for a reason of disarming simplicity: there is a perpetual war of the ME. The Second World War had a beginning and an end. The war of the ME began the moment there were two men on this earth, and it will go on as long as any remain.

Imagine this despotic ME born among other despotic ME: that makes a family. And in this family, the first war is the most silent, it is waged at the table, in the unequal sharing of an attention, in the father's gaze that settles on one son rather than another, in the harmless phrase that amputates something invisible and essential. No one sees the blood. Yet it flows. These families form villages, then towns, then regions, then those gigantic and sovereign ME-countries, which naturally carry on the ancestral tradition by excluding from their perimeter all the other ME-countries, and by going to war against them if necessity, or simply appetite, makes itself felt. Within these ME-countries proliferate other ME, just as absolute: the ME-sex, the ME-race, the ME-religion. Each with its invisible borders. Each with its founding exclusions. Each convinced of being the only legitimate ME in a world that was waiting for it alone.

Too many ME on this earth, and not enough feeling for one another. Each of us curled in on himself, watching over the state of his possessions, armed with all that it is possible to be, going at times as far as preventive wars, that ultimate perversion: to conquer before having been attacked, to subdue before having been challenged. There is nothing above the ME. Only a ME that is more complex, better armed, more cleverly hidden beneath a sophisticated disguise, a ME that has learned to say we the better to rule.

What, then, is to be done against this invasive and dictatorial ME? The answer is cruel in its frankness: nothing. It is impossible to keep the ME from existing. Every philosophy that has claimed to abolish it has bred monsters; every political system that has sought to erase it has produced tyrants. The ME is not destroyed. It is passed through. The only thing to be done is to remind it, tirelessly, tenderly, firmly, that it has meaning only if it leads to the other ME. That it is real only if it agrees to tune itself to a wavelength able to catch and translate the other wavelengths. That the horizon does not begin at the limit of its possessions, but precisely where the other begins.

There exists a machine capable of such a feat. It appears in no manual of philosophy, in no constitution, in no peace treaty signed beneath the chandeliers of the great capitals. Yet it beats in every chest, even the most closed, even the ones most armored in old scars. It alone can gather all the ME into a country without borders, without customs, without a flag to plant in the flesh of the other. This country is not decreed. It is not inherited. Nor is it conquered, for all conquest is still an act of the ME. It is given. It is risked. It trembles, at times. It is lost, often. But it returns, always, because it is the only thing on this earth that yields nothing, and for which, mysteriously, men have always been ready to lose everything.

Somewhere at this very moment, a child holds out his hand to a stranger. He does not yet know that he has a ME. He does not yet know that he ought to be afraid, that one day he will have to defend, mark out, protect. He holds out his hand, simply, and in this gesture so small, so fragile, so infinitely threatened by all that the world will hasten to teach him, lies perhaps the only answer humanity has ever known, and that it spends its whole life forgetting.
