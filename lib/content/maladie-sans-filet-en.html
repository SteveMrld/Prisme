Leila Janah, founder of Samasource, died in Manhattan in January 2020, at thirty-seven, of a rare sarcoma. Her trajectory opens a question that the societies of independent work still struggle to put into words. That of serious illness in a professional regime where the body is the instrument of production, and remains, for the most part, without a safety net.

On 18 November 2019, from Manhattan where she was undergoing treatment at the Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, Leila Janah posted on Instagram a text of a few paragraphs that was nothing like a founder's press release. Seven months earlier, in the spring, she had received the diagnosis of an epithelioid sarcoma, a rare cancer of the soft tissues that accounts for less than one percent of sarcomas and whose existence she had until then been unaware of. In it she writes that she has changed her relationship to work and accepted that she could no longer carry everything. She describes her illness as a rare and strange beast, and speaks of her astonishment at the complexity of the human body as much as at the affection that reaches her.

She is thirty-seven and founded Samasource in 2008, a data-annotation company for artificial intelligence that at this time employs several thousand workers in Kenya, Uganda and India, and has helped lift tens of thousands of people out of extreme poverty. A year earlier, she restructured the whole into a for-profit company, with a foundation bearing her name as principal shareholder, in order to reach the capital required for scale. Her path belongs to that generation of social entrepreneurs trained at Harvard and seen at Davos, whose media visibility masks the tension of an economy that relies on individual commitment to carry what the institutions have ceased to take on.

In the weeks that follow this post, despite the progression of the illness, she continues to read stories to her stepdaughter and her godchildren, according to what the company she founded states after her death. On 23 January 2020, the Food and Drug Administration grants accelerated approval to Tazverik, the first treatment ever authorized for this rare cancer. Janah dies in Manhattan the next day.

The Janah case reaches well beyond its American frame alone. It points to a gray zone that runs through the contemporary economies of independent work, and that the social-protection systems struggle to cover without producing new gaps. In France, the abolition of the Régime social des indépendants at the turn of the 2010s and 2020s attached non-salaried workers to the general scheme. A self-employed worker therefore comes under Social Security. Sick leave is nonetheless still subject to conditions for the opening of rights more restrictive than those of employees, the daily allowances are calculated on often volatile incomes, and a recently established entrepreneur may find himself, on receiving a severe diagnosis, with effective cover far below what his status had led him to expect.

Private provident insurance, meant to fill these gaps, plays an uneven role. The contracts almost systematically include exclusions for prior medical conditions, waiting periods, and compensation ceilings pegged to declared incomes that young independents often understate for tax reasons. At the moment of diagnosis, what was signed a year earlier determines a trajectory on which it is no longer possible to act. The work of DARES and INSEE devoted to non-salaried workers documents a stronger exposure to psychosocial risks and a lesser recourse to care, in a landscape where the liberal professions and micro-entrepreneurs come under neither the same funds nor the same rules.

The term entrepreneur covers a series of very different realities that public communication tends to conflate. The founder of a Series A funded start-up, a freelance graphic designer, a platform driver and an established lawyer share neither the incomes, nor the cover, nor the real exposure to illness. They share, on the other hand, a characteristic that salaried employment, in its classic forms, had at least partly defused. The worker's body is also the exclusive instrument of production. Its stopping marks the stopping of the activity. This continuity, which most of the European social systems of the twentieth century had built in order to break, becomes the rule once more in an economy that multiplies hybrid statuses without rebuilding the corresponding protections.

The American case, in which Janah belongs, pushes the logic to its conclusion. Health insurance remains largely tied to traditional salaried employment. Founders insure themselves individually, at rates that follow age and medical history, or go through the structures of their own company once it is funded. The gap between a Samasource executive enjoying the best oncological care in Manhattan and an independent worker without cover remains immense. Yet both belong to the same institutional landscape where serious illness is negotiated first individually before being taken on collectively. Janah's trajectory does not expose a total absence of a net, of which she was never deprived. It exposes the degree to which, in contemporary independent work, protection against the health event is a matter of personal engineering, exposed to blind spots that neither luck, nor success, nor status quite prevents.

Serious illness, in the course of an independent worker's life, does not only meet the gaps in social protection. It also meets a cultural silence peculiar to the entrepreneurial world, where interruption for reasons of health is received as a kind of admission. The narratives prized by this world draw on a register of surpassing oneself, of resilience, of endurance, that leaves little room for the expression of vulnerability without converting it at once into a lesson. Janah's post of November 2019 is telling of this constraint. It manages to put a renunciation into words, but it does so in a language of gratitude and learning, as if illness had to yield a teaching in order to be publicly sayable.

This constraint of sayability does not belong to entrepreneurship alone, even if it intensifies there. It runs through the whole of the contemporary world of work. In the clinic for suffering at work that she founded in 1997 at the Nanterre hospital, the psychiatrist Marie Pezé has described across her books how the body becomes the last place in which the pressures that speech can no longer say are inscribed. The pathology, whether musculoskeletal, cardiac or psychic, often appears at the precise moment when the individual can no longer hold both his professional role and the acknowledgment of his exhaustion. The individual responsibilization of the worker, which has imposed itself as the managerial horizon for forty years, aggravates this mechanism by depriving each person of the collective resources that allowed, in other configurations, the complaint to be voiced together. Entrepreneurship extends this logic to its point of maximum tension. The project leader is at once employer and employee, beneficiary and victim of a system he embodies alone.

The independent worker who keeps quiet about his illness does not do so out of mere reserve or strategy. The framework in which he operates has internalized the norm of permanent availability, and to break this norm amounts to breaking with the professional identity itself. To announce to one's clients, one's investors, one's team, that a serious illness imposes a withdrawal, often amounts to setting off a sequence of worry, of withdrawn trust, of reassessed commitments. This mechanism proceeds from no ill will. It works because it is inscribed in the very expectations of the milieu, and because the entrepreneur has himself internalized it before becoming its target.

The silence then unfolds on several interlocking levels. There is the one kept before clients so as not to lose a contract, the one kept before teams so as not to weaken a still young structure, the one sometimes kept before loved ones so as not to turn the illness into a permanent relational event. There is, finally, the silence one keeps toward oneself, when fatigue and pain are read as signals to neutralize rather than as symptoms to heed. This last silence is the one that the clinicians of work meet most regularly. The independent worker arrives at the consultation with a pathology already settled in, because for months he treated his bodily alarms as obstacles to his productivity, not as messages about the state of an organism subjected to a tension that no structure regulates in his place.

The public framework, for its part, remains largely in retreat. French occupational-health policies, built on a salaried model, struggle to grasp populations that escape classic occupational medicine. No arrangement equivalent to the compulsory periodic check-up exists for independents, whose medical follow-up depends entirely on their personal initiative. Prevention campaigns target companies, local authorities, sometimes professional branches, but rarely reach the millions of non-salaried workers who form a structural part of the French labor market. The silence is therefore not a matter of a single professional culture. It rests on the absence of an official space in which the health of these workers might be thought collectively.

The psychic question, in the entrepreneurial path, does not allow itself to be reduced to a variant of salaried burnout. It has its own mechanics, which specialized research is beginning to document with precision. The American psychiatrist Michael Freeman published in 2015, with Sheri Johnson and their team at the University of California, San Francisco, and at Berkeley, a study titled Are Entrepreneurs Touched with Fire?, whose results continue to be taken up in the specialized literature. In it he shows that company founders present, at levels significantly higher than the general population, mood disorders, forms of chronic anxiety, histories of depression. Entrepreneurship draws profiles already marked by these fragilities, tests them against conditions that aggravate them, and produces trajectories where professional success and psychic suffering coexist in a lasting way.

This coincidence is structural. The independent worker works in a regime of permanent uncertainty that no institution comes to absorb in his place. He carries alone the decisions whose consequences engage incomes, teams, a reputation. He works hours that exceed those of classic salaried employment, without the counterparts in terms of leave, compensatory rest, collective frameworks for decompression. He maintains with his activity a relationship of identity that makes the distinction between professional failure and personal failure difficult. When the company wavers, it is not only a project that is called into question. It is the image the individual has built of himself through this project.

Entrepreneurial burnout differs in this from salaried burnout. In the salaried framework, exhaustion generally comes from the collision between a workload and an organization that generates it without recognizing the individual limits of those who absorb it. The entrepreneur, by contrast, is at once the source of the load and the one who bears it. There exists, in his trajectory, no hierarchy to which the exhaustion can be imputed, nor any collective arrangement to call upon to have its legitimacy recognized. The diagnosis, when it comes, is often accompanied by a further sense of failure. The individual reproaches himself not only for having broken down, but for having arranged the conditions of his own collapse.

Platforms of psychological support intended for executives and founders are multiplying, in France as in the English-speaking world. Their growth testifies to a real awakening, but it also reveals a limit of the arrangement. These offerings remain largely tied to a market logic, where consulting a psychologist becomes a service to be bought in the same way as a software subscription or an accounting service. Access therefore remains unequal, the best-funded executives enjoying a support that independents in economic difficulty cannot afford, at the very moment when they would need it most.

The model of entrepreneurial wellness, which borrows heavily from Silicon Valley, also displaces the problem rather than treating it. The programs of meditation, of coaching, of optimization of sleep and performance, send the worker a further injunction to take charge of his own durability. They address him as a system of which he is the manager, and whose upkeep falls to him to ensure. This language, which means to be benevolent, extends the individual responsibilization that is already at the heart of the entrepreneurial regime. It asks the project leader to add to his duties that of preventing his own exhaustion, without questioning the conditions that produce this exhaustion.

The psychic question, in this context, cannot be thought of as an individual problem to be settled by individual solutions. It calls for a reflection on the very structure of independent activity. What forms of solidarity among peers can make up, even partly, for the absence of a work collective? What professional spaces can welcome speech about fatigue, depression, fear, without converting them into narratives of triumph? These questions go beyond entrepreneurs alone. They concern any society that multiplies the forms of work in which individual responsibility is growing and collective protection residual.

The ground on which Samasource deployed its activity, Kenya, Uganda, India, belongs to economies where the question of the social protection of independent workers arises in terms radically different from those that structure the European or North American debate. In these countries, informal work is not a blind spot of classic salaried employment. It is the rule. According to the work of the International Labour Organization, more than eighty-five percent of the working population of sub-Saharan Africa works in the informal economy, without a contract, without health cover, without retirement contributions. Serious illness, in these contexts, strikes individuals who have never enjoyed any net at all, and whose families are the only organized recourse should the body fail.

This situation, which the Western gaze often reads as a deficiency, has produced forms of mutualization that deserve to be examined for themselves. The West African tontines, the village mutual-aid associations of East Africa, the networks of community solidarity in South Asia, have for generations constituted mechanisms of protection that work without institutional mediation. Their effectiveness remains limited in the face of heavy pathologies, whose costs exceed what a family or village circle can absorb. Their existence is a reminder that social protection does not have as its sole model the great European public systems built in the twentieth century. Other forms, more fragmented, more horizontal, have existed and continue to exist, and the responses being built in these regions to the illness of independent workers often hybridize modern arrangements with these old organizational memories.

Rwanda has put in place since the mid-2000s a community health-insurance system, the Mutuelles de santé, which covers a majority of the population, informal workers included. Ghana has developed a National Health Insurance Scheme funded by a combination of value-added tax and contributions. Kenya, the historic ground of Samasource's establishment, has gradually extended its National Hospital Insurance Fund to workers in the informal sector, with mixed results but a real trajectory. These experiments are not finished models. They are laboratories where the question of the protection of independents against the health event is treated without presupposing the prior existence of a mass salariat, and they yield lessons that the Western economies of hybrid work would do well to examine.

India, where Samasource also employed a significant share of its workers, presents another textbook case. The country established in 2018 a national program named Ayushman Bharat, intended to cover the hospital expenses of five hundred million of the most modest people, a large majority of them informal workers. The architecture of this program, the difficulties of its implementation, the tensions between the federal level and the states, make it a precious ground of observation for anyone wishing to understand how a society builds health cover for populations that salaried employment has never encompassed. The difference from the European approach does not lie in a lesser ambition. It lies in a different starting point, which compels invention rather than adaptation.

The Danish case, often cited in French debates on flexicurity, deserves to be moved from its dominant position. It points to a very particular configuration, resting on a homogeneous labor market, high taxation, an unequaled culture of collective bargaining, which make it hard to transpose. The experiences of the countries of the global South, precisely because they start from less favorable conditions, offer lessons that more directly concern the societies where independent work develops without the whole system having the means to reorganize itself around it. The protection of the entrepreneur against illness is not a question that will be solved by the mere tracing of a Scandinavian model. It supposes a plurality of arrangements, hybrid, often unfinished, of which the Western economies would have an interest in seeing how they are pieced together elsewhere.

This decentering is not a theoretical exercise. It engages the way in which a society such as France, which carried to its highest the model of universal social protection tied to salaried employment, thinks the future of a labor market where independents represent a growing and lasting share of the working population. The responses emerging in Nairobi, Kampala, New Delhi or Kigali, imperfect though they are, work a matter close to the one with which the European systems are confronted. To ignore them amounts to depriving oneself of a practical intelligence whose value does not depend on the level of economic development of the countries that produced it.

There remains the figure with which this article opened. Leila Janah left behind a book, Give Work, published in 2017, in which she states with clarity the conviction that will have organized her whole activity. Work, when it is dignified, stable, paid at a level that allows one to live, constitutes in her view the only lasting lever out of poverty. Aid, even massive, even well-intentioned, treats symptoms. Work, for its part, transforms the position an individual occupies in the world. This thesis, which she defended before the World Economic Forum, in the boardrooms of Silicon Valley and as far as the slums of Nairobi, supposes a fundamental confidence in the capacity of the human body to produce, to create, to transform its environment through sustained effort. It rests, without ever putting it explicitly, on the assumption of an available body.

The illness that took her at thirty-seven removed that very availability from her. Epithelioid sarcoma, a slow cancer that often settles under the skin of a limb, gradually made impossible what her own framework of thought identified as the first condition of human dignity. In the post published on Instagram in November 2019, she writes explicitly that she had had to change her relationship to work. This is not a sentimental confession. It is the philosophical revision of a life that had built itself around a thesis, suddenly confronted with the situation that this thesis had left in the shadows. What happens when the worker's body withdraws? What becomes of the dignity built by work when work itself is no longer possible?

This question, which she did not have time to put publicly into the terms of an essay or a book, her final trajectory addresses to all those whose professional identity rests on individual production. The entrepreneur, the freelancer, the artist, the artisan, the independent in all its forms, share this foundation with her. They have made work the axis of their existence, and of their body the exclusive instrument of that work. When this instrument fails, they do not only discover that they are ill. They discover that their collective protection is thinner than they had reckoned, that the net they believed to be woven of their entourage, their peers, their savings, their insurance, lets through far more than it holds. They also discover, sometimes, that the culture in which they moved has made them incapable of putting their own collapse into words other than as a piece of learning.

What Janah's trajectory addresses to contemporary societies therefore has to do, first of all, neither with her company, nor with her model, nor even with the social impact she produced in Kenya, Uganda or India. It has to do with that moment, little documented, little glorified, when a woman of thirty-seven, seated in a hospital room in Manhattan, posts a few paragraphs to tell her loved ones and her public that she has ceased to be able to carry what she was carrying. This moment contains, in condensed form, the question that independent work poses to all the societies that organize themselves around it without having built the institutions that would make it possible to absorb its failures. The dignity she had made her own and had sought to pass on to others, she also sought, in all likelihood, in this public letter. She found it in a formulation that was no longer that of the fight, but that of withdrawal.

The history of ideas keeps a long tradition of thinkers whose last thought revises the first, and whose revision often weighs as much as the initial thesis. Janah did not have time to write the second book that might have deepened this revision. She left, in its place, a post of a few paragraphs, a company that continues without her, a foundation that bears her name, and the image of a woman who, despite the progression of her illness, went on reading stories to her stepdaughter and her godchildren. This image, soberly reported by those close to her, weighs in the balance of what she leaves us. It is a reminder that the dignity a work can confer does not necessarily go out when the work stops. Other forms persist, more discreet, less visible, which no longer belong to economic production and which nonetheless hold an existence together to its end.

The societies that want to think seriously about the protection of independents against illness would do well to meditate on this persistence. To build arrangements, adjust schemes, negotiate cover, all of this is necessary and the present article has not ceased to suggest it. The fact remains that no arrangement entirely fills the space opened by the question posed by Janah's trajectory. That of a professional identity grown so totalizing that it leaves few resources for existing otherwise when the body no longer follows. To work at loosening this totality, to make room in entrepreneurial lives for spaces that are not those of work, to recognize these spaces institutionally and collectively as legitimate, constitutes perhaps, beyond the mechanisms of social protection, the deepest undertaking a society can take on for its independents. Leila Janah, in her own way, had begun it in her last months. It remains to be carried on.
