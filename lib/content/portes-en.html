Every person we encounter is a continent of which we explore only one shore. A first impression is not a window opening onto someone. It is a door closed on everything else.

We think we know people. We think we have seen them, understood them, placed them. We have had an experience with them, sometimes just one, sometimes several, and we have drawn from it a conclusion that has hardened over time into certainty. But most of the time, we have not known the person. We have known the door through which we entered to discover them.

That door might be a first conversation, a professional context, a reputation that preceded them, a moment of crisis, a founding disagreement. It colours everything that follows, creates a filter through which we interpret every gesture, every word, every silence. And we do not see this filter. We believe we are looking at the person. We are looking at the filter.

Human beings are not characters. They are palimpsests, layered pages that the years have covered in contradictory writings without ever quite erasing the ones beneath. The man who is hard in business may be disarmingly tender with his children. The woman who seems distant at dinner parties may be the one who calls her friends in the middle of the night when they are going through something difficult. The quiet colleague everyone finds cold may write poetry. We do not know. We have never entered through that door.

The problem is not that we judge. Judging is a cognitive necessity, a way of imposing order on the chaos of human relationships. The problem is that we freeze. We take a snapshot of a living being and treat it as a definitive portrait. We mistake the first page for the whole book.

There are people we have never truly met despite years of proximity. And there are people we met in five minutes because we entered through the right door, the one that opens directly onto what is most real in them. This kind of encounter does not depend on time spent together. It depends on the angle of entry. Two people can share ten years of common existence and remain strangers. Two others may have had a single conversation and never quite leave one another.

What this reality demands of us is not naivety. It is something more difficult and more rare: the willingness to revise. To accept that what we have seen is not all there is to see. To remain curious about a person even when we believe we already know them. There is a form of humility in allowing oneself to think, from time to time, that perhaps we entered through the wrong door, and that behind the others, someone is still waiting to be discovered.

We pass by many people in a lifetime. Not because they were absent. Because we looked at their facade and believed we had seen their interior. And because no one ever taught us that knowing someone is a journey, not a destination.
