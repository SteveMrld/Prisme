Water: the next great
geopolitical fracture

From Himalayan glaciers to African dams. How the mastery of water is silently redrawing power relationships and heralding the conflicts of the century.

In 2021, satellite images revealed that Lake Poopó, Bolivia's second-largest lake, had virtually disappeared. Where fishermen had lived from their nets just decades earlier, nothing remained but expanses of white salt and abandoned villages. Ten thousand kilometers away, that same year, the Renaissance Dam in Ethiopia completed its second filling, reducing the flow of the Blue Nile by a fraction sufficient to alarm Cairo. Two apparently unrelated events. A single thread connecting them: water, a resource our civilizations have always considered a natural good, is becoming an instrument of power.

For millennia, human societies built themselves around rivers. The Nile made Egypt. The Tigris and Euphrates made Mesopotamia. The Indus gave rise to one of the earliest urban civilizations in history. These rivers nourished, transported, delimited. They gave. The twenty-first century inverts this relationship: societies now exhaust their rivers.

The water century, the thesis

Three converging forces make water strategic at an unprecedented scale. Demographic growth first: we will be ten billion by 2050, and each additional inhabitant is a water consumer: directly, through drinking and hygiene, and indirectly, through food. Agriculture accounts for 70% of global freshwater withdrawals; feeding two billion additional humans requires drawing more.1

Climate change next, which redistributes water more than it creates or destroys it. Some regions receive more precipitation, but in the form of extreme events that are difficult to capture. Others dry out durably. Glaciers, which function as natural water towers by storing winter precipitation as ice and progressively releasing it in summer, are retreating at documented rates. And the intensification of agriculture and industry, finally, which multiplies competing uses of a resource whose global stock does not grow.

The result is quantifiable. Four billion people experience water shortages for at least one month per year. Global demand is expected to increase by 20 to 30% by 2050. But these aggregate figures mask what is in reality the nature of the problem: water is not scarce on average; it is poorly distributed. And this distribution is, increasingly, a political question.

Transboundary basins, the new geography of power

The world has 310 transboundary river basins involving 153 states. These basins are the terrain of the most structural water tensions, because they create an irreducible geographic dependency: the upstream state controls the tap of the downstream state. This power relationship did not wait for the twenty-first century to exist, but it has intensified as upstream states acquired the technical capacity to mobilize it.

The architecture of these dependencies is often counter-intuitive. Turkey controls the sources of the Tigris and Euphrates, on which a large part of Iraqi and Syrian agriculture depends. China controls the plateaus from which the Indus, Brahmaputra, Mekong, Yangtze, and Yellow River originate: the water sources for nearly two billion people spread across India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Myanmar, Thailand, Cambodia, and Vietnam. Ethiopia controls 85% of the Blue Nile's flow, which constitutes Egypt's principal water source.2

In each of these cases, water geopolitics follows the same logic: the upstream state invests in dams, presents this as a sovereign right to development, and the downstream state experiences this decision as an existential threat. Water-sharing treaties, when they exist, were negotiated in climatic and demographic contexts no longer relevant today.

The Himalayas, water tower under pressure

The glaciers of the Himalayas and the Tibetan plateau constitute the world's largest freshwater reservoir outside the poles. They feed five of Asia's great rivers (the Indus, Ganges, Brahmaputra, Yangtze, and Mekong) which directly or indirectly sustain nearly two billion people. These glaciers function as natural regulators: they store winter precipitation as ice and release it progressively in summer, smoothing seasonal variations.

Climate warming is degrading this regulatory mechanism. A study published in Science in 2023 established that Himalayan glaciers are losing mass at a rate ten times higher than measured at the beginning of the twentieth century.3 In the short term, this accelerated melting produces floods and a deceptive abundance. In the medium term, when the glaciers are significantly reduced, summer flows will collapse, precisely when agricultural needs are highest.

China has controlled a large part of the Tibetan plateau since 1951. This geographic position confers a structural hydrological advantage that some analysts describe as a "hydraulic empire": the capacity to affect the water cycle of a dozen states by deciding where to build dams on its own rivers. Beijing has built on the upper Mekong a chain of eleven dams whose effects on downstream flows have been documented by the Stimson Center: between 2019 and 2020, a severe drought in Thailand, Laos, and Cambodia coincided with abnormally high levels in Chinese reservoirs.4

India-Pakistan, water between two nuclear powers

The Indus Waters Treaty signed in 1960 between India and Pakistan is often presented as a model of water diplomacy: it survived three wars, several major military crises, and decades of tension. Negotiated under World Bank auspices, it divides the waters of six rivers between the two countries along geographic lines: the eastern rivers to India, the western rivers to Pakistan.

But this treaty, designed for a different climatic and demographic context, is showing its limits. India has multiplied dam projects on rivers allocated to Pakistan, which Islamabad interprets as violations of the treaty's spirit. Climate change alters flows outside the assumptions on which the sharing was calculated. And the political relationship between the two countries is regularly strained to the point where the waters of the Indus become a tool of diplomatic pressure.5

The stakes extend beyond the bilateral question. If the Indus Waters Treaty, the most robust of transboundary water-sharing agreements, were to collapse, it would be a major signal for global water governance: it would mean that even the best-constructed agreements cannot withstand the combined pressures of climate change and geopolitical competition.

The Middle East, where water is worth more than oil

Jordan has approximately 60 cubic meters of renewable freshwater per person per year. The UN-defined threshold for absolute scarcity is 500 cubic meters. This ratio (one eighth of the minimum) places Jordan among the most water-poor countries in the world, in a region that concentrates several of the most critical situations on the planet.

Gulf states have responded to this constraint through large-scale technology: seawater desalination. Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, and Kuwait now produce a significant share of their drinking water from the sea. These installations represent a considerable strategic investment, but they are energy-intensive, concentrated on coastlines, and vulnerable. A targeted strike on major Saudi or Emirati desalination facilities would deprive millions of people of drinking water within hours. This vulnerability is not theoretical; it is documented in regional military risk analyses.6

The Israeli-Palestinian conflict contains a water dimension systematically underestimated in conventional geopolitical analysis. Access to water in the West Bank is regulated by agreements dating from the Oslo Accords, whose revision mechanisms were never activated. The Mountain Aquifer, shared between Israel and the Palestinian Territories, is the subject of documented competition in several UNEP reports.

Africa, water as a crisis multiplier

The Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) is Africa's largest hydroelectric plant. Its construction, begun in 2011 and completed in 2020, is presented by Addis Ababa as an act of economic sovereignty: Ethiopia, one of the world's poorest countries, intends to use its hydrological advantage to electrify and develop itself. The Nile is its river. It has the right to dam it.

Egypt sees things differently. 90% of its water comes from the Nile. Nilotic agriculture feeds a population of more than one hundred million people in a country where 95% of the territory is desert. The filling of the GERD, even partially slowed, is perceived in Cairo as an existential threat. President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi has publicly declared that the waters of the Nile are a "red line." The trilateral Ethiopia-Egypt-Sudan negotiations have yet to produce any binding agreement.7

In the west of the continent, Lake Chad illustrates another mechanism. In fifty years, its surface has decreased by 90% under the combined effect of climate change and agricultural withdrawals. This progressive disappearance has disrupted the pastoral and agricultural economies of four countries (Chad, Niger, Nigeria, Cameroon) that depended on its resources. Researchers in the ECOWAS network document a direct correlation between the lake's shrinkage, internal migrations, and the instability that enabled Boko Haram's expansion in the region.8 Water did not cause jihadism. But its disappearance created the conditions for its spread.

The invisible war over water

Water conflicts do not resemble conventional wars. There are no declarations, no fronts, no peace treaties. They take diffuse forms: sabotage of hydraulic infrastructure, manipulation of flows to weaken a downstream adversary, coercive diplomacy that conditions water sharing on political concessions, economic pressure on dependent states.

The Pacific Institute, the reference organization for cataloguing water-related conflicts, has documented more than 400 violent events linked to water in a single recent year. These events range from irrigation canal sabotage in local rural conflicts to attacks on pumping stations in civil wars, to the use of dams as weapons of war.9 In Syria, control of the Tabqa dam on the Euphrates was an explicit military objective for the Islamic State, then for Kurdish and American forces. In Ukraine, the Kakhovka dam on the Dnieper was destroyed in 2023, depriving hundreds of thousands of people of drinking water.

This "hydro-cold war" is compounded by a silent competition for underground aquifers: those fossil water tables that accumulate over millennia and empty over decades. Saudi Arabia depleted a large portion of its fossil reserves to produce wheat in the 1980s and 2000s before abandoning this policy. Pakistan is pumping its aquifers at a rate that, according to projections from the Pakistan Council of Research in Water Resources, will lead to their exhaustion in several Punjab regions by 2025-2030.

The new water powers, and what happens to the others

Facing these pressures, some states have developed water mastery that constitutes a real strategic advantage. Israel is the most documented case: confronted since its founding with structural scarcity, it developed a technological and institutional ecosystem that makes it a water power despite its aridity. The country recycles approximately 90% of its wastewater for agricultural irrigation, a rate without global equivalent. It has perfected large-scale desalination, drip irrigation, and precision hydrological modeling.10

Singapore, a 730 km² island with no aquifer or river, today produces part of its water from rain, wastewater recycling ("NEWater"), and desalination. This "four taps" strategy has allowed it to reduce its dependence on Malaysia, whose water supply treaties historically constituted a geopolitical vulnerability. The Netherlands, a large part of whose territory lies below sea level, has developed world-class expertise in water management that has become an economic and diplomatic export.

These successes share a common feature: they require massive long-term public investment, robust institutional governance, and a political culture capable of treating water as a strategic common good rather than a commodity. These are the capacities lacking in the states most exposed to water stress. The concept of water bankruptcy (a state where demand permanently exceeds available resources without an adjustment mechanism) does not threaten states that have anticipated. It threatens those that have not had the means, the time, or the political will.

The most documented projections identify several basins as candidates for this collapse in the coming decades: the Pakistani Indus, the Jordan, the aquifers of northern India, the Colorado in the United States, and several regions of North Africa. In each of these cases, water bankruptcy is not an event; it is a process: slow, at first invisible, then suddenly irreversible.

Prospective, What comes next

Three dynamics for the decades ahead

The militarization of water. Hydraulic infrastructure (dams, pumping stations, irrigation networks) will become increasingly explicit military targets in regional conflicts. The destruction of the Kakhovka dam in 2023 set a precedent. The absence of a binding international legal framework on the protection of these infrastructures in wartime is a gap that malicious actors will continue to exploit.

Water diplomacy as a power tool. States that control the sources of major transboundary rivers possess a lever that their downstream neighbors cannot ignore. This structural asymmetry will deepen as climate change makes water resources more variable and more precious. China, Turkey, and Ethiopia are the actors who best master this instrument.

Water as a migration trigger. UNHCR and IOM models project that water stress will be one of the principal drivers of major migrations in the second half of the century. People will not flee "water" itself, but the collapse it triggers: agriculture failing, cities becoming uninhabitable, states losing their governing capacity.

The twenty-first century will probably not be dominated by direct wars over water: open conflicts over a shared resource are rare in history, even if the tensions it generates can fuel other conflicts. But water will become the invisible thread connecting several of the century's great crises: food, climate, migration, political. Global geopolitics will no longer play out only around straits and pipelines. It will also play out around glaciers, dams, and aquifers: where power is built in silence, and where its collapse can be sudden.
