The distorting mirror, identity and self-image

Aitana Lopez is a model who does not exist. Generated by artificial intelligence, she accumulates hundreds of thousands of followers, collaborations with international brands, five-figure monthly revenues. Her body is perfect because she has no body. Her skin is smooth because she has no skin. She is the finished version of a phenomenon that had begun well before generative AI, with the first Instagram filters.

The problem is not aesthetic. It is epistemic: when millions of users, a large proportion of them teenagers, move through a visual environment populated by filtered, augmented, retouched, and now entirely generated bodies, their calibration of what a real body looks like becomes miscalibrated. Studies conducted notably by Boston University have documented that some teenagers are considering cosmetic surgery to resemble their own retouched photos, something plastic surgeons have termed "Snapchat dysmorphia."3 The filter has become the reference. Reality has become the problem.

The platforms did not create polarization. They built the infrastructure that makes it profitable. That is an important distinction, and a far heavier responsibility.

This distortion is part of a broader phenomenon that Daniel Boorstin had anticipated as early as 1962 in The Image: the hypertrophy of artifice and simulacrum in a consumer society where "appearing" structurally takes precedence over "being."4 What the platforms did was to industrialize this logic until it became the air that younger generations breathe.

The normalization of hateful speech

Amnesty International analyzed more than 228,000 tweets sent to 778 female politicians or journalists in the United Kingdom and United States over one year. The result: a woman receives an abusive message every thirty seconds. Women of color are disproportionately targeted.5 These figures are not accidents; they are the predictable consequence of architectures that amplify extreme content.

A study conducted by researchers at the University of Manitoba established the typical profile of online harassers: narcissism, Machiavellianism, psychopathic and sadistic tendencies. Their attacks are gratuitous, without apparent reason.6 What makes them structurally different from harassers of other eras is the sense of impunity conferred by the screen, and the unlimited resonance chamber provided by the platforms.

This phenomenon does not stop at individual abuse. It erodes something deeper: the very possibility of adversarial debate. When any public position exposes one to organized harassment campaigns, when visibility becomes a risk, an increasing proportion of qualified voices withdraws from the digital public sphere. What remains is not more representative of society; it is more extreme, louder, more willing to cause harm.

Young people, involuntary experimental subjects

The consequences for adolescents constitute the most documented and most troubling dossier. Meta faces lawsuits in several American states for features judged deliberately addictive on Facebook and Instagram, promoting compulsive use that is harmful to the education and sleep of younger users. Visual filters are specifically implicated for their role in the progression of eating disorders.7

TikTok, in an internal investigation made public against its will, acknowledged that its algorithms guided vulnerable users toward increasingly extreme content, a phenomenon also documented by researcher Jonathan Haidt, whose work establishes a correlation between smartphone adoption and the measurable deterioration of American teenage girls' mental health from 2012 onward.8

In Silicon Valley, many executives and engineers who helped build these platforms limit or ban screens for their own children. This asymmetry, designing products for other people's children that one refuses to one's own, has not gone unnoticed. It says something essential about what these actors know about what they have built.

The legal lock and its consequences

The regulation of platforms runs up against a fundamental obstacle. In Europe, the 2000 directive and the French law of 2004 granted social networks the status of host rather than publisher, a distinction that considerably reduces their responsibility for distributed content. The general terms of use of most platforms also place the legal relationship under Californian law and the First Amendment of the American Constitution, which guarantees a far broader freedom of expression than in Europe.9

This lock is not an accidental anomaly. It has been actively defended by the platforms during every legislative attempt to address it. The European Digital Services Act constitutes the most ambitious attempt to partially lift it, imposing moderation and algorithmic transparency obligations on very large platforms. Its early effects are measurable but insufficient: transparency obligations have not produced the algorithm disclosure that regulators hoped for.

What remains to be done, and why it is difficult

Norwegian researcher Andy Oxman conducted experiments in Ugandan schools on critical thinking education. His conclusions are counterintuitive: children resist misinformation better than adults, because they are less inclined to defend pre-existing beliefs. This cognitive plasticity of young people is simultaneously a vulnerability, they absorb everything, and a resource: they can learn to discriminate, to question, to verify. But this education requires a political will that school systems have not yet translated into systematic pedagogical practices.10

The spirit of the internet pioneers, born of the libertarian counterculture of the 1970s, convinced that universal connection would produce universal emancipation, seems today to belong to another century. Not because that spirit was wrong. It simply had no theory of conflict. It imagined rational humans in open spaces, without modeling what the attention economy would do, without anticipating recommendation algorithms, without seeing that freedom of expression without an architecture of deliberation produces less collective wisdom than organized noise.

Marshall McLuhan promised in 1967 the advent of a global village created by new technologies. That village exists. The question is whether its inhabitants want to live in it, or whether they still have the choice to leave.
