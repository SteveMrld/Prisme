Marilyn Séphocle never sought the spotlight. Perhaps that is why she lasted.

Born in Martinique, a doctor in Germanic studies from New York University, holder of an MBA from Howard University and of a master's in international policy from Johns Hopkins, she settled in Washington in the 1980s and never left. She joined the faculty of Howard University, the most prestigious historically Black university in the United States, where she built from scratch a program of simultaneous interpretation in twelve languages, training generations of students in world diplomacy over decades. She herself speaks nine languages.

In 1991, she joined a small group of Democrats supporting a young couple of provincial lawyers named Bill and Hillary Clinton. She would write to her parents that they would probably not win, but that she was supporting them because they reminded her of the Kennedys. In 1995, she founded the Women Ambassadors Foundation and the annual conference that bears that name, gathering each year in Washington women ambassadors from around the world. She took Kamala Harris, then a mere student at Howard, to demonstrate in front of the South African embassy for the release of Nelson Mandela.

Today the president of IDEAS Inc., a consulting firm whose clients include African governments, the World Bank, the African Union and French institutions, she observes America with the lucidity of one who has known it from within for forty-eight years, without ever having ceased to be something else.

Soara met with her.

You left Martinique for Washington more than thirty years ago. At what moment did this city cease to be a destination and become an anchor?

Before settling in Washington, I spent eight formative years in New York, where I was a student at New York University. The city had an indescribable character: a constant teeming of opportunities and encounters, a place where anything could happen and where the sky seemed to have no limit. One had to adapt very quickly, however, especially at a young age, to an effervescent and demanding way of life.

Having come initially for a year to perfect my English, I stayed eight years. During that time, I studied English and then German, while working at the United Nations.

When I left New York for Washington, the latter became a true anchor for me. It offered everything I wanted: a city on a human scale, far more peaceful than New York, endowed with a welcoming architecture that evoked both Paris and the West Indies.

From the first moments, I was won over. Washington became a home from the moment I met one of my mentors, Dr. Nichols. He made me understand that I not only had my place here, but also a mission to accomplish in the United States. Another anchor was Dr. Anne-Marie Hogart, a Haitian professor of French at the University of the District of Columbia, deeply devoted to the Haitian community.

Another key element of my path is Howard University, an institution historically dedicated to the African American community. After the abolition of slavery, Black Americans remained excluded from the traditional universities, which led to the creation of establishments such as Howard, founded by African Americans as well as by committed white people. It is a rigorous university, of recognized prestige. In Martinique, I often read the magazine Ebony, which, in a fascinating article, compared Howard to Harvard. Howard occupies a singular place in the history of the United States, like Washington in the political and cultural life of the nation. Many important figures were trained there.

Finally, Washington was always a city dear to my parents, above all to my mother, sensitive to its cultural richness and its serenity.

You taught at Howard University for decades, at the heart of Black American memory, as a Martinican. Two experiences of the Black world, two radically different histories. What did this crossing reveal to you?

I teach at Howard, where I have held a chair for many years. This university occupies a particular place in the intellectual and cultural history of the United States. It is the university of Kenneth Kaunda, of Mandela's children, of the first Black mayor of New York, David Dinkins, of the first woman vice-president of the United States, Kamala Harris, of inventors such as Charles Drew or Alice Parker, who devised central heating. It is also that of great names of American cinema, Phylicia Rashad, Taraji Henson, Chadwick Boseman, and of intellectuals such as Alain Locke, Toni Morrison, Léon-Gontran Damas, Nikole Hannah-Jones or Ta-Nehisi Coates. It constitutes one of the great centers of the production of knowledge on the African American experience and has trained several generations of researchers and activists who marked the struggle for civil rights.

As a Martinican, my view of the Black world is broader than a single national experience. I am a Black Caribbean woman, of African descent within the American frame as within the French and European frame, and my path was built among several spaces. I have lived longer in the United States than in Martinique, to the point that some perceive me as an African American. Yet I was born in Martinique, I grew up there, and I have always kept a deep bond with my island. This dual belonging allows me to observe Martinican society and American society from within as from without. That is, in my eyes, a privilege.

The history of Black Americans and that of the West Indians are often presented as two radically different realities. I do not share this view. There are of course important differences, linked to the national contexts, to the languages, to the cultures and to the political trajectories proper to each society. But these histories belong to one and the same continuum, that of the post-slavery societies of the Americas. The experiences of Black Americans, of West Indians, of Brazilians or of Afro-Latin Americans are not identical, and each of these peoples has developed its own vision of the world. Yet, beyond the national specificities, one discovers an astonishingly vast common ground.

The more I live in the United States, the more I am struck by the deep similarities between African Americans and the peoples of the Caribbean. They are not limited to a history marked by slavery and its consequences. They are expressed in gesture, in attitudes, in the ways of celebrating joys or facing ordeals. One finds similar conceptions of family and community: in many African American as in West Indian families, the elders occupy a central place, guardians of the collective memory. The resemblances appear even in eating habits, inherited from the survival strategies of the enslaved populations. Thus, the high prevalence of arterial hypertension in several Black communities of the Americas has been linked by many researchers to the historical influence of diets very rich in salt, those salted foods or foods preserved in salt that the populations reduced to bondage received.

This closeness is explained by a shared history. These are peoples who inherited the trauma of slavery and had to evolve in societies where they were often marginalized or relegated to the periphery of power. They developed comparable forms of resilience and resistance in the face of systems built around racial hierarchies close to veritable caste systems. That is why I am convinced that the resemblances among the Black communities of the Americas are more numerous and deeper than their differences.

The American specificity nonetheless remains real. After the Civil War, the Thirteenth Amendment, ratified in December 1865, abolished slavery, except in cases of incarceration. There then opened the Reconstruction, during which the federal government tried to integrate the former slaves into civic life: the Fourteenth Amendment granted citizenship to anyone born in the United States, the Fifteenth prohibited in theory any racial discrimination in the exercise of the right to vote, and African Americans were elected to public office. But the withdrawal of federal troops from the South, in 1877, allowed the white southern elites to regain control and to install the segregation known as "Jim Crow," which would endure until the civil rights movement of the 1950s and 1960s. The Supreme Court legitimized it in 1896, in the Plessy v. Ferguson ruling, by enshrining the principle of "separate but equal," while the infrastructures reserved for African Americans remained far inferior.

The exclusion was not only educational or social, it was political and carceral. Poll taxes, literacy tests and so-called "grandfather" clauses served to keep African Americans away from the ballot box. From the 1870s, several southern states leased prisoners to private companies, to mines, to plantations: this system of convict leasing struck African Americans very disproportionately, often arrested for minor reasons, vagrancy or absence of a work contract, before being leased as a labor force almost deprived of rights. To this was added extrajudicial violence: between the end of the nineteenth century and the middle of the twentieth, several thousand African Americans were victims of lynchings. Faced with these violences, more than six million of them left the South between 1916 and 1970, during the Great Migration.

Abolition did not, then, mean equality. And the observation holds for all the post-slavery societies, from the West Indies to Brazil, from Colombia to the United States: an unfinished freedom. Concretely, this translates into limited access to bank credit and to property, into discrimination in employment, into police violence, into the poor academic streaming of children at school, into gentrification and the displacement of populations. It is to bring these realities into dialogue that in 1991 I created the Afro-European African American Forum, which allows me to invite each year to Washington, to the Black Caucus, elected officials and figures from the Overseas territories so that they meet their African American counterparts.

You founded the Women Ambassadors Foundation in 1995. Thirty years later, women hold more diplomatic posts. Has power changed, or only the faces that exercise it?

Originally, I founded the Women Ambassadors Foundation in 1995 starting from an observation: there were very few women diplomats, and few Black diplomats, within the State Department. At the first Women Ambassadors Conference, which I organized that year, there were only five women ambassadors. They are today forty-seven out of one hundred seventy-six in Washington, and one makes the same observation in other great capitals, from Bonn to Paris, where our foundation also organized conferences.

In the 1990s, very few countries trusted women to represent them abroad. Some even argued that the function was not compatible with motherhood, that it was too demanding for a woman supposed to remain constantly available. There were all sorts of quibbles and excuses. Then a few women ambassadors arrived on the international scene, and one realized one thing, which I developed in my book Then They Were Twelve: the embassies led by women functioned not only very well, but stood out on certain points. These women assumed their role of ambassador and, in a certain way, the one traditionally associated with the ambassador's wife, attentive to the way things were presented and placing the emphasis on the situation of women in their societies.

When I called the State Department to organize activities and ask for lists of names, I was systematically asked whether it was the ambassadors' wives or the women ambassadors themselves. This confusion persisted for years, so strange did the idea that a woman could represent her country appear. Yet, in the 1990s, these women represented their country brilliantly, sometimes arousing a kind of jealousy in their male counterparts, simply because, being few, they were particularly visible in the eyes of the American press.

Diplomatic power resides of course at the level of presidents, prime ministers and foreign ministers. But the ambassador has a margin of action, and when the ambassador is a woman, it is not only a change of the face of power: it is another way of conceiving diplomacy. To represent one's country is to represent it in its totality. Male diplomats often speak of the population in a global way; women more spontaneously integrate an attention to the place of women in the economy, industry, trade. That changes the game, for many studies show that when women flourish fully, society as a whole fares better.

This year, the Women Ambassadors Foundation celebrates its thirty years. During this period, we have encouraged many women to embrace diplomatic careers, carried out advocacy work with states, created internships in embassies, organized debates with the multilateral institutions and presented, until recently, lists of potential candidates to the State Department. And we accomplished all of this with limited means.

You have been close to Hillary Clinton since the early 1990s. In 2016, she became the first woman to win the nomination of a major American party, the Democratic Party, before losing to Donald Trump. In hindsight, how did you experience that defeat, and what did it teach you about America?

In 1991, Aurelia Brazeal, Bill Clinton's campaign director, advised me to get closer to a young couple who needed support for their presidential campaign. So I went to the headquarters, at the corner of 21st Street and M Street. They were both sitting cross-legged, surrounded by some twenty young people. Flyers littered the floor, everyone called one another by their first name, and at the center of the room there were pizzas. The atmosphere was good-natured. Bill had a very Kennedyesque air, Hillary never parted with her smile and her energy. They welcomed me with open arms. I shared with them my fears of not convincing the American electorate, with my slight foreign accent and my brand-new nationality. Bill retorted with a great burst of laughter: "Americans come in all accents, all races, all colors and all persuasions." That very evening, I wrote to my parents: "I am taking part in the presidential campaign of a certain Bill Clinton. He surely will not win. But I am having a wild time. He and his wife are very likable." His opponent, Bush senior, from an immensely rich Texan family, had just given Americans a resounding victory in the Gulf War, and the polls seemed assured to him. I could not conceive that the candidate for the presidency of a superpower could be so approachable, so different from Europe and so many other nations. The rest belongs to history. On the evening of the inauguration, at the Arkansas ball, we were a small group backstage with Bill and Hillary, and we pinched our arms, so unreal did this victory seem to us.

Years later, facing Trump, the scenario reversed itself: victory seemed within reach. Before Hillary, Shirley Chisholm, of Caribbean origin, had been in 1972 the first woman to seek the nomination of a major party. But Hillary Clinton remains the first to have won it in a major and competitive political context.

The 2016 election was, for a large part of Democratic opinion, a profound shock. Not only by its result, but because it contradicted a trajectory perceived as progressive. Hillary Clinton appeared as a candidate of experience, who had sat in the Senate and led American diplomacy as Secretary of State, mastered her files and dominated the debates on the technical level. Yet she loses in the Electoral College, while winning the popular vote. This discrepancy reinforced the idea of a politically and culturally fragmented America.

During the preceding decades, from the 1990s to the Obama era, the United States had put forward an image of modernity in which the access of women to the highest responsibilities passed for a marker of democratic progress. Many considered the election of a woman to the presidency not only possible, but almost inevitable. 2016 qualified, even contradicted, this optimistic reading, revealing deep cleavages around gender, identity and political representation. Hillary Clinton herself used the expression "basket of deplorables" to describe a portion of Trump's voters, a controversial formula but revealing of the polarization of the moment.

From the point of view of the social sciences, this election brought to light very differentiated electoral behaviors. About 55 percent of white women voted for Donald Trump, while African American women voted nearly 94 percent for Hillary Clinton. Angela Davis traced the genesis of this rift between white and Black women to the history of the suffragist movement, between 1840 and 1920. These gaps show that the female electorate cannot be analyzed as a homogeneous bloc. These dynamics are not peculiar to the United States, but they take on a particular intensity there, in a country shaped by relations of race, class and gender. Other countries have already had women leaders, which raises questions about the American specificity in the representation of female power. At bottom, 2016 acted as a revealer: that of a nation torn between an identity founded on the idea of progress and a far more contradictory electoral reality.

You took Kamala Harris to demonstrate in front of the South African embassy when she was a student at Howard. Thirty years later, she found herself a candidate for the presidency of the United States, propelled in haste after Joe Biden's withdrawal, against Donald Trump. America decided without hesitation. What does this verdict say about the country you have observed for forty-eight years?

Yes, Kamala Harris was my student in 1986. It was the time when I was preparing my doctoral thesis at New York University while teaching at Howard. I encouraged my students to engage in the great debates of their time, and many took part in the demonstrations in front of the South African embassy against apartheid, which would not end until the early 1990s, with the release of Nelson Mandela in 1990 and the first multiracial elections of 1994. Kamala Harris has herself since evoked the importance that certain teachers had in awakening her to questions of social justice. At the time, I was the age of my students: we shared the same concerns and the same sense of urgency.

It is difficult to measure how much the American political landscape has changed. In the 1980s, the very idea that an African American might one day reach the presidency seemed distant; many young people thought they would never see it in their lifetime. The election of Barack Obama in 2008 upended this perception.

To understand the defeat of Kamala Harris against Donald Trump in 2024, one must recall the context. After Joe Biden's withdrawal in the summer of 2024, she had only one hundred and seven days to build a national campaign, while Trump had occupied the media stage for nearly a decade. Despite this, she managed to mobilize a large part of the Democratic electorate and to wage a competitive campaign right up to the vote.

Her defeat surprised many observers. Trump's legal troubles and the controversies of his past passed for decisive obstacles to his reelection. It was nothing of the kind. To understand this result, one must go beyond simplistic explanations. The economic factors played a part: a portion of the electorate placed inflation and the cost of living at the heart of the vote, and Trump managed to convince them that a billionaire would be better placed to respond to it. But the cultural factors weighed just as much. The idea of a woman at the head of the executive continues to arouse resistance in certain segments of the population, as many studies attest. These reluctances are limited neither to one community nor to one origin: they run through society as a whole in various forms. I have often observed that power remains spontaneously associated with male figures, and that these representations sometimes influence electoral choices unconsciously, even when voters invoke mostly economic considerations.

The results also showed the diversity of behaviors within the African American electorate. Black women figured among the most strongly mobilized groups for Kamala Harris, while about 20 percent of Black men made more varied choices, sometimes reflecting a form of ambient machismo. Moreover, certain aspects of contemporary popular culture maintain demeaning representations of women, in particular Black women, and help to shape the environment in which the campaigns unfold.

My relationship with Kamala Harris was not built in a constant closeness. I knew her as a student, then our paths separated: she pursued her law studies before the career we know, district attorney of San Francisco, attorney general of California, senator, then vice-president. I nonetheless crossed her path again over the years, hearing her evoke her youthful memories, seeing her return to Howard as a senator and then as vice-president, meeting her sister on several occasions. For those of us who knew the America of the 1980s, to see a former African American student become vice-president remains a historic event. That her candidacy aroused so many hopes while revealing the country's deep divisions recalls how much the questions of race, gender and power remain central in the contemporary history of the United States.

The Civil War was never truly settled, it was suspended. Would Donald Trump then be not a rupture but a return, the resurgence of a tension that has run through this country since its origins? Do you share this reading?

Entirely, I share this reading of the history of the United States.

The Civil War was never truly settled. A former student of mine, the writer Ta-Nehisi Coates, explains in one of his works that it has often been presented as a conflict between gentlemen from which no one really emerged victor or vanquished, a way of softening its scope and its consequences. In my eyes, the Civil War was, to a certain extent, badly managed by the victors. After the Union's victory, there was no break with the social and political structures that had supported slavery: a compromise with the South was sought rather than a deep transformation of society.

American history is, moreover, dotted with compromises between North and South. One may think of the Missouri Compromise of 1820, four decades before the war, which illustrates this constant will to preserve the balance between the two regions. The Thirteenth Amendment itself, by maintaining an exception in cases of incarceration, furnished the white southern elites with the lever of the Jim Crow laws, conceived to criminalize Black people and prolong slavery in another form. These compromises also rested on the economic and family ties that bound the elites of the two camps: it was not rare for one and the same family to hold interests in the plantations of the South and in the industry of the North, and despite the war, these connections endured.

Even after the North's victory, many symbols of the Confederacy were allowed to subsist. The Confederate flag continued to be flown in the public space of several southern states, and its memory remained anchored in certain communities. Instead of looking clearly at slavery as a moral and historical tragedy, certain circles maintained a nostalgic memory of it, right down to those reenactments, those reconstructions in which one dons period uniforms to replay the battles. Through these practices a hierarchized vision of society was perpetuated, a caste culture inherited from the slave system that continued to influence social relations well after abolition. Strikingly, many European immigrants settled in the South sometimes adopted these representations: in a society structured by racial hierarchies, the newcomers were encouraged to identify with the dominant group rather than with the marginalized populations.

In recent years, citizen movements have called this heritage into question. In several cities, statues of Confederate leaders have been removed or relocated, notably after the demonstrations that followed the death of George Floyd. In this perspective, Trump does not appear as a rupture, but as the reactivation of old tensions. He gives certain voters the feeling that ideas or symbols marginalized since the civil rights era can again be expressed openly. For his supporters, it is a matter of "defending a cultural or historical identity"; for his critics, he legitimizes nationalist and racially exclusive discourses. His posture becomes dangerous when he demands that American museums, in particular the African American Museum in Washington, present not facts, but a watered-down version of slavery.

"The Civil War is not entirely over."

The racial question remains one of the deepest lines of fracture in American society. In this sense, yes, one can consider that the Civil War is not entirely over. Not on the battlefield, of course, but in memories, in collective representations and in the political conflicts that still run through the country.

The election of Obama perhaps gave the illusion of an America reconciled with itself without resolving its deep fractures. Trump won in 2016 after two Obama terms, lost in 2020 to Joe Biden, won again in 2024 against Kamala Harris. If the Democratic terms had lived up to their promises, could Trump have won twice? Do the Obama and Biden presidencies bear a share of responsibility for what followed?

For most Americans, the presidency of Barack Obama remains associated with a period of relative institutional stability and of renewal of the national narrative. His two terms, a veritable breath of oxygen and hope for the United States as for the rest of the world, are also perceived as a moment when the deep contradictions of American society were both brought to light and left largely unresolved.

Obama enters the White House in January 2009, in the aftermath of the financial crisis of 2008 and of the most serious recession since the 1930s. His administration applies itself first to stabilizing a weakened economic system while restoring the country's international credibility. The recovery is real, carried by the creation of millions of jobs, and the Affordable Care Act, "Obamacare," achieves the most important extension of health coverage in decades. The Dodd-Frank Act, for its part, strengthened financial regulation in the wake of the collapse of the major banking institutions. On the political level, Obama embodies an unprecedented coalition: he obtains about 92 percent of the African American vote, large majorities among Latino, Asian and Native American voters, and nearly 40 percent of the white vote. His election in 2008, then his reelection in 2012, mark a deep reconfiguration of American electoral dynamics.

But this transformation also brings to light structural tensions. For some, the Obama presidency symbolizes the advent of a "post-racial" America; for others, it reveals the persistence of inequalities that political representation alone does not suffice to resolve. Migration policy is the example of this: despite his commitments in favor of a reform, the Obama administration oversees a significant number of deportations, which complicates his legacy among a part of the Latino electorate. His presidency can therefore be read both as a historic moment of symbolic progress and as a partial management of the country's fractures.

Joe Biden takes office in January 2021 in a wholly different context, marked by the effects of the Covid-19 pandemic, the disruption of the labor market and the tensions on the supply chains. Like Obama in 2009, he inherits a weakened economy. His term is carried by a vast recovery plan, the American Rescue Plan, then by the Inflation Reduction Act, one of the most important climate and industrial investments in American history. Like Obama's, it is nonetheless run through by a constant tension between substantial legislative successes and a lasting polarization: despite the policies implemented, the country remains profoundly divided.

This continuity raises questions: have the successive Democratic administrations, despite their achievements, been able to treat the structural roots of polarization? It is in this frame that the recent sequence is inscribed, the election of Trump in 2016, his defeat in 2020, his return in 2024. More than a linear trajectory, American political life seems to function by cycles of mobilization and reaction, each administration responding to the tensions that it itself helps to reinforce.

At the heart of this reading is the capacity of the United States to settle the legacy of slavery and segregation. The Thirteenth Amendment, by abolishing slavery while authorizing forced labor in cases of penal conviction, carries an ambiguity widely discussed in contemporary analyses of the carceral system. The United States, it is argued, never fully rebuilt its social order after emancipation, nor even after civil rights: it transformed the forms of inequality without abolishing its structures. The Obama presidency appears, then, less as the culmination of a reconciliation than as a powerful symbol of progress superimposed on unresolved tensions. The central question is not only that of the success of the Obama or Biden presidencies, but that of knowing whether modern administrations truly have the instruments necessary to resolve historical fractures as deep as these.

The sociologist William Julius Wilson showed that American deindustrialization had broken whole communities well before Trump mobilized them. In France, Christophe Guilluy had mapped the same fracture: a peripheral country that the metropolitan elites had ceased to see, and that the Rassemblement National ended by capturing by seizing the working-class vote historically won by the left. On both sides of the Atlantic, the same abandonment, the same reversal. Does this parallel speak to you?

Yes, this parallel is very pertinent. It brings to light structural transformations that go well beyond the electoral conjunctures.

Across the whole Western world, deindustrialization has brought about a massive displacement of productive activities toward Asia and other destinations. This globalization of the chains of production has offered the great companies considerable advantages, a less costly labor force, an optimization of costs, an increased integration of markets. But it has also unbalanced the industrial societies, provoking the disappearance of working-class jobs, the weakening of whole regions and the lasting fragilization of certain social fabrics. Automation and robotization have accelerated the substitution of human labor in new sectors, and for a part of the working classes, the Western economies have offered fewer stable prospects.

One has thus witnessed a kind of unbinding between capital and territory. Where industrial capitalism was rooted in national spaces, with implicit forms of social compromise, it has become more mobile, more global, in part deterritorialized. This shift has left a great political and symbolic void: the old mediations, unions, mass parties, workers' organizations, have weakened without being fully replaced.

It is in this context that far-right discourses have gained an audience, by proposing more identitarian readings of the economic transformations. Nationalism presents itself as a simple response to a complex and lasting social and economic insecurity. In this recomposition, immigrant populations and visible minorities are sometimes designated as responsible for the structural difficulties, whereas they are themselves the victims of them. Another displacement of public debate concerns the growing place of cultural and identity issues, in particular around gender. In certain media spaces, these questions have such visibility that they eclipse the fundamental economic debates, and often distorted assertions circulate, for example on gender transition at school, feeding very polarized representations. The force of these narratives lies not so much in their veracity as in their capacity to offer simple explanations for profoundly complex phenomena. This drift has intensified, culminating in the European Parliament's recent vote for a contested reform that hardens the Union's migration policy and opens the way to "return centers" in third countries. Carried by a coalition of the far right, the right and some who call themselves centrists, the result marks a reversal of values; the cries of "send them back" heard in the chamber echo the slogan of Trump's supporters toward immigrants.

At the same time, the concentration of economic and media power in the hands of large groups exacerbates this simplified nationalist discourse. The energy, extractive or digital industries play a central role in the contemporary economies, while weighing indirectly on the frameworks of political debate. One finds oneself before a structural tension: on one side a globalized and deterritorialized economy, on the other discourses founded on closure, the designation of culprits or the restoration of an order that recalls the 1930s. On both sides of the Atlantic, there is more convergence than divergence: deindustrialization, globalization and the recomposition of the political systems produce similar social fractures, and therefore similar political responses.

You have lived in the United States for more than thirty years, but you look at France with the lucidity of distance. What French fractures have you discerned from Washington that those who live there no longer perceive?

The fracture that strikes me most is no doubt the one that separates the European discourse on independence from its political, economic and cultural reality. From Washington, one observes a paradox: Europe, and France in particular, maintain with the United States a relationship made of admiration, criticism and imitation. The criticisms addressed to America are numerous and often legitimate, on inequalities, access to care, polarization. Yet the American models continue to exert a considerable influence on European choices, whether it is a matter of higher education, popular culture, the digital economy or modes of governance. The United States remains an implicit reference, sometimes even when it serves as a counterexample.

This fascination translates a deeper difficulty: that of Europe in fully asserting its own vision of the world. The continent nonetheless has considerable assets, its cultural diversity, its intellectual tradition, its social model, but it struggles to transform them into strategic power. The technological domain offers an eloquent illustration of this: while the great American digital platforms have acquired an unprecedented global influence, Europe has not managed to bring forth actors of the same scale. This dependence goes beyond the economy; it touches sovereignty, the circulation of information, the capacity to define one's own priorities. The observation holds for industry: for decades, strategic European companies have been weakened or absorbed in a globalization whose rules were often defined elsewhere. The takeover of the French Alstom by the American General Electric is the illustration of this.

What sometimes lacks in Europe is the confidence necessary to defend its interests with constancy. Too often, it oscillates between the will to assert its autonomy and the temptation to place itself under the protection of the United States. This ambiguity finds part of its origin in history: the memory of the Second World War lastingly shaped the transatlantic relationship, and the American intervention in the liberation of Western Europe remains deeply anchored in consciences. But a legitimate recognition should not turn into a lasting dependence.

What also challenges me is the gap between the principles Europe proclaims and certain of its practices. France likes to define itself as the homeland of human rights and of universalism. The ambition is noble, but it exposes the country to a particular requirement of coherence. In a world where information circulates instantaneously, the gaps between the displayed values and the concrete decisions are observed with growing attention, notably in the countries of the South. This is particularly true for historical memory. As a descendant of slaves, I remain conscious of the extraordinary fragility of this history: the transatlantic trade tore millions of men, women and children from their land, many perished during the crossing, others succumbed to the violences of the slave system, and those who survived transmitted, at the cost of immense suffering, the very possibility of our existence. We are the heirs of a chain of improbable survivals, and memory is not only a duty toward the past, it is a responsibility toward the future. Thus France's abstention on the United Nations resolution of 25 March 2026, qualifying the transatlantic trade and racialized slavery as among the gravest crimes against humanity, constitutes a great missed appointment with history, and will remain engraved as a betrayal of the very principles of liberty, equality and fraternity.

There is another French fracture: the gaze cast on its former African colonies. As much as France shows itself prudent toward the United States, Russia or China, so much does it keep, like many European nations, what some call the "colonial reflex." This reflex, a veritable law of the strongest, consists in denying the procession of massacres, pillages and humiliations of colonization, sometimes in glorifying it, and above all in setting itself up as moralizer and authority over Africa. African youth takes note of this and observes that the methods have hardly changed: pillaging of resources, establishment of military bases, pollution of soils with complete impunity, gorged elites and impoverished peoples, whose only way out becomes the perilous journey across the Mediterranean, toward a destiny marked by uncertainty, rejection and sometimes death. The world changes faster than France, and other actors have positioned themselves on the continent. The demonstrations of young Kenyans against the Africa Forward summit show that they reject a repackaging of the old neocolonialism that returns under other skies, anglophone this time, with promises of massive investments, but also foreign soldiers endowed with immunity. A great nation examines its conscience, listens and draws the lessons of the past. It does not reproduce the same patterns.

Martinique is a French department in a Caribbean made up mostly of independent nations. This status has a history, a logic, contradictions. Is it still tenable in the twenty-first century?

That depends on the way departmentalization is managed. Today, Martinique, Guadeloupe and the Overseas territories in general are societies that wear the clothes of departmentalization over colonial undergarments. Let me explain: after the abolition of slavery, the békés were compensated, while the true victims received nothing, not a cent. They had to build themselves shacks of sheet metal, survive as they could, obtain a plot of land, a goat or two, live off fishing. For decades, life in the West Indies remained below the poverty line. From the 1960s and 1970s, things began to evolve, but it took our deputies, in particular Aimé Césaire, insisting on a certain parity.

This parity is not quite achieved. One need only see with what condescension chlordecone was handled: American researchers who work today on the subject are horrified, convinced that if it had occurred in Paris, the State would have shown far more diligence. Imagine the same water shortages in Paris, in Lyon or in Bordeaux. One sometimes has the impression that these are territories kept to maintain control, for France, without the overseas territories, would be only the 43rd maritime power in the world; thanks to them, it is the second. To be the second maritime power, to sit on the Security Council, to be present in the Americas and in institutions such as the Organization of American States, all of this contributes to its influence. Yet no citizen from the overseas territories has ever represented France in these bodies, and the West Indianization of the leadership remains flagrant by its absence, in the West Indies as in Guiana.

There subsists that old colonial reflex which consists in trusting the white person more, the béké more precisely. By working with all the components of the overseas societies, and in particular with the people of African descent, the economy of these territories could only grow. Yet there still exists a glass ceiling for the populations of African descent. In my childhood, this double standard was expressed in the speeches of visiting ministers, who spoke with paternalism of "the affection they bear for these populations": one was in the affective register for the people of African descent, in the economic register with the békés. Today, this discourse has been replaced by "the State does a great deal for the West Indians," a phrase one would never use for the Parisian or the Alsatian. The term is hurtful, for if one is part of a nation, one does not do "for them," one does "with them," at their side. France would have every interest in taking seriously the populations of color, in particular of African descent, and in endowing them with real means to advance the country's economy. The békés, who constitute a minority, have far more access to French power and to capital than the West Indian of color, and this since slavery: there is there a flagrant disparity, encouraged by the State.

Even access to property and the possibility of returning to the West Indies when one lives in mainland France have become great challenges for the West Indian of African descent, in competition with people from the mainland and Europeans whose financial means are often superior. The overseas territories become unaffordable for the overseas people themselves: one witnesses a gentrification, a form of genocide by substitution. I salute in passing the work of Senator Catherine Conconne, whose "Allé viré" program puts in place means to counteract this state of affairs.

As for the status, should it change? Yes and no. We are in the era of great ensembles: a Martinique alone, economically vulnerable, would perhaps not be a viable project. An ensemble of Martinique-Guadeloupe-Guiana, autonomous or independent, is an idea too rarely explored. The viability of a country depends on its adherence to a solid economic project, on its will to form a nation, on its capacity to understand the great geopolitical stakes and to find its role in them. Size is not always decisive: Singapore is smaller than Martinique, and yet figures among the best students in the world. Its first prime minister, Lee Kuan Yew, had inherited a country in ruins and made of it a jewel, thanks to the newly acquired discipline of the Singaporeans and their adherence to his economic program.

From Washington, you have observed the relations between Africa and France reconfigure themselves deeply. Does what you have seen resemble a historic turning point or one more crisis that we go through without drawing the consequences?

It is both an additional crisis and a historic turning point whose implications we still refuse to measure fully.

In the American universities, as in many research centers, France is increasingly analyzed through the prism of its colonial heritage. This perception results neither from a Russian propaganda campaign nor from a Chinese influence: it stems from a historical reading that is widely documented and taught. The question of the CFA franc is a striking illustration of this. Many wonder about the permanence, in the twenty-first century, of a monetary system whose origins go back to the colonial era, and struggle to understand why such an anachronistic architecture survived so long without being deeply called into question. More broadly, many researchers consider that mechanisms of economic and political dependence subsist.

For a long time, the French imprint in West Africa was particularly visible. In countries like Senegal or Côte d'Ivoire, the French military presence was often one of the first symbols perceived by foreign visitors, maintaining the image of a persistent and shocking influence of the former colonial power. When one examines the economic stakes, the control of strategic infrastructures, of ports, of natural resources, the questions become even more numerous.

To this is added the weight of memory. The assassinations of major figures of the continent continue to mark African consciences. I think of Thomas Sankara, whom I had the honor of meeting in 1982 when I was working at the United Nations, but also of Patrice Lumumba or of Colonel Muammar Gaddafi. These figures remain strong symbols in the African and Afro-descendant political imagination. These events are neither forgotten nor concealed: they are studied in universities the world over. What is today called into question is a conception of international relations founded on the political, economic or strategic control of weaker states. Yet it should be possible to develop partnerships profitable to all (win-win) without depriving populations of their sovereignty. One often speaks of French interests, which is legitimate, but there also exist Burkinabe, Nigerien, Malian or Senegalese interests, which deserve the same respect. Any balanced relationship supposes the mutual recognition of the interests of each.

That is why the idea that the relations between Africa and France "are reconfiguring themselves deeply" remains below the reality: we are witnessing a veritable change of era. A turning point began under the presidency of Nicolas Sarkozy, certain of whose orientations aroused strong tensions with several African leaders. But the most striking moment of rupture remains no doubt the fall and death of Muammar Gaddafi in 2011. Beyond his person and the controversies that surrounded his regime, Gaddafi embodied for many the idea of an Africa capable of asserting itself as an autonomous power on the international scene. Whether or not one shares this vision, he represented a continental ambition.

Has France truly drawn the lessons of the evolution of its relations with Africa?

I do not think so, and on this point I am rather categorical. France, like a part of Europe, often keeps the conviction of being right and still struggles to listen to what African youth tells it. Yet this youth expresses very clear expectations. During my travels on the continent, I encourage it above all to believe in its own future and to build opportunities on the spot, but the economic conditions still have to allow it.

One of the major stakes lies in the local transformation of natural resources. The extractive industries should not limit themselves to exporting raw materials: they should create value on the spot, develop sectors and generate skilled jobs. Without this step, the African economies remain prisoners of a model in which wealth is produced locally but captured elsewhere. I often pose a simple question to young Africans: would you rather risk your life crossing the Mediterranean on a makeshift boat, or build an activity in your country and travel afterward to Europe as an entrepreneur, partner or tourist? The answer is generally obvious.

There is a missing piece in this equation: the behavior of certain multinationals of the extractive sector, perceived as excessively predatory, anxious to maximize short-term profits without seeking a lasting balance with the countries that host them. From then on, one should not be surprised that certain African states turn toward other partners when these companies, or the countries that support them, are judged too demanding. Another path was possible: a more balanced sharing of the benefits, more margin for the host states, partnerships founded on reciprocity. The problem is also one of transparency. Western public opinion knows the amounts of the aid granted to Africa, but much less the value of the resources extracted from the continent and the distribution of these riches. This asymmetry feeds mistrust.

I remember a meeting where a great oil company presented its activities in an African country. Questioned about their social contribution, its representatives first answered that it fell to the State, and not to the company, to ensure the social missions. The argument is understandable on the level of principles. But what struck one was the contrast between the scale of the profits and the modesty of the projects put forward under the heading of social responsibility: the construction of a small school with very limited means, valued in the communication as a humanitarian action of scope. It is precisely this gap between the discourses and the realities of the field that today feeds a deep questioning. More than a passing crisis, it is a fundamental debate on economic justice, sovereignty and the very nature of international partnerships in the twenty-first century.

In The Origin of Others, Toni Morrison wrote that race is not a natural reality but an instrument of power. Does this idea seem to you still to illuminate the America of today?

Absolutely. Toni Morrison had perfectly grasped one of the fundamental mechanisms of modern history. As a side note, I occupy today one of the offices she used at Howard. It is for me a great pride, all the more so as this same office had also been occupied by the Guianese poet and writer Léon-Gontran Damas, one of the major figures of Negritude.

When Toni Morrison asserts that race is an instrument of power, she underscores an essential historical reality: as soon as a human group can be identified visually, catalogued and reduced to a physical characteristic, it becomes easier to dominate it, to exploit it, to keep it in a subordinate position. Race is therefore not a relevant biological datum, it is a social and political construction in the service of relations of power.

The question is whether contemporary America has truly absorbed this lesson. I am not certain of it. A part of the country has understood it, but another continues to promote a profoundly selective national narrative. We are witnessing an attempt to rewrite American history in which slavery, segregation and structural discrimination occupy an increasingly marginal place: in certain states, the debates on school programs, libraries, museums and collective memory testify to this will to minimize certain swaths of the past. This narrative often presents the United States as the exclusive work of European pioneers come to develop an almost empty territory, ignoring the decisive contribution of the Indigenous peoples, of African Americans, of Latinos and of many other communities.

One of the consequences of this incomplete memory is the persistent underestimation of the African American contributions. For generations, Black inventors, scientists, entrepreneurs, artists and intellectuals played a determining role without receiving the recognition they deserved. It is a point I often raise with my students: a large part of the history of American innovation cannot be understood without them, and yet these trajectories remain largely absent from the popular narratives and sometimes even from the school programs.

This avoidance appears even in political debate. During the 2024 campaign, Nikki Haley aroused a lively controversy by answering a question on the causes of the Civil War without explicitly mentioning slavery, whereas the war is inseparable from it. This avoidance is not always the doing of people born in the United States: certain immigrant populations arrive with their own social hierarchies and may reproduce, consciously or not, systems of classification inherited from their country of origin.

At bottom, Toni Morrison's reflection retains all its currency because it obliges us to look beyond the racial categories themselves, to examine the mechanisms of power that they serve. The real question is not only to know how societies define human differences, but why they define them thus, and to whom this definition is profitable. As long as this interrogation remains at the heart of American political and cultural life, her work will continue to illuminate contemporary debates with a remarkable pertinence.

Aimé Césaire, Édouard Glissant, Maryse Condé, Patrick Chamoiseau: four major figures who deeply renewed French-language literature and contemporary thought. Yet a question remains: does West Indian literature occupy in France the place it deserves in the literary pantheon, or does it remain assigned to a peripheral position?

These names should constitute one of the uncontested pillars of contemporary French literature. They nonetheless still have the ambiguous status of authors who are major elsewhere, but partly peripheral in France. And this contradiction says much about the country's relationship to its own margins.

The observation is brutal: West Indian literature is often more studied, celebrated and institutionalized in the United States and in the Anglo-Saxon academic world than in France itself. In the French departments of thousands of American universities, these authors are not exotic annexes of the program, they are central axes of it. Césaire, Glissant, Condé or Chamoiseau are read there as thinkers of the contemporary world, not as overseas variants of the French canon. In France, the pattern remains largely unchanged: an implicit hierarchy continues to distinguish the center and the periphery, the "classics" and the "authors from...," as if literary thought were fully formulated only from Paris.

This imbalance is all the more striking in that it no longer stems from ignorance, but from a structural delay of recognition. The critical tools exist, the works are translated, studied, awarded prizes. Yet their integration into the national literary narrative remains partial, as if their universal scope still had to be validated by an external gaze. Frantz Fanon embodies this paradox all by himself: a major author of the twentieth century, read from Africa to the United States and from Latin America to the African American movements, he remains in France too often confined to a specialized academic reading, whereas elsewhere he is a political thinker of the first rank, mobilized right down to the contemporary debates on race and decolonization. Why does France seem to need its own authors to be consecrated elsewhere in order to recognize them fully?

The tribute paid to Maryse Condé at the Panthéon certainly marked a symbolic turning point. But it comes late, and it appears isolated in an institutional landscape where the representation of overseas literatures remains marginal. Beyond the symbols, the reality is harsher: in the great cultural institutions, in the school programs, in the places of intellectual legitimation, West Indian authors remain underrepresented. Not absent, but constantly relegated to secondary categories, as if their universality still had to be demonstrated.

This logic goes well beyond literature. It runs through the whole cultural and media field, where the overseas territories still appear too often under a double register, folklorized or associated with narratives of crisis. Between invisibilization and stigmatization, the space for complexity remains narrow. And yet, the talents exist, incontestable, in all domains, literature, research, medicine, engineering. Initiatives such as the ceremonies devoted to the "Overseas Talents" have tried to correct this invisibilization by bringing to light trajectories of excellence, but these efforts remain insufficiently relayed in the media and institutional space. The problem is therefore not creation, it is recognition, and this recognition remains profoundly unbalanced. As long as France continues to think its margins as cultural peripheries rather than as centers of intellectual production in their own right, it will miss an essential part of its own modernity.
