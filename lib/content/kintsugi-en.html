The preciousness of life is never revealed so clearly as in its fragility. It is at a loved one's bedside, in the silence of a hospital room, that one suddenly understands what it means to be present. It is in the brutal absence of someone we loved that we measure the immensity of what will not return. Death is not the opposite of life. It is its developer, the way shadow draws light.

The golden lines of kintsugi

The Japanese have this ancestral practice of kintsugi: repairing broken porcelain with gold, making fractures not a shame to conceal but a beauty to celebrate. There is in this philosophy a vertiginous wisdom about the preciousness of what has been damaged by time and use.

Our lives carry these same golden scars. Bereavements, failures, sorrows — everything that has broken us has also made us unique, irreplaceable. The preciousness of life does not reside in intact perfection. It resides in these fault lines that tell our story. A vase without cracks has not lived. A life without wounds has not loved.

To repair is to make an act of faith in duration. It is to say to the object, to the relationship, to oneself: you deserve that I stop, that I take care, that I devote a portion of my limited life to restoring your beauty.

But we have forgotten the art of repair. Our civilization has made disposability its silent religion. What is damaged gets replaced. What is cracked gets abandoned. We live in the empire of perpetual newness, where objects are born already condemned to obsolescence, where bonds unravel at the first snag, where people themselves become interchangeable, swiped away with a thumb, erased with a click.

This frenzy of replacement conceals a deep terror: that of permanence, commitment, long time. To repair is to accept that something is worth saving. Kintsugi is not merely an artisan technique. It is an ethic. It is the radical refusal of this culture of erasure. Where our era sees in the break an ending, the Japanese master sees a beginning. The gold that winds through the veins of the porcelain does not hide the wound; it transfigures it. It says: here, something happened. Here, life left its mark. And this mark is precious.

Presence as an act of resistance

In a world saturated with noise, offering someone your full attention has become a subversive act. We live scattered, dispersed among notifications and manufactured urgencies, present nowhere through being connected everywhere. Our conversations resemble stopovers; we pass through them, we do not dwell in them. Our eyes slide over faces like water over stone.

Yet there is no love without presence. No true love in the half-glance, in fragmented listening, in that way of answering someone while thinking of something else. To love is first of all to be there, entirely, radically, without escape. It is to make the other, for a moment, the absolute center of the world.

This quality of presence has become so rare that it borders on the sacred. When someone looks at us, when they listen without their mind wandering toward their own preoccupations, we feel it physically, like a sudden warmth, like a hand placed on a shoulder. We exist more fully under that gaze. We take on relief.

Perhaps this is the secret we have lost: the preciousness of life is measured by the quality of attention we bring to it. What we pass through distractedly fades immediately, like a dream at morning. Only what we have fully inhabited remains, those moments when we were present enough to feel time passing through our veins.

What will not return

There are childhood mornings one still remembers: the particular light of a Sunday, the smell of coffee in the kitchen, the voice of someone no longer there. These moments had nothing extraordinary about them at the time. They were simply life, ordinary and daily. It is their disappearance that revealed them for what they were: treasures buried beneath habit, diamonds we took for pebbles.

We never know, in the moment, what will become precious. The last conversation with a friend. The last summer of a vanished era. The last look exchanged before a parting. Preciousness always reveals itself afterward, in the lack it hollows, in the void it leaves that nothing will fill.

Perhaps this is our only possible wisdom: to live as though each instant already contained its own nostalgia, as though we were remembering the present from a future where we will miss it. With gratitude rather than anxiety. By welcoming rather than clinging. To hold each moment as one holds a bird, firmly enough that it does not fly away, gently enough not to smother it.

The urgency of gentleness

Faced with the brevity of everything, some choose frenzy, accumulating, conquering, devouring the world as if to ward off the void to come. Others choose cynicism: if nothing lasts, then nothing has value. Both responses miss something essential. They are flights, refusals to face this simple and terrible truth: we are mortal, and this is why we are alive.

The true answer to the preciousness of life is gentleness. Gentleness toward others, who share our mortal condition and who carry, like us, invisible wounds. Gentleness toward ourselves, who bear our burdens as best we can, with our wavering strength and intermittent courage. Gentleness toward the time that passes, which is not our enemy but our traveling companion.

Life is precious not despite its fragility, but because of it. An eternal flower would be just one more object, a dusty trinket on the shelf of the universe. It is because it fades that we lean down to look at it, that we breathe its fragrance, that we find it beautiful enough to bring tears. So it is with everything that lives, with everything that passes, with everything that will not return.

Fewer things, more moments. The kind one does not forget.
