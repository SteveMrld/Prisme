Cairo, June 4, 2009. Barack Obama, forty-fourth president of the United States, takes the podium at Al-Azhar University. He is forty-seven, eight months in power, and about to deliver the most ambitious speech of his presidency. In the room: rectors, religious dignitaries, students from across the Arab world. Outside, a billion people watching. The Arab world, Africa, Southeast Asia are waiting to understand whether this man is different from his predecessors. He begins: "I have come here to Cairo to seek a new beginning between the United States and Muslims around the world." The phrase sounded different from what chanceries were accustomed to hearing, less like a diplomatic overture than an admission.

Primary source Barack Obama — Cairo Speech, Al-Azhar University, June 4, 2009. White House / US National Archives.

This speech has often been reduced to a symbolic gesture, well-constructed rhetoric from talented speechwriters. That reading goes too fast. Obama explicitly acknowledged the US role in the 1953 Iranian coup, addressed colonization without evasion, quoted the Quran, named the Palestinian question with a directness unusually frank for a sitting American president. He spoke of Palestinian suffering in the same sentences as Israeli security, acknowledged that democracy could not be imposed from the outside, asked the Islamic world for an honest look at its own contradictions in exchange for an honest look from America at its own. In the streets of Cairo, Beirut, Dakar, Jakarta, people watching the broadcast began to cry, not from sentimentality but from a relief many could not have formulated a few weeks earlier: seeing the world's dominant power speak differently and suggest, for the first time in a long while, that it might be capable of acting differently.

The man before the president

This hope had a biography, and it had begun long before Cairo. Barack Hussein Obama was born in 1961 in Honolulu, to a Kenyan father and a white mother from Kansas. He grew up between Hawaii and Indonesia, in worlds that did not speak to each other and that he learned to inhabit simultaneously. This dislocated childhood he turned into a rare intellectual resource: the capacity to occupy several positions at once, to understand from the inside what it means to be at the margin of several identities without being fully accepted in any of them. He read with a voracity his close friends describe as almost physical. As a teenager, he discovered Toni Morrison and her novel Song of Solomon, and would later say he read it not just to learn to write, but to learn to be and to think. At Harvard, he became the first Black student to serve as president of the Harvard Law Review. In Chicago, he worked as a community organizer in the South Side before entering politics through the Illinois State Senate.

When he announced his presidential candidacy in February 2007, he chose to do so on the steps of the Old State Capitol in Springfield, Illinois, where Abraham Lincoln had prepared his own presidency a century and a half earlier. The parallel was not accidental. Obama had studied Lincoln with an almost obsessive attention, and his inaugural address of January 2009 would carry as its official theme the words of the Gettysburg Address: A New Birth of Freedom. He made the journey by train from Philadelphia to Washington, retracing the trip Lincoln had made before him. This conscious and deliberate staging told not only a personal ambition. It posed a question to America: was it capable, a century and a half after Lincoln, of finally honoring what it had promised? The answer of November 4, 2008, was overwhelming. Elderly Black men wept in Mississippi polling stations. Crowds gathered spontaneously in cities around the world, uninvited.

What he accomplished

The financial crisis greeted him barely elected. Markets were collapsing, Lehman Brothers had vanished, the global economy was reeling. Obama inherited a disaster he had not caused and managed it with cold rigor: the bank bailouts stabilized the system, and the economic recovery that followed was one of the longest in American history. Unemployment, which had reached 10% in October 2009, fell to 4.7% by the end of his term. Fifteen million eight hundred thousand jobs were created between 2010 and 2017. Real GDP grew 16.3% over the entire period. This is real, measurable, and it would be dishonest not to say so. But here is what is less readily acknowledged: not a single Wall Street executive was criminally prosecuted for the crisis he had helped cause. The banks were rescued with public money, their executives departed with their bonuses, and the Obama administration deliberately chose not to open the files. Growth had occurred. It had not trickled down equally for everyone; the Black American unemployment rate remained double that of whites at the end of the term.

The Affordable Care Act nonetheless remains one of the most significant social advances any American president has managed to enact since Medicare and Medicaid under Johnson in the 1960s. What Obama originally wanted was a public option, a universal safety net financed by the state. What he obtained, after months of exhausting negotiations with a hostile Congress and an insurance industry whose influence on American political life is difficult to overestimate, was a hybrid reform. The number of uninsured Americans fell from 43.8 million in 2008 to 28.6 million in 2016. In the world's wealthiest country, which had refused for decades any system of universal protection, fifteen million additional people gaining access to healthcare is a transformation that many European countries took decades to accomplish.

On October 9, 2009, barely nine months into office, the Nobel Committee awarded him the Peace Prize. Obama himself seemed surprised. His acceptance speech in Oslo was one of his most honest: he acknowledged having done nothing to merit this recognition, recalled that the United States was at war in two countries and that he himself was ordering military operations. There was in this exercise a rare honesty, and perhaps a certain naivety about what the years ahead would demand of him.

On May 2, 2011, in the Situation Room of the White House, Obama watched in real time the Navy SEALs operation that ended in Osama bin Laden's death at his hideout in Abbottabad, Pakistan. The photograph that circulated worldwide shows him seated in a corner of the room, in a casual jacket, concentrated and grave, surrounded by his advisers including Hillary Clinton, hand over her mouth. The photograph would circulate for a long time, less as a celebration than as a document about what American presidencies always end up becoming, whatever the promise at the outset.

What he did not keep

Guantanamo did not close. Obama had signed the closing decree on his first day in the Oval Office. Congress systematically blocked detainee transfers, negotiations stalled, the prison remained open for eight years. A man whose career was built on constitutional law allowed a structure that openly violated it to operate, year after year.

The drones. This is where the gap between the Cairo speech and the acts is most brutal. Under Obama, the drone strike program in Pakistan, Yemen, and Somalia exceeded in volume and intensity everything his predecessor had done. Thousands of people were killed in countries with which the United States was not officially at war. The Bureau of Investigative Journalism documented between 384 and 807 civilian casualties in Pakistan alone, a figure probably underestimated because the American definition of combatant included any man of fighting age present in the strike area. Each week, in what his aides called among themselves "Terror Tuesday," Obama personally reviewed the list of targets to be eliminated. The same man who had delivered the Cairo speech signed extrajudicial execution orders every week at the other end of the world.

Libya 2011: the intervention to protect Benghazi from a massacre was defensible. The complete absence of a post-intervention plan was not. Libya became a failed state, a hub for arms trafficking toward the Sahel and human trafficking toward Europe. Obama acknowledged this himself in 2016 in an interview with The Atlantic, calling this absence of post-intervention strategy his worst foreign policy failure. On Syria, the red line publicly drawn in 2012 on the use of chemical weapons, crossed by Assad in 2013 with over a thousand deaths, then abandoned without military response. The message was received as it was meant to be received by those who wanted to receive it that way.

What will endure

In December 2012, Obama presented the Presidential Medal of Freedom to Toni Morrison, the novelist who had shaped part of his adolescent thinking. He quoted Song of Solomon from the White House podium. Morrison, who had supported him since 2008 for his "creative imagination" and had said she felt American for the first time when he was elected, was in the room. It was the image of an America that seemed finally capable of reconciling itself with its own ghosts.

Four years later, Donald Trump was elected President of the United States. The causal link is not simple but it would be dishonest to ignore it. Eight years of a Black, educated, multilingual, Nobel laureate president who quoted Lincoln and Morrison from the White House produced, in a large part of the white American electorate, a reaction of which Trump was the most brutal expression. Obama did not create Trump. But his presidency revealed fractures no one had wanted to face, fears and resentments that American politics had learned to manage through silence and that his very existence brought to light.

One must perhaps ask, in the end, whether the Obama disappointment says more about the United States than about Obama himself. A country whose military budget exceeds that of the next ten combined, whose economy has been structured since 1945 around the projection of power, whose institutions are designed to resist change more than to enable it. Within that framework, the Cairo speech was perhaps not a promise Obama was going to keep or betray. It was a window briefly opened onto what America could be if it were not first what it is.

Those who wept in the streets of Dakar in November 2008 were not naive. They had heard something real in that man, something that was genuinely there. What they had perhaps not measured, and what Obama himself had perhaps underestimated, is that even men who understand the world with that precision do not necessarily change it. Sometimes they render its mechanism visible with remarkable clarity, without being able to extricate themselves from it.
