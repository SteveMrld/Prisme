Iraq, or when skepticism was right

One need only return to the period preceding the invasion of Iraq in 2003. Some journalists had dared to question the claims of the American and British governments about the existence of weapons of mass destruction. They were labeled conspiracy theorists, naifs, anti-Americans. Time proved them entirely right. The weapons did not exist. The evidence dossiers had been falsified or exaggerated. And the mainstream press, in its vast majority, had relayed the official narrative without exercising the elementary skepticism that its mission demanded.2

This episode deserves to be recalled every time one is tempted to too quickly equate a dissenting opinion with disinformation. Deliberate disinformation, which manipulates facts to serve interests, must be combated with rigor. But difference of opinion, the questioning of dominant narratives, skepticism toward official sources: these postures are the very heart of critical thinking. Reducing them to suspicion would impoverish collective intelligence and truncate reality of its complexity.

The attention economy as an engine of distortion

The journalism crisis is not only ethical. It is economic. Traditional media have lost the bulk of their advertising revenues to digital platforms, without finding a solid alternative model. In this context of permanent financial pressure, the temptation is strong to favor what generates audience, sensationalism, controversy, strong emotion, at the expense of what takes time and costs money: investigation, verification, contextualization.

Social media algorithms have aggravated this dynamic by rewarding content that elicits strong reactions. Information that provokes outrage gets shared more than nuanced analysis. A sensational revelation attracts more clicks than a careful correction. This mechanism creates structural pressure on newsrooms to produce content that performs in the algorithmic sense, often at the expense of its actual informational value.3

The figures of a structural detachment

The Reuters Institute Digital News Report 2025 data, 48 countries and nearly 100,000 respondents, gives a precise measure of the scale of the phenomenon. Trust in news stagnates at 40% globally, four points below the peak reached during the pandemic. The share of the population reporting actively avoiding news has reached 40%, up from 29% in 2017: an absolute record. In the United Kingdom, interest in news fell from 70% in 2015 to 38% in 2024, a drop of 32 points in less than a decade. In Argentina, from 77% to 45% in seven years.4

These figures do not describe a disinterest in reality. They describe a divorce between the public and the institutional forms of information. The distinction is crucial.

The newsfluencers, symptom of a rupture

In the void left by distrust, two types of actors have emerged. On one side, individuals whose name has become a media brand: Joe Rogan reaches 22% of Americans every week, Tucker Carlson 14%, HugoDécrypte in France has 2.8 million YouTube subscribers. These "newsfluencers" — the term is the Reuters Institute's — are not defined by an editorial line or a professional code, but by a direct and personal relationship with their audience. They offer what institutional media no longer provides: a sense of unfiltered access.

On the other side, new structured independent media have built alternative models: Mediapart on subscription and investigative journalism, Blast on crowdfunding, Tortoise Media in the UK on slow journalism. These actors are media in the proper sense, with newsrooms, editorial lines, and ethical charters. They are not interchangeable with the former.

The public is not fleeing depth. It is fleeing superficiality dressed up as seriousness.

Concentration, the other side of the problem

Understanding the disaffection without looking at who owns the media would be incomplete. In France, nine billionaires now control more than 80% of national titles. Acquisitions have accelerated: Vincent Bolloré absorbed the Lagardère group at the end of 2023, Rodolphe Saadé acquired BFMTV and RMC in summer 2024 then Brut in September 2025, Bernard Arnault took Paris Match in October 2024. In the United States, the Ellison family acquired CBS and Paramount in August 2025 and is now targeting Warner Bros. and CNN. Nine of the ten wealthiest Americans own or run media companies.5

This concentration does not explain everything. But it creates a structural condition in which editorial independence becomes a remarkable exception rather than a norm.

Toward an ethical awakening

Distrust of traditional media has opened considerable space for alternatives. Some produce serious and rigorous journalism. Others contribute precisely to the disinformation they claim to combat. Media disintermediation is not in itself a solution — it is a new terrain on which the same questions arise with redoubled urgency.

What is lacking is not so much an additional regulator as an internal demand. A broadcast regulator can sanction the most serious breaches. It cannot compel a newsroom to cross-check its sources, give a platform to counterarguments, or separate analysis from commentary. These choices belong to journalists themselves, and to their editors. If established media do not undertake this ethical renewal of their own accord, this shift will accelerate. And with it, the fragmentation of a shared public sphere which is, despite everything, a condition of democracy.

Truth has never been an immediate given. It is the result of a process — verification, contradiction, methodical doubt. It is precisely this work that journalism, in its best version, has always had as its mission to perform. That mission has not changed. The context in which it must be exercised has been radically transformed.
