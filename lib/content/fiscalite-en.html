There exists in France a well-established tradition: promising not to raise taxes while simultaneously raising levies. This is not necessarily dishonest. It is often worse: it is precision.

Because the word "tax" (impôt) does not designate, in French fiscal law, the totality of what the state collects from households and businesses. It designates only a portion of it. And this distinction, technical in appearance, lies at the heart of all the misunderstandings that structure public debate on taxation.

One word, multiple realities

The mandatory levy (prélèvement obligatoire), a broader concept than the tax (impôt), refers to any constrained payment made to a public administration without individualized direct counterpart. Under this definition fall taxes in the strict sense, but also levies, social contributions, special charges, fees, and a constellation of mandatory payments whose names change with the times and governments.

The distinction is not merely formal. It produces real political effects.

A tax (impôt) is voted by Parliament, inscribed in the finance law, debated publicly. A social contribution (cotisation sociale) follows a different logic: it finances a right in return, retirement, healthcare, unemployment. A levy (taxe) in theory remunerates a service or compensates an externality. A special charge (contribution) finances a specific objective of general interest.

These distinctions are legitimate legally. They become problematic when they serve to discreetly shift the fiscal burden from one category to another, without democratic debate being able to take the full measure of it.

The semantic war

A political official can state with full technical rigor that they have not raised income tax while simultaneously having increased the actual tax burden on households through other means. A new charge, an expanded copayment, a reformed contribution, a levy created under another name.

The word "contribution" sounds less constraining than "tax." "Fee" evokes a service rendered. "Social contribution" suggests an acquired right. These connotations are not innocent.

They modulate the perception of the levy without changing its economic nature: in every case, money leaves the taxpayer's assets to join a public fund.

This blurring is not always intentional. It is also the product of historical stratification: each tax reform adds a layer without removing the previous one; each new levy receives a name that distinguishes it from those already in existence. The result is a system where even specialists struggle to draw up an exhaustive inventory.

The mandatory levy rate:
necessary, insufficient

Faced with this complexity, economists have constructed a synthetic indicator: the mandatory levy rate, expressed as a percentage of gross domestic product. This indicator has the advantage of exhaustiveness: it aggregates all mandatory levies, regardless of their designation.

But it has its own limitations.

It says how much is collected. It does not say what is done with it. Two countries can show the same mandatory levy rate with radically different redistribution systems, public services of very unequal effectiveness, social transfers whose nature and beneficiaries vary considerably.

It also does not distinguish levies by their actual incidence. A tax formally borne by a company may be passed on to the final consumer. A nominally employee-borne contribution may economically be carried by the employer. The legal incidence and the economic incidence of a levy are two different things, and it is the economic incidence that matters for understanding who pays.

The real question

The debate about the level of levies is legitimate and necessary. But it is secondary to a question that France rarely poses with the rigor it deserves: what are these levies for?

No rational citizen objects to paying taxes in exchange for public services that are worth their cost. The question is not "how much?" but "for what?" and "with what effectiveness?"

This is the conversation that semantic blurring makes difficult. As long as the fiscal debate remains captive to the war of words, it cannot become what it should be: a debate about collective choices, public priorities, and the quality of the social contract that levies are meant to finance.

Untangling the vocabulary is not a technical exercise reserved for specialists. It is the condition for an honest democratic debate about what we collectively choose to finance together, and what we refuse to finance alone.

Key takeaways
01

The word "tax" covers only part of what the state collects. Social contributions, levies, special charges: dozens of mandatory payments fly beneath the radar of democratic debate.

02

This complexity allows the actual tax burden to be increased without ever "raising taxes": a permanent political sleight of hand.

03

French taxpayers pay among the highest rates in any democracy, without having a clear picture of it. Much collected, little acknowledged.
