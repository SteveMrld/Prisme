I followed the results of the latest municipal elections with a great deal of attention. And anxiety as well.

Like many, I observe the rise of extremes. And like many, I could be tempted to see this as fatality. A drift toward more anger, more mistrust, more fractures. But I refuse this resignation. I refuse to abandon the democratic battlefield to those who prosper on fear and retreat.

So I turned toward what I know best, and in which I believe: the world of work.

An error of diagnosis

We continue to believe that democracy plays out primarily at the ballot box, in parties, in institutions. Of course, all of this matters. But democracy is made well before the vote. It is built, or undone, in the ordinary experiences of daily life. In the way we live with others. In the way we feel recognized, useful, and heard.

And, for millions of people, this plays out first in their work environment.

A study by HEC Paris conducted by Yann Algan, Antonin Bergeaud, and Camille Frouard among 3,909 private sector employees has recently put words to this reality.

Professional isolation precedes democratic withdrawal. Isolation feeds mistrust. And mistrust feeds retreat.

But let us be clear-eyed: causality is circular. Does one vote for the extreme because one is isolated? Or does one become isolated because one is already distrustful? The answer is uncomfortable: probably both.

A loop forms: isolation, mistrust, retreat. What begins in a team often ends in the voting booth, or in abstention.

Anomie has settled into our organizations

None of this is new. More than a century ago, Émile Durkheim was already describing anomie, that form of disintegration of the social bond that leaves individuals without bearings, without belonging, without a common compass.

Work, whether we like it or not, remains one of the great places where these bearings are built. It confers a status, a role, a usefulness, an inscription in a collective. When it no longer fulfills this function, or fulfills it poorly, it is not only the organization that becomes fragile. It is society itself.

And today, this anomie has settled into our organizations. In teams under constant tension. In working rhythms that no longer leave time to build bonds. In environments where technology, and artificial intelligence in particular, however practical they may be, allow everything to be done alone: working alone, ordering alone, learning alone, sometimes even suffering alone.

We have streamlined processes and gained in efficiency, but we have impoverished relationships and forgotten the human beings.

The company is a political institution

We must have the courage to say it: the company is a political institution. Political in the sense of the city, not the partisan sense.

This does not mean it can or should bear everything alone. It is neither the school, nor the family, nor the state. Democratic fractures do not arise solely at work, and they will not be resolved there alone. But to refuse this role on the pretext that it is not exclusive would be a mistake.

For after the family, the company is one of the last places where different individuals still meet, cooperate, confront one another, and sometimes recognize themselves in a collective. In other words: it is where one learns to trust and to make society.

You do not only manage organizations. You produce human experiences.

Every employee who eats lunch alone. Every ignored idea. Every silence in a meeting. These are not details. This is political material.

An organization where individuals no longer speak to one another, no longer trust one another, no longer feel recognized does not only produce disengagement. It produces mistrust. And that mistrust does not stop at the company door. It overflows. It expresses itself. It votes, or it no longer votes.

Conversely, a company that creates bonds, recognition, and cooperation does not only practice good management. It strengthens the collective. It stabilizes society.

The bond is not an optional extra

We have made a major error. We have treated the bond as an optional extra when it is infrastructure. Invisible. Fragile. But decisive.

Added to this fragmentation of the bond is another poison: the normalization of an increasingly uninhibited racism and antisemitism. They thrive in a public climate saturated with one-upmanship, short-sighted political calculations, and media staging that too often transforms fear into spectacle. Through constant instrumentalization, identity fractures end up lodging themselves in minds, in conversations, in ordinary behaviors. And when hatred becomes utterable again, it is our very capacity to live together that is damaged.

Because at bottom, democracy never disappears all at once. It erodes. In silences. In solitudes. In those moments when no one feels concerned anymore.

Democracy is not an acquired right. It is a bond. And a bond, by definition, can break. But it can also be repaired.

On condition that we want it to be. On condition that we refuse the normalization of hatred. On condition that we rebuild, concretely, the foundations of trust.

Nothing is inevitable. Retreat is not a destiny. Mistrust is not a fatality. We can still choose to make society. And this choice is decided. It is built, every day, in our institutions, in our discourse, and in our organizations.

Democracy needs rules. But it holds, above all, through our capacity not to give up on one another.
