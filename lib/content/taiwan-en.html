Taiwan, strategic maritime highway

The coveted jewel of the electronics industry, pivot of the Asia-Pacific. The small island that makes great powers tremble.

SM
Steve Moradel
Geopolitics & Strategy

If nothing at the outset predestined this small semi-presidential representative democracy to become one of Asia's greatest economic powers, Taiwan's vitality, technological prowess, and constant innovation across numerous fields have allowed it to become the pivot of the Asia-Pacific in strategic terms. Its technological genius and geostrategic position confer upon this small island of 23 million people, situated to the southeast of mainland China, a unique global advantage, and fuel the ambitions of both Beijing and Washington. The recent visit of US House Speaker Nancy Pelosi, the highest-ranking sitting American official to visit since Newt Gingrich in 1997, only further degraded already at-bottom Sino-American relations. For in Beijing's eyes, Taiwan is a separatist province belonging to the People's Republic of China, and any challenge to the "one China" principle is perceived as an attack on its sovereignty and territorial integrity.

The Taiwan Strait, a vital maritime artery

In January 2010, the Obama administration's resumption of arms sales to Taiwan totaling $6.4 billion and the reinforcement of NATO naval presence in Asia signaled a shift in American strategy in the region.

On September 15, 2021, Australia, the United States, and the United Kingdom announced the establishment of a new trilateral military alliance, known as AUKUS, aimed at countering Chinese expansionism in the Indo-Pacific. For America, the aim is to maintain regional military superiority and restrain Chinese hegemony contrary to its interests in the region. The island of Taiwan, and particularly its strait, are at the heart of the American strategic pivot in the western Pacific.

The Taiwan Strait is not just any strait: it is a genuine vital maritime artery for international trade. This highly strategic strait forms part of the South China Sea and connects it to the East China Sea. It constitutes a mandatory passage for the principal commercial maritime flows linking Europe, the Middle East, and the major Chinese, Korean, and Japanese ports.

Disrupted by the pandemic, destabilized by the Russo-Ukrainian war and China's recent military exercises around Taiwan, global maritime freight faces one of the greatest transportation crises in history. The global economy has never been more dependent on the sea and its straits. While most maritime oil traffic passes through the Strait of Hormuz, Malacca, Bab-el-Mandeb, Suez, and the Bosphorus, in the Taiwan Strait, 48% of the 5,400 container ships operating worldwide transited in 2022 according to Bloomberg. The outbreak of a war in the region would have catastrophic consequences for the global economy.

The chip war has already begun

The semiconductor industry has not escaped the rising Sino-American rivalry. Numerous Chinese companies, DJI, Huawei, Hikvision, SMIC, have been placed on the Entity List, preventing any American company from collaborating with them. By sanctioning Huawei and, more broadly, China with respect to access to American technologies, particularly next-generation semiconductors, Washington is sketching the contours of a new geo-techno-political doctrine. For it is the semiconductor industry that today crystallizes Sino-American tensions.

China, the United States, and Taiwan, respectively the world's largest consumer, largest producer, and unchallenged leader in semiconductors below 7 nanometers, play a central role in this chip geopolitics. This market could alone be worth more than $1 trillion by 2030. The Covid-19 pandemic has significantly fragmented supply chains and highlighted their strategic importance.

100% of the world's most advanced semiconductor manufacturing capacity (below 10 nanometers) is currently located in Taiwan (92%) and South Korea (8%).

TSMC, the crown jewel of the Taiwanese economy and Asia's largest market capitalization at $440 billion, dominates the field of miniaturization with 92% of global production of semiconductors below 7 nm. TSMC also announces the manufacture of semiconductors below 2 nm by 2025. At the center of all ambitions, TSMC is today the world's most strategically important company. More than ever, Taiwan has become the central stake around which the new Sino-American cold war is being shaped.

In recent years, global giants have launched a genuine race toward miniaturization. The microprocessors that counted 4,000 transistors a few decades ago now count, at equal surface area, nearly 80 million. China, aware of its lag, has massively invested through the National Integrated Circuit Industry Fund, nicknamed the Big Fund, to target technological self-sufficiency.

In response, the US Senate passed the $280 billion CHIPS and Science Act. Approximately $52 billion will be dedicated to building semiconductor manufacturing facilities on American soil. Europe, far behind, nevertheless possesses two gems: ARM in the UK and above all ASML in the Netherlands, the world leader in photolithography machines. Brussels's plan for €42 billion in public investment by 2030 points in the right direction, but the lag behind China and the United States remains considerable.

A revenge on History

The normalization of relations between the United States and China, sought by Richard Nixon and Mao Zedong, now seems distant. While no one can predict whether a war will break out between the dominant power and the ascending power seeking to supplant it, the consequences of a military conflict would undoubtedly be catastrophic for both sides and for the rest of the world. The Thucydides Trap teaches us one essential thing: in our historic-tragic past, the rapid emergence of any new power has resulted, in 11 out of 15 cases since 1500, in war.

On July 1, 2021, the Chinese Communist Party celebrated its 100th anniversary. On this occasion, Xi Jinping, from Tiananmen Square, committed to reunifying Taiwan with China. He often recalls that China has a long memory, frequently referencing the two Opium Wars waged by Western powers in the nineteenth century, experienced as a national humiliation.

Xi Jinping's new nationalism feeds on a strong sense of revenge toward Westerners, guilty of having once invaded Chinese shores before imposing unequal treaties on the Middle Kingdom. Because China wishes to take its revenge on History, Xi Jinping has made the reunification of Taiwan an objective that cannot, as he has declared, be deferred from generation to generation.

If Taiwan were invaded and TSMC stopped, the global economy would lose access to advanced chips. There is no short-term Plan B.
