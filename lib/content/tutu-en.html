There is one image of Desmond Tutu that the West has retained: the archbishop in his purple robe, arms raised, weeping with joy the day Nelson Mandela was elected President of South Africa in April 1994. That image is true. It says something essential about this man. But it does not say everything. It does not say that a few months later, Tutu would publicly challenge his friend Mandela over his ministers' salaries. It does not say that he would criticize the ANC for twenty years with the same intransigence he had directed at the apartheid regime. It does not say that in 2013, at eighty-two years old, he would announce that he would never again vote for the party that had liberated his country.

Desmond Tutu was not a saint. He was something rarer and more useful: a man who applied his principles without looking to see which way the wind was blowing.

Klerksdorp, 1931

Desmond Mpilo Tutu was born on October 7, 1931, in Klerksdorp, in the Transvaal. His father was a schoolteacher, his mother a domestic worker and cook at a school for the blind. He wanted to become a doctor, but the studies were too expensive for his family. He became a teacher, like his father. In 1957, he resigned to protest the poor quality of education imposed on Black South Africans by the apartheid regime. This quiet gesture was already a signature: he did not accept injustice by merely enduring it.

He turned to theology. Ordained as an Anglican priest in 1961, he earned a master's degree at King's College London in 1966. He returned to South Africa, taught, and began to understand that the Church could be a space of resistance that the regime could not easily neutralize. In 1975, he was appointed Dean of St. Mary's Cathedral in Johannesburg, the first Black person to hold this position. His pulpit became a platform.

The voice of the voiceless

During the 1970s, most ANC leaders were either imprisoned, like Mandela on Robben Island, or in exile. Tutu filled the void. His ecclesiastical position afforded him relative inviolability (arresting an Anglican archbishop was diplomatically costly) and he used it. He denounced the pass laws from his pulpit, organized marches, preached at Steve Biko's funeral in 1977 before thousands of people under the eyes of the regime's police.

In 1978, he became the first Black Secretary General of the South African Council of Churches. From this position, he led a methodical civil resistance, called for international economic boycotts, addressed the entire world. The regime harassed him, confiscating his passport multiple times. He continued.

In 1984, the Nobel Committee awarded him the Peace Prize. In his Oslo address, he did not speak about himself. He spoke of mothers selling potatoes at train stations to feed their children. Of fathers separated from their families eleven months out of twelve in mining hostels. Of 3.5 million people forcibly displaced to the homelands. He said: this prize is for them.

Ubuntu: a weapon Africa gave to the world

Tutu's most enduring intellectual contribution is not his activism. It is his political and theological formulation of ubuntu, that Bantu philosophical concept that says, in its simplest version: I am because we are.

In contrast to the Cartesian "I think, therefore I am," ubuntu posits interdependence as the primary condition of humanity. A person exists fully only through their relationships with others. What diminishes another diminishes me. What elevates another elevates me. Community is not a framework within which the individual evolves; it is the condition of their very existence.

Tutu took this principle and made it the backbone of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission, which he chaired from 1995 to 1998. The central idea was radical: instead of retributive justice, punishing the guilty, South Africa chose restorative justice. Perpetrators testified publicly about their crimes. Victims testified to their suffering. Amnesty was granted in exchange for complete truth. Reconciliation does not erase crimes; it names them, exposes them to the light, and attempts to rebuild a common community on this shared truth.

Tutu replied to those who found this too lenient: "Saying that these people were monsters would relieve us of any responsibility. Monsters have no moral responsibility. But these men were human. And that is precisely why they can change." This is theology. It is also politics of rare sophistication.

The no to his own allies

Apartheid fell. Mandela was elected. Tutu wept with joy. And then he resumed his habitual work: saying what he thought, whatever the consequences.

He challenged Mandela over ministers' salaries, which he judged indecent in a country still poor. He confronted the ANC over arms trading, which brought the new government considerable revenues but stained its moral record. He denounced South Africa's silence in the face of Robert Mugabe's regime in neighboring Zimbabwe, a silence he judged shameful for a country that had benefited from international solidarity during its own dark years.

Under Jacob Zuma, he sharpened his tone further. He denounced endemic corruption, calling the president's conduct incompatible with the values of the historic ANC. In 2013, he crossed the threshold: he publicly announced that he would no longer vote for the ANC. For a man of his generation and history, this was an act of considerable gravity. It was not an ideological break. It was a moral break.

Tutu himself had disagreed with Mandela over the Truth and Reconciliation Commission. Mandela wanted to "turn the page." Tutu replied that you cannot turn a page without having read it. That sentence says everything.

The battles nobody expected

In the final decades of his life, Tutu took positions that his ecclesiastical milieu did not readily forgive. He publicly defended the rights of homosexual people, comparing homophobia to the racism of apartheid. He supported the right to die with dignity. He took a persistent position in favor of Palestinians, which earned him accusations of antisemitism that he refuted by distinguishing his criticism of Israeli state policy from any anti-Jewish sentiment.

These positions regularly put him at odds with natural allies. He did not care. He had long since learned that moral consistency costs more than political consistency.

December 26, 2021

Desmond Tutu died on December 26, 2021, in Cape Town, at the age of ninety. A national week of mourning was declared in South Africa. Mandela had died eight years earlier. With Tutu, the last great figure of the generation that had wrested freedom from the apartheid regime disappeared.

He leaves behind more than a political legacy. It is a method. Start from moral demand. Never apply it selectively according to the identity of the one being judged. Accept being alone when necessary. And never confuse loyalty to a cause with loyalty to those who carry it.

Ubuntu says: I am because we are. Tutu lived this sentence to the end, including when it commanded him to say no.
